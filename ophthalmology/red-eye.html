Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Always document visual acuity (each eye) in acute red eye, and screen for pain, photophobia, reduced vision, unilateral marked redness, and trauma/foreign body/chemical injury.
  • If any red-flag feature is present → same‑day specialist assessment (ophthalmology/ED route depends on local model and time of day).
  • Contact lens wear + painful red eye ± photophobia/reduced vision → treat as possible microbial keratitis (sight‑threatening) → same‑day emergency ophthalmology.

1) NICE CKS-derived “red flag” approach (as summarised in QI literature)

  • A UK primary care QI paper describing NICE CKS Red eye recommends documenting (including negatives): pain, photophobia, visual acuity, and laterality; and same‑day ophthalmology referral if red flags are present.
  • The paper lists five core red flag features (consistent with many UK teaching resources): moderate/severe pain, photophobia, reduced visual acuity, foreign body/penetrating injury, and marked redness.

2) College of Optometrists: urgency of referrals (red eye conditions that need urgent/emergency)

  • The College of Optometrists urgency table explicitly flags serious red-eye diagnoses for urgent pathways (e.g. infective keratitis, scleritis, iritis/uveitis, and severe traumatic red eye including chemical burns and penetrating injuries).
  • This is useful as a safety net when primary care cannot confidently distinguish benign conjunctivitis from sight‑threatening causes.

3) Right Decisions (example Scottish emergency eye guidance): pattern → action

  • NHS Highland Right Decisions “Red eye” emergency guideline uses symptom clusters to separate: foreign body/corneal abrasion/arc eye (often dischargeable with topical antibiotic + analgesia) vs more concerning patterns (e.g. pain + photophobia + decreased vision → corneal epithelial disturbance and/or other serious causes).
  • Local processes differ, but the shared principle is: pain/photophobia/reduced vision in a red eye warrants urgent specialist review unless a clearly benign diagnosis is made and symptoms are mild.
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • Confirm the D&G same‑day route for red-eye red flags: ED vs eye clinic vs optometry-led urgent service (and out-of-hours process).
  • Confirm whether there is a preferred local pathway for contact lens–associated keratitis (direct ophthalmology vs ED first).
  • Confirm whether primary care should initiate any empirical topical antibiotics before urgent referral (often discouraged if it delays assessment/culture; local policy varies).

Red eye (pain/photophobia/reduced vision) — urgent referral triggers

Background

  • Most red eyes are benign (e.g. conjunctivitis, subconjunctival haemorrhage), but some are sight-threatening.
  • Treat pain, photophobia and/or reduced vision in a red eye as red flags until proven otherwise.
  • Contact lens wear + painful red eye is high risk for microbial keratitis → needs same-day specialist assessment.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Check and document (including negatives): laterality, duration, discharge/itch, pain severity, photophobia, and visual acuity (each eye, with usual correction).
  • Always ask: contact lens use; trauma/high-velocity injury; foreign body sensation; chemical exposure (alkali worse).
  • Look for emergency features: corneal haze/opacity, irregular pupil, ciliary flush, hypopyon, severe unilateral headache ± nausea/vomiting (possible AACG), proptosis/fever/painful eye movements (possible orbital cellulitis).
  • If trained/equipped: fluorescein stain for epithelial defect/ulcer; lid eversion for subtarsal foreign body.

Primary care management

  • Chemical injury: irrigate immediately (do not delay). Arrange urgent same-day specialist review after irrigation.
  • If red flags: arrange same-day specialist assessment (local route). Avoid delays.
  • If clearly benign and no red flags (normal VA, minimal pain, no photophobia): manage as conjunctivitis/dry eye per local guidance + give strict safety-net advice (return same day if worse, or any pain/photophobia/vision change develops).
  • Contact lens users: stop lenses; keep lenses/case; do not restart until reviewed and symptoms fully resolved.

Who to refer (same day)

  • Same‑day ophthalmology/emergency eye service (or ED per local model) if any of:
    • Reduced visual acuity (new).
    • Moderate/severe pain.
    • Photophobia.
    • Contact lens wear with red eye (especially painful/photophobic/blurred vision) → suspect microbial keratitis.
    • Trauma (high‑velocity; glass/metal), suspected penetrating injury, retained foreign body.
    • Chemical injury (after immediate irrigation).
    • Corneal opacity/ulcer, irregular pupil, hypopyon, severe unilateral headache ± nausea/vomiting, proptosis/fever/painful eye movements.
  • Include in referral: VA each eye, contact lens status, trauma/chemical history, pain/photophobia, fluorescein findings (if done).

Who not to refer

  • Clearly benign red eye with normal VA, no photophobia, and no/modest pain only (e.g. mild conjunctivitis/dry eye), provided safety-netting is explicit.
  • Subconjunctival haemorrhage without pain/photophobia/vision change (manage conservatively; check BP/anticoagulants as appropriate).
