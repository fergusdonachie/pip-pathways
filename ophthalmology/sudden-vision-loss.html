Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Painless sudden monocular visual loss: treat as time‑critical until proven otherwise — key “can’t miss” causes include CRAO (retinal stroke), retinal detachment, and GCA (if >50 or suggestive symptoms).
  • CRAO / amaurosis fugax: specialist sources treat CRAO as a form of acute ischaemic stroke and recommend urgent triage to emergency medical attention + vascular risk assessment (secondary prevention), while simultaneously arranging ophthalmology review.
  • Flashes/floaters ± “curtain” field defect: this pattern is the classic retinal tear/detachment pathway — needs same‑day assessment with dilated fundoscopy; if there is any suspicion of detachment (history or exam) this is an urgent eye‑casualty/vitreoretinal referral.

1) Right Decisions (NHS Highland) — “Sudden visual loss” emergency guideline

  • Provides a pragmatic “what it looks like / what to do” for common sudden‑vision‑loss syndromes in urgent care.
  • CRAO: sudden profound monocular loss; RAPD; pale retina/cherry red spot may be seen. Guidance emphasises exclude GCA if >50; if GCA suspected give urgent IV steroids/admit, and arrange ophthalmology review same day (or next day if OOH).
  • Amaurosis fugax: treat via TIA protocol (also recognise GCA can present this way).
  • Retinal detachment: progressive shadow/field loss, flashes/floaters; advice includes position on back and arrange ophthalmology review same day (next day if OOH).

2) NHS Lothian RefHelp — “Flashes and floaters” guidance notes (optometry/primary care interface)

  • Emphasises that new flashes/floaters need careful history and (ideally) dilated fundus examination by an experienced clinician.
  • Features that warrant urgent referral include vitreous pigment (“tobacco dust”), vitreous/preretinal haemorrhage, retinal break/tear, and retinal detachment.
  • Patients should be asked about a subjective visual field defect (“curtain”) — if present they should attend eye casualty/urgent assessment.

3) AHA scientific statement (2021) — CRAO = retinal stroke

  • States that acute CRAO is a medical emergency and that systems should prioritise early recognition and triage to emergency medical attention.
  • Highlights the need for urgent screening and treatment of vascular risk factors (secondary prevention) and that IV thrombolysis may be effective in selected cases (specialist decision).
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • Confirm D&G routing for suspected CRAO: 999 to ED/stroke service vs direct eye casualty (and how to coordinate both).
  • Confirm the default local route for flashes/floaters: community optometrist same day vs direct referral to ophthalmology, and what to do out of hours.
  • Confirm local “red flag” threshold for GCA visual symptoms (immediate steroids/admission pathway + which service owns initial management).

Sudden vision loss

Background

  • Sudden visual loss is an emergency symptom until a time‑critical cause is excluded.
  • In primary care/OOH, focus on rapid routing rather than definitive diagnosis.
  • Key “can’t miss”: retinal stroke (CRAO), retinal detachment, giant cell arteritis (GCA) (age >50 or symptoms), and acute angle‑closure glaucoma (painful red eye).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Immediate questions (seconds):
    • Monocular or binocular? Sudden or progressive? Painful or painless?
    • Any field defect (“curtain/shadow”)? Flashes/floaters?
    • Any neurological symptoms (speech, face/arm weakness, ataxia)?
    • Age >50: any GCA symptoms (temporal headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, PMR symptoms, constitutional symptoms)?
  • Minimum exam to document (don’t delay transfer):
    • Visual acuity (each eye, with pinhole if available).
    • Pupils (look for relative afferent pupillary defect if trained).
    • Red eye/pain? Cornea hazy? Nausea/vomiting (angle‑closure pattern).
    • If safe/able: fundus view (may be limited).
  • Pattern clues (for routing):
    • Painless profound monocular loss ± RAPD → consider CRAO/retinal vascular occlusion (treat as emergency).
    • Flashes/floaters + new field defect (“curtain”) → retinal tear/detachment until proven otherwise.
    • Transient monocular loss (seconds–minutes) → amaurosis fugax (treat as TIA; also consider GCA).
    • Age >50 + visual symptoms + GCA features → time‑critical (steroids/admission pathway).

Primary care management

  • Do not delay urgent referral for blood tests, fundoscopy, or paperwork if time‑critical cause is suspected.
  • If suspected CRAO / retinal stroke: treat as medical emergency — arrange emergency medical attention (local route) and same‑day ophthalmology review.
  • If suspected GCA with visual symptoms: follow the local GCA emergency pathway (often immediate steroids + admission).
  • If suspected retinal detachment: avoid pressure on the eye; follow local advice (some pathways advise lying flat) and arrange same‑day ophthalmology.
  • Document for the receiving service: onset time, monocular/bilateral, pain/redness, VA each eye, key symptoms (flashes/floaters/field defect), GCA symptoms (>50), vascular risk factors/anticoagulants.

Who to refer

Routing depends on local model (ED/stroke vs eye casualty vs optometry). Use the fastest safe option.

Emergency (999 / ED / acute assessment)

  • Immediate emergency referral if any of the following:
    • Suspected CRAO (painless profound monocular loss; RAPD; “retinal stroke” pattern), or
    • Visual loss with new neurological symptoms (possible stroke), or
    • Suspected GCA with visual symptoms (age >50 + jaw claudication/temporal headache/scalp tenderness/PMR symptoms), or
    • Suspected acute angle‑closure glaucoma (painful red eye, headache, nausea/vomiting, haloes, hazy cornea).
  • Route (draft): 999/ED for stroke/GCA patterns + parallel same‑day ophthalmology contact where available.

Same‑day ophthalmology / eye casualty

  • Same‑day ophthalmology if:
    • Flashes/floaters with visual field defect (“curtain/shadow”) or reduced vision (possible retinal tear/detachment/vitreous haemorrhage), or
    • Sudden monocular visual loss where cause is uncertain (treat as urgent until assessed).
  • Out of hours (draft): if not an ED/999 pattern, arrange next‑day ophthalmology per local system.

TIA pathway (urgent neurovascular assessment)

  • Amaurosis fugax (transient monocular visual loss) → manage as TIA (local pathway) and also consider GCA if >50 with suggestive symptoms.

Who not to refer

  • Do not reassure/observe true sudden visual loss without a same‑day plan.
  • If symptoms are typical visual migraine (positive visual phenomena such as zig‑zags/kaleidoscope, bilateral, 15–20 min, normal exam once settled) and no red flags: usually no referral is needed (safety‑net if atypical).
