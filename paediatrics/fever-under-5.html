DGRefHelp drafts / Fever in under 5s

Primary care triage for fever with no obvious source in children aged under 5: traffic‑light risk stratification, age/temperature thresholds, same‑day escalation, and safety‑netting (NICE NG143; sepsis cross‑reference NG254).
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑14

Evidence summary (NICE NG143 + NG254)

Key recommendations used for this draft (NICE NG143)
  • First identify immediately life‑threatening features (airway/breathing/circulation compromise, decreased level of consciousness).
  • Risk‑stratify using the traffic light system (green/amber/red). Management is directed by the level of risk.
  • Record core observations (Temp, HR, RR, cap refill) and check dehydration signs. Cap refill ≥3s is an amber marker.
  • Age/temperature thresholds: <3 months with temp ≥38°C is high risk; 3–6 months with temp ≥39°C is at least intermediate risk.
  • Do not use duration of fever alone to predict serious illness; but fever ≥5 days should trigger assessment for Kawasaki disease.
  • Remote assessment: any red features should be face‑to‑face within 2 hours; amber features need face‑to‑face with urgency by clinical judgement; green can be managed at home with advice on when to seek further help.
Sepsis cross‑reference
  • NG143 explicitly prompts: think “Could this be sepsis?” and refer to NICE NG254 where symptoms/signs indicate possible sepsis.
  • This page does not reproduce NG254 risk tables; it signposts them and focuses on NG143’s practical fever framework.

High‑yield “specific illness” prompts in NG143

  • Meningococcal disease: fever + non‑blanching rash (especially purpura, ill‑looking, CRT ≥3s, neck stiffness).
  • Bacterial meningitis: fever + neck stiffness / bulging fontanelle / decreased level of consciousness / convulsive status epilepticus; note classic signs may be absent in infants.
  • Pneumonia: fever + tachypnoea / crackles / indrawing / nasal flaring / cyanosis (and SpO2 ≤95% is an amber feature in traffic‑light table).
  • Septic arthritis/osteomyelitis: fever + swollen joint/limb, non‑weight bearing or not using extremity.
  • UTI: fever + urinary symptoms (dysuria, frequency, new bedwetting, malodorous urine, etc.) or abdominal/loin tenderness.
  • Kawasaki disease: fever ≥5 days plus mucocutaneous features (conjunctivitis, cracked lips/strawberry tongue, rash, extremity changes, cervical nodes).
Scope note
This draft is for children aged under 5 with fever where there is no obvious source at initial presentation. If the presentation suggests a clear focused diagnosis (e.g., otitis media), use the focused condition pathway and still safety‑net for deterioration.

Fever in under 5s — assessment + initial management (primary care)

1) Immediate danger check (first pass)

  • Identify any immediately life‑threatening features (airway/breathing/circulation compromise; decreased level of consciousness).
  • If yes: urgent emergency transfer (999) + immediate supportive care per local protocol.

2) Core assessment (do for all)

  • Record: temperature, HR, RR, capillary refill time (CRT); BP if HR/CRT abnormal and facilities available.
  • Assess hydration: mucous membranes, urine output, skin turgor; look for dehydration markers (e.g., prolonged CRT, weak pulse, cool extremities).
  • Look for a source and screen for “specific illness” patterns (meningococcal disease/meningitis, pneumonia, septic arthritis, UTI, Kawasaki, encephalitis).
  • Think: Could this be sepsis? (see NICE NG254) — do not rely on fever alone.

3) Risk stratify (traffic light)

  • Red (high risk) features include: pale/mottled/ashen/blue; no response to social cues; appears ill to clinician; does not wake/stay awake; weak/high‑pitched/continuous cry; grunting; RR >60; moderate/severe chest indrawing; reduced skin turgor; bulging fontanelle; non‑blanching rash; neck stiffness; status epilepticus; focal neuro signs/seizures; age <3 months and temp ≥38°C.
  • Amber (intermediate risk) features include: pallor reported; not responding normally/no smile; wakes only with prolonged stimulation; decreased activity; nasal flaring; SpO2 ≤95% in air; tachypnoea (age‑banded); crackles; tachycardia (APLS thresholds); CRT ≥3s; dry mucosa; poor feeding; reduced urine output; rigors; fever ≥5 days; limb/joint swelling/non‑weight bearing; age 3–6 months and temp ≥39°C.
  • Green (low risk) features: normal colour; responds to social cues; content/smiles; wakes quickly; strong normal cry/not crying; normal skin/eyes; moist mucous membranes.

4) Decide urgency / where to send

  • Red: urgent same‑day specialist/secondary care assessment (often ED/paeds unit). If remote assessment: face‑to‑face within 2 hours. If immediately life‑threatening: 999.
  • Amber (no red): face‑to‑face assessment is required; urgency by clinical judgement. If no clear diagnosis: either provide a safety net (see below) or refer for paediatric assessment.
  • Green only: home care with robust safety‑net advice.

5) Investigations (high‑yield primary care framing)

  • If urinary symptoms or no clear source: consider UTI assessment (urine sample + dip/culture per local pathway; see also NICE UTI in under 16s guidance).
  • Infants <3 months with fever: NICE recommends blood tests (FBC, blood culture, CRP) + urine testing for UTI; CXR only if respiratory signs; stool culture if diarrhoea.
  • Do not routinely do CXR in children with signs suggesting pneumonia if not admitted.

6) Antibiotics (principles)

  • Do not prescribe oral antibiotics to children with fever without apparent source.
  • If suspected meningococcal disease: give parenteral antibiotics at the earliest opportunity (benzylpenicillin or 3rd‑gen cephalosporin) per NICE meningitis/meningococcal guidance and local protocol.

7) Safety net (minimum)

  • Provide clear verbal/written advice on red flags and how to access help urgently.
  • Consider planned follow‑up (time + place) for amber‑risk presentations being managed in the community.
  • Explicitly tell carers to seek urgent help if: child becomes less responsive, breathing worsens, feeding/urine output drops, rash becomes non‑blanching, seizures occur, or parental concern escalates.
