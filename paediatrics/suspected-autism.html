Evidence summary (triangulated)

  • Guidelines: Based on NICE CG128 (Recognition, referral and diagnosis of autism in children and young people) and CG170.
  • Key Signs: Persistent difficulties in social interaction and communication, coupled with restricted/repetitive behaviours.
  • Red Flag: Regression in language or social skills at any age is a significant red flag requiring immediate referral.
  • Assessment: History should cover developmental milestones, social interaction (eye contact, joint attention), and play skills. Parental concern is a strong predictor.
  • Referral Pathways: Usually involve a multidisciplinary team (Community Paediatrics, CDC, or CAMHS) depending on the child's age (often Under 5 vs School Age).

Background

  • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is characterized by persistent deficits in social communication/interaction and restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviour.
  • Parental concern regarding development or behaviour should always be taken seriously.
  • Early recognition and referral allow for timely support and intervention.

Assessment

  • Red Flags (Preschool <5 years):
    • Social: Lack of social smile, limited eye contact, not following a point (joint attention), preference for solitary play.
    • Communication: Delayed speech, unusual language (echolalia), lack of gestures (pointing/waving).
    • Behaviours: Repetitive movements (flapping/spinning), distress at minor changes, unusual sensory interests.
  • Red Flags (School Age 5-11 years):
    • Difficulty making/keeping friends; difficulty understanding social rules.
    • Literal interpretation of language (misses sarcasm).
    • Intense, highly focused interests; rigid preference for routine.
    • Behavioural outbursts ("meltdowns") often related to transitions or unstructured time.
  • Red Flags (Adolescents):
    • Long-standing social difficulties becoming apparent as demands increase.
    • Social isolation; preference for online interaction.
  • Clinical Observation: Observe behaviour/interaction during consultation. Look for dysmorphic features.

Primary Care Management

  • Rule out other causes: Check vision and hearing.
  • Information Gathering:
    • Detailed developmental history and milestones.
    • Family history of autism/neurodevelopmental conditions.
    • School/Nursery information: Reports are crucial for referral triage.
  • Support:
    • Validate concerns and manage expectations (waiting times).
    • Signpost to support (e.g., National Autistic Society).
    • Advise parents to engage with school SENCO (diagnosis not required for support).

Who to refer

  • Immediate Referral: Any child with regression in language or social skills.
  • Routine Referral: Persistent concerns about autism based on signs/red flags (social communication + restrictive/repetitive behaviours).
  • Routing (Check Local Pathway):
    • Under 5s: Typically to Community Paediatrics or Child Development Centre (CDC).
    • School Age / Adolescents: Typically to CAMHS or dedicated Neurodevelopmental Pathway.

Who not to refer

  • Isolated speech delay with good social communication (refer to Speech & Language Therapy).
  • Behavioural difficulties clearly linked to environmental factors/trauma (consider Early Help / Social Work / Parenting Support).
