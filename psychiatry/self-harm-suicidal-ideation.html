Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Immediate safety first: establish whether there is immediate concern about safety and whether urgent physical treatment is needed (NICE NG225).
  • Psychosocial assessment: at the earliest opportunity after self-harm, a mental health professional should carry out psychosocial assessment; do not delay purely because the person is intoxicated or awaiting completion of medical treatment (NICE NG225).
  • Do not use risk tools/scores: do not use risk scales to predict future suicide or repetition, or to decide discharge/treatment (NICE NG225).
  • Primary care referral priority: in primary care, referral to mental health professionals is a priority if distress is rising/high/sustained, self-harm or suicidal intent is increasing, the clinician is concerned, or the person asks for support (NICE NG225).

What this page is (and isn’t)

  • This draft is for initial primary care triage (including remote triage) and routing. It is not a substitute for specialist psychosocial assessment.
  • Local crisis services and out-of-hours routes vary; these are captured under Local agreement needed.

Self-harm / suicidal ideation (primary care)

Background

  • Self-harm (NICE NG225) = intentional self-poisoning or self-injury, irrespective of purpose.
  • Priorities in primary care: address urgent physical needs, assess immediate safety, and route for timely psychosocial assessment (do not use risk scores to decide discharge).

Immediate triage (do first)

  • If immediate danger (active attempt/active self-harm, cannot stay safe, reduced consciousness, severe agitation/psychosis, uncontrolled bleeding, serious overdose/poisoning) → use 999 / ED.
  • If urgent but stable (significant suicidal intent with plan/means, escalating self-harm, severe distress, severe intoxication, psychosis/mania, major safeguarding concern) → arrange same‑day urgent assessment via local acute/crisis route.
  • Always check for safeguarding concerns (domestic abuse, exploitation, children/dependants at risk) and follow local procedures.

Assessment (primary care) — keep it decision-focused

  • What happened: method/means, timing, injuries/poisoning, current physical symptoms.
  • Current intent: suicidal thoughts, intent, plan, access to means, rehearsal/preparation, reasons for living/protective factors.
  • Context: triggers, recent losses, relationship issues, work/financial stress, domestic abuse.
  • Mental state: agitation, hopelessness, psychotic symptoms, mania, severe anxiety.
  • Substances: alcohol/drug intoxication or withdrawal (may raise immediate risk).
  • Support: who is with them now, who can stay with them, willingness to involve family/carers.
  • Capacity/consent: can they engage with a plan? consent to share information (seek senior/legal advice if capacity/confidentiality dilemmas).
  • Do not use risk assessment tools/scales to predict suicide or repetition, or to decide discharge/treatment (NICE NG225).

Primary care management (while arranging help)

  • Address physical health needs (wounds/overdose) and do not delay escalation if urgent care is required.
  • Reduce access to means where possible (therapeutic discussion about removing/locking away tablets/sharps/ligatures; involve family/carers if appropriate).
  • Safety plan (short-term): agree what they will do if urges escalate (who to contact, where to go, crisis numbers, 999 threshold).
  • Involve family/carers with consent where possible; even without consent, they may provide useful information (NICE NG225).
  • Medication safety: consider medicines review and limiting quantities of high-toxicity drugs (e.g., TCAs) as part of safer prescribing (local policy).

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency and the correct local route (crisis/CMHT/ED/SDEC). This is local-process sensitive.

999 / ED now

  • Life‑threatening injury or poisoning/overdose; reduced consciousness; uncontrolled bleeding.
  • Active attempt/active self-harm or cannot keep self safe.
  • Immediate violence risk to others or severe behavioural disturbance requiring emergency support.

Same‑day urgent assessment (local crisis/ED/SDEC)

  • Significant suicidal intent with plan and access to means.
  • Escalating frequency/severity of self-harm, or increasing suicidal intent.
  • Severe distress, severe intoxication, psychosis/mania, or major safeguarding concern.

Urgent mental health assessment (priority)

  • After any self-harm episode presenting in primary care: consider referral to mental health/social care services for psychosocial assessment, or inform existing mental health team (with consent).
  • Make referral a priority when (NICE NG225): distress is rising/high/sustained; self-harm/suicidal intent is increasing; the clinician is concerned; or the person requests support.

Who not to refer

  • Avoid “no follow‑up” after self-harm. If not referring urgently, ensure active follow‑up, support resources, and care for coexisting mental health problems.

Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)

Safety net
If the person becomes unsafe, begins self-harming, takes an overdose, or cannot be supervised safely, use the emergency route (999/ED). If they are safe to remain in the community, ensure a clear plan for urgent assessment and follow-up.
