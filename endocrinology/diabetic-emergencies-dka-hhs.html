Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Suspected DKA or HHS is an emergency → same‑day hospital assessment (usually ED/999 depending on illness severity).
  • DKA suspicion: diabetes (known or new) or glucose >11 mmol/L plus clinical features (vomiting, abdominal pain, tachypnoea/Kussmaul, dehydration, reduced consciousness). If available, check ketones: blood ketones >3 mmol/L or urine ketones ≥2+ = high.
  • DKA can be euglycaemic (normal/near‑normal glucose) — do not exclude DKA if symptomatic; consider SGLT2 inhibitors and prolonged fasting/vomiting.
  • HHS typical profile: marked dehydration, very high glucose (often ≥30 mmol/L), high osmolality (≥320 mOsm/kg) with no significant ketonaemia (≤3 mmol/L) and no significant acidosis (pH >7.3; bicarbonate ≥15). In primary care you usually won’t measure osmolality/pH — treat as emergency based on clinical picture + glucose/ketones.

1) NICE CKS (Type 1 diabetes) — “Suspecting diabetic ketoacidosis”

  • CKS gives a pragmatic primary care suspicion set: known diabetes or features of diabetes or significant hyperglycaemia (capillary glucose >11 mmol/L) + symptoms/signs (vomiting, abdominal pain, Kussmaul breathing, dehydration, reduced consciousness).
  • CKS explicitly notes hyperglycaemia may not always be present (DKA with normal glucose can occur, e.g. in people on insulin therapy; also consider starvation/vomiting states).
  • Ketone thresholds in adults: urine ketones ≥2+ or blood ketones >3 mmol/L = high.

2) JBDS (HHS) — updated guideline (open access summary)

  • JBDS describes characteristic biochemical features of HHS (used in hospital): marked hypovolaemia, osmolality ≥320 ([(2×Na+) + glucose + urea]), glucose ≥30, ketones ≤3, pH >7.3 and bicarbonate ≥15.
  • JBDS highlights that mixed DKA/HHS can occur — so a ketone result doesn’t always separate them cleanly; if unwell, treat as emergency regardless.

3) NICE NG17 (Type 1 diabetes in adults) — DKA requires emergency hospital treatment

  • NICE information for the public reinforces: DKA is due to ketone build‑up and requires emergency hospital treatment.
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • Confirm local route for same‑day assessment when patient is stable but DKA/HHS is suspected (ED vs SDEC/acute medicine vs diabetes team direct).
  • Confirm whether D&G expects primary care to use capillary blood ketone meters (and whether practices have access) vs urine ketones only.
  • Confirm local advice for people on SGLT2 inhibitors with possible euglycaemic DKA (stop drug; same‑day assessment threshold; where to send).

Diabetic emergencies (DKA/HHS) — recognition & triage

Background

  • DKA and HHS are diabetic emergencies. If suspected, arrange same‑day hospital assessment (usually ED/999 depending on how unwell).
  • DKA can occur in type 1 (and sometimes type 2) diabetes; euglycaemic DKA can occur (normal/near‑normal glucose), especially with prolonged fasting/vomiting and in people taking SGLT2 inhibitors.
  • HHS typically affects older people with type 2 diabetes and develops over days with profound dehydration and very high glucose.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Initial triage (ABCDE): check observations; consider NEWS2 if available. If shocked, severely drowsy/confused, or unable to keep fluids down → 999.
  • Suspect DKA in someone with known diabetes or new diabetes symptoms, or capillary glucose >11 mmol/L, plus any of:
    • nausea/vomiting
    • abdominal pain
    • tachypnoea or deep “sighing” (Kussmaul) breathing
    • dehydration
    • reduced level of consciousness (irritability, lethargy, drowsiness, confusion)
  • Check ketones if practical (do not delay transfer if very unwell):
    • Blood ketones >3 mmol/L = high (supports DKA).
    • Urine ketones ≥2+ = high.
    • Remember: DKA can be present with normal/near‑normal glucose (euglycaemic DKA).
  • Suspect HHS if: very high capillary glucose (often ≥30 mmol/L) with marked dehydration and increasing drowsiness/confusion; ketones often absent/low. (Hospital diagnosis uses osmolality ≥320 mOsm/kg.)
  • Look for triggers: infection, vomiting/gastroenteritis, missed insulin/meds, MI/stroke, pancreatitis, steroids/antipsychotics/diuretics, alcohol/drug misuse.

Primary care management

  • Do not manage suspected DKA/HHS in the community. Arrange same‑day hospital assessment.
  • 999 / blue‑light transfer if any red flags: shock/hypotension, severe dehydration, Kussmaul breathing/respiratory distress, persistent vomiting, severe abdominal pain, reduced consciousness/confusion, seizures.
  • If stable enough for non‑blue‑light transfer: discuss with local acute pathway and send urgently the same day (do not book routine clinic).
  • While arranging transfer: do not delay for bloods; provide medication list (incl. SGLT2 inhibitors) + timing of last insulin/diabetes meds; document ketone result if measured.

Who to refer

This is an emergency triage page. If suspected DKA/HHS, refer same day.

  • Send same day for hospital assessment if suspected DKA or HHS.
  • 999 / ED immediately if any red flags: shock/hypotension, severe dehydration, Kussmaul breathing/respiratory distress, persistent vomiting, severe abdominal pain, reduced consciousness/confusion, seizures.
  • If ketones are high (blood ketones >3 mmol/L or urine ≥2+) treat as suspected DKA and send urgently.
  • If very high glucose (often ≥30 mmol/L) + dehydration + confusion with low/absent ketones: treat as suspected HHS and send urgently.

Who not to refer

  • If DKA/HHS is suspected: do not keep in primary care.
  • If glucose is modestly raised but the patient is well and ketones are negative, manage per the relevant diabetes pathway with safety‑netting (this page is for emergencies only).
