Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Don’t miss emergency / type 1 diabetes: hyperglycaemia with ketones, rapid weight loss, dehydration, vomiting, or very unwell → same‑day acute assessment (possible DKA/HHS). Type 1 can occur at any age/BMI.
  • At diagnosis, structured education is part of care: NICE says offer structured education at diagnosis (type 2) and offer a proven structured programme for type 1 (typically within 6–12 months).
  • Baseline complications/CV risk checks are part of “day 1” setup: weight/BMI, BP, smoking; renal (eGFR) and urine ACR; feet; arrange eye screening; document hypoglycaemia risk/driver status if relevant.

1) NICE NG28 — Type 2 diabetes in adults (management)

  • Structured education should be offered at the time of diagnosis with annual reinforcement and review.
  • HbA1c monitoring: 3–6 monthly until stable; then 6‑monthly.
  • Self‑monitoring of capillary glucose is not routine unless on insulin, there is hypoglycaemia, or specific short‑term scenarios (e.g. steroids).
  • First‑line glucose‑lowering medication: standard‑release metformin (unless contraindicated / not tolerated; then modified release).

2) NICE NG17 — Type 1 diabetes in adults (diagnosis + early care plan)

  • Make an initial diagnosis of type 1 diabetes on clinical grounds in adults with hyperglycaemia; typical features include ketosis, rapid weight loss, onset <50, BMI <25, and personal/family autoimmune disease — but do not use age/BMI alone to include/exclude.
  • At diagnosis, develop an early care plan including: medical assessment; education plan; insulin/self‑monitoring plan; driving/special situations; and baseline assessment including weight/BMI, foot exam, urine ACR + eGFR, and psychological wellbeing.

3) SIGN diabetes guidance (Scotland)

  • SIGN highlights comprehensive diabetes care beyond medicines: foot disease prevention, visual impairment prevention, kidney disease, and psychosocial aspects (SIGN 116/154 suite). (Note: SIGN 116 is older, but still provides a Scotland‑focused framework.)

New diabetes diagnosis (adults) — baseline tests + referral/education route

Background

  • New diabetes diagnosis needs two parallel actions: (1) make it safe today (exclude emergency/type 1) and (2) set up long‑term care (baseline complications/CV risk checks + education + treatment plan).
  • Type 1 diabetes can occur at any age and BMI. If in doubt, treat as possible type 1 and seek same‑day specialist/acute advice.
  • Structured education is part of diabetes care: offer at diagnosis for type 2; offer a proven programme for type 1 (typically within 6–12 months).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Same‑day / emergency features (possible DKA/HHS): very unwell, vomiting, dehydration, drowsy/confused, Kussmaul breathing, hypotension, severe abdominal pain, or ketones present (blood ketones or urine ketones).
  • Features suggesting type 1 diabetes (NICE): ketosis, rapid weight loss, onset <50, BMI <25, autoimmune history — but do not use age/BMI alone to rule in/out.
  • Confirm the diagnosis (if not already clear): HbA1c and/or plasma glucose per local diagnostic policy; review meds (steroids/antipsychotics) and intercurrent illness.
  • Baseline data at/soon after diagnosis:
    • Weight, BMI, BP; smoking status; alcohol; activity; diet.
    • Bloods: renal function (eGFR), lipids; consider LFTs and FBC (local bundles vary).
    • Urine: albumin:creatinine ratio (ACR) and dip (document haematuria/protein).
    • Feet: pulses, sensation, skin, deformity; identify urgent foot problems.
    • Eyes: ensure diabetic retinal screening is arranged.
  • Driving/work: check if they drive professionally or have hypoglycaemia risk; plan monitoring accordingly.

Primary care management

  • If stable and likely type 2 diabetes:
    • Start lifestyle support (diet/activity/weight) and offer structured education at diagnosis.
    • Agree an initial HbA1c target and monitoring plan (typically 3–6 monthly until stable).
    • Consider metformin (standard release) as first‑line medication unless contraindicated/not tolerated.
    • Do not routinely issue glucose‑monitoring strips unless a clear indication (e.g. insulin, hypoglycaemia risk, steroids).
  • Safety‑net: advise urgent review if vomiting, dehydration, drowsiness, rapid deterioration, or ketones; consider checking ketones if symptomatic hyperglycaemia.
  • Cardiovascular risk: assess BP/lipids/smoking and manage per local CV prevention guidance (statin/ACE‑i decisions are local‑policy sensitive).

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency. Local processes vary: include a clear summary of symptoms, ketone status, and key results (HbA1c/glucose, eGFR, ACR if available).

Same‑day acute assessment (ED/acute medicine)

  • Any concern for DKA/HHS: very unwell, vomiting, dehydration, confusion/drowsiness, hypotension, Kussmaul breathing, or ketones present.
  • Pregnancy with hyperglycaemia or suspected diabetes (urgent obstetric/diabetes pathway).

Urgent specialist diabetes input

  • Suspected type 1 diabetes (clinical pattern or ketones), or uncertainty about classification where insulin may be needed.
  • Marked symptomatic hyperglycaemia requiring rescue therapy (local protocols vary).

Routine referral / onward setup

  • Newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes: refer/book structured education and ensure community diabetes support (DSN/dietitian) per local pathway.
  • Any new diagnosis: ensure retinal screening and foot risk stratification are set up (local routes).

Who not to refer

  • Clinically well adult with likely type 2 diabetes, no ketosis/acute illness, and clear primary care pathway for baseline checks + education + first‑line treatment.
