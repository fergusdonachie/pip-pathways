Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Thyroid storm is rare but time‑critical: if suspected (systemic toxicity + severe thyrotoxicosis), this is a 999 / ED resus problem, not a routine endocrine referral.
  • For symptomatic thyrotoxicosis without shock/toxicity, supportive treatment (often a beta‑blocker) can reduce symptoms while awaiting specialist assessment (NICE NG145).
  • Baseline confirmation usually starts with TSH then reflex FT4/FT3 (NICE NG145). If confirmed thyrotoxicosis, specialist workup often includes TRAb to confirm Graves’ (NICE NG145).

1) NICE NG145 (thyroid disease: assessment & management)

  • Investigation approach: for adults where secondary thyroid dysfunction is not suspected, measure TSH first; if TSH is low, measure FT4 and FT3 in the same sample.
  • Initial treatment note: transient thyrotoxicosis (e.g. thyroiditis) usually needs supportive treatment such as beta‑blockers.
  • Confirmed thyrotoxicosis: in adults, differentiate Graves’ vs other causes using TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb); consider technetium scan if TRAb negative.

2) Thyroid storm / endocrine emergency (secondary sources + NHS example guideline)

  • Thyroid storm typically presents with a combination of marked tachycardia/arrhythmia, hyperthermia, CNS disturbance (agitation/delirium), and GI/hepatic features, often with precipitating illness (infection, surgery, trauma, iodine load, stopping antithyroid meds).
  • NHS trust endocrine emergency guidelines commonly emphasise: treat as time‑critical hospital emergency requiring senior input, monitoring, and multi‑drug treatment (beta‑blockade + antithyroid drug + iodine + steroids + supportive care).

Hyperthyroid red flags (thyroid storm risk) — primary care triage

Background

  • Hyperthyroidism/thyrotoxicosis can cause significant morbidity (AF, heart failure, weight loss, osteoporosis) and rarely can progress to thyroid storm (life‑threatening endocrine emergency).
  • This page is a triage aid: identify who needs emergency care, who needs urgent specialist assessment, and what baseline tests are useful before referral.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Confirm suspected thyrotoxicosis
    • Bloods: TSH (if low, add FT4 and FT3 in same sample).
    • Ask about: iodine exposure (contrast/amiodarone), recent pregnancy, thyroiditis symptoms, missed antithyroid meds.
  • Red flags / emergency patterns
    • Thyroid storm / systemic toxicity: high fever, marked tachycardia/AF, agitation/delirium, vomiting/diarrhoea, jaundice, heart failure, hypotension/shock.
    • Cardiac complications: new AF, chest pain, syncope, decompensated heart failure, resting HR persistently very high.
    • Goitre compression: stridor, dyspnoea, dysphagia, hoarse voice/voice change, rapid enlargement.
    • Thyroid eye disease red flags: reduced visual acuity, diplopia, pain, colour desaturation, corneal exposure.
    • Pregnancy/postpartum or children/young people with suspected hyperthyroidism → urgent specialist input.
  • Basics: pulse, BP, temperature, weight; signs of HF; thyroid exam (size/tenderness/nodules); eye signs.
  • ECG if palpitations, tachycardia, breathlessness, dizziness/syncope, or age >50.

Primary care management

  • If thyroid storm suspected: call 999 / send to ED (do not manage in community).
  • If symptomatic thyrotoxicosis but stable:
    • Consider beta‑blocker for symptom control (follow local policy; caution in asthma/COPD, hypotension, bradycardia).
    • Safety‑net: worsening breathlessness, chest pain, syncope, confusion, fever, dehydration → same‑day assessment.
  • Do not start antithyroid drugs in primary care unless there is an agreed local protocol / specialist advice.

Who to refer

Emergency / same‑day (999 / ED)

  • Suspected thyroid storm (systemic toxicity: high fever, marked tachycardia/AF, delirium, vomiting/diarrhoea, jaundice, heart failure, hypotension/shock).
  • Thyrotoxicosis with decompensated heart failure, chest pain/ACS concern, syncope or haemodynamic instability.
  • Goitre compression symptoms (stridor/airway concerns).

Urgent specialist assessment (route depends on local service)

  • Confirmed thyrotoxicosis with significant symptoms, persistent marked tachycardia, or new AF (even if stable).
  • Pregnancy/postpartum thyrotoxicosis.
  • Children/young people with suspected hyperthyroidism.
  • Suspected Graves’ with active eye disease or visual symptoms.

Referral letter essentials

  • Symptoms and duration; vitals (HR/BP/temp); ECG result (if done).
  • TFTs (TSH, FT4, FT3); current meds incl. amiodarone/iodine exposure; pregnancy status.
  • Red flags present/absent (HF features, neuro symptoms, fever).
  • Route (draft placeholder): SCI Gateway → Endocrinology (urgent) OR Acute medicine/SDEC for same‑day assessment, depending on stability. Confirm local pathway.

Who not to refer

  • Isolated mild symptoms with normal thyroid function tests (consider other causes; recheck TFTs if symptoms evolve).
  • Borderline abnormal TFTs without symptoms: repeat as per local/NICE guidance and clinical context (do not over‑diagnose during acute illness).
