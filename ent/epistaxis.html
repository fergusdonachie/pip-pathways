Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Unwell / heavy ongoing bleed (airway concern, haemodynamic compromise, syncope, ongoing brisk bleeding) → treat as an emergency (999/ED/same‑day acute pathway).
  • Most epistaxis is anterior and stops with correct first aid: sit forward + pinch the soft part of the nose continuously for 10–20 minutes.
  • Recurrent epistaxis usually needs moisturising / antiseptic cream (vestibulitis/crusting), risk‑factor review (antithrombotics, nasal trauma), and safety‑netting; escalate if red flags for malignancy or bleeding disorder.

1) First aid works (but only if done properly)

  • RCEMLearning and multiple NHS pathways emphasise that a large proportion of ED presentations could be avoided with correct first aid.
  • Technique: upright, lean forward; pinch the soft (fleshy) part of the nose firmly and continuously for at least 10 minutes (often taught as 15–20 minutes without releasing pressure). Encourage spitting out blood (don’t swallow).
  • Ice packs to forehead/neck are not reliably helpful, but sucking ice may reduce nasal blood flow (RCEMLearning); ENT SHO includes ice as an adjunct while pressure is applied.

2) Don’t miss emergencies and high‑risk groups

  • Start with ABC assessment even if bleeding appears to have stopped (RCEMLearning).
  • Higher risk for deterioration / significant blood loss: older/frail people, people with known coagulopathy, and those on anticoagulants/antiplatelets.
  • ENT SHO “red flags”: full‑dose anticoagulants (e.g. warfarin/heparin), ongoing bleeding despite effective packing, trauma with altered anatomy.

3) Antithrombotics: don’t stop blindly

  • ENT UK patient guidance: nosebleeds are a common side effect of antithrombotics; do not stop these medicines unless advised by a clinician.
  • ENT SHO: there is usually no benefit stopping warfarin/aspirin if bleeding stops with simple measures or an anterior pack; consider reversal only for torrential bleeding not responding to packing or uncontrolled anticoagulation.

4) Recurrent epistaxis in primary care: focus on cause + prevention

  • Several NHS pathways (e.g. Remedy) point to vestibulitis/crusting management (topical antiseptic/antibiotic ointment) and avoiding nasal trauma as first‑line for recurrence.
  • Investigations are often unnecessary in a well patient after a single self‑limited bleed; consider FBC if heavy/recurrent, and coagulation/INR if on warfarin or coagulopathy suspected.
Red flags suggesting serious underlying cause (consider urgent assessment/referral)
  • Haemodynamic compromise, syncope, ongoing brisk bleeding despite correct first aid.
  • Bleeding disorder pattern: easy bruising/bleeding elsewhere, known thrombocytopenia or liver disease.
  • Possible head/neck malignancy features (examples used in NHS pathways): nasal obstruction, persistent unilateral rhinorrhoea, facial pain, cranial neuropathy, proptosis, persistent lymphadenopathy.
  • Children <2 years with epistaxis (rare: consider underlying cause/trauma; follow safeguarding procedures).

Epistaxis

Background

  • Most epistaxis is anterior and self-limiting, but it can be serious in frail patients and those on anticoagulants.
  • The most important first step is correct first aid: sit upright, lean forward, and pinch the soft part of the nose continuously for 10–20 minutes.
  • Two things not to miss: (1) an unstable/heavy bleed (airway or haemodynamic compromise) and (2) red flags for underlying disease (bleeding disorder or head/neck malignancy features).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Immediate triage (ABCDE): if active bleeding assess airway risk + haemodynamic compromise (tachycardia, hypotension, syncope, ongoing brisk bleeding).
  • History (key): duration, unilateral/bilateral, recurrent episodes, trauma/nose picking, anticoagulants/antiplatelets (warfarin/DOACs/aspirin/clopidogrel), liver disease/bleeding history, cocaine.
  • Examination: vitals; look for ongoing bleeding; check for posterior trickle (blood in oropharynx); consider nasal mucosa crusting/vestibulitis; look for red flags (nasal obstruction, persistent unilateral discharge, facial pain, lymphadenopathy, cranial nerve symptoms).
  • Tests (when indicated):
    • FBC if heavy/recurrent bleed or frail.
    • INR if on warfarin; coagulation studies only if anticoagulant use or coagulopathy suspected.

Primary care management

  • First aid (gold standard): sit upright, lean forward, pinch the soft part of the nose firmly for 10–20 minutes without releasing; encourage spitting out blood.
  • If it stops: reassure + safety-net. Avoid nose blowing/picking for a few days; avoid strenuous exercise and very hot drinks/food for 24–48 hours.
  • If recurrent but not actively bleeding: treat crusting/vestibulitis (e.g. topical antiseptic/antibiotic cream such as Naseptin® or mupirocin; check peanut/soya allergy for Naseptin). Consider moisturising measures and address triggers (rhinitis, trauma).
  • Antithrombotics: do not advise stopping aspirin/warfarin/DOACs without a plan — coordinate with the appropriate service if significant/recurrent bleeding.

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency. Err on the side of same‑day assessment if the patient is frail, anticoagulated, or the story suggests significant blood loss.

Emergency / same‑day assessment (999 / ED / acute pathway)

  • Any haemodynamic compromise, airway concern, syncope, or ongoing brisk bleeding.
  • Bleeding not stopping after correct first aid (10–20 minutes continuous pressure).
  • Significant trauma or suspected posterior bleed (blood down throat, bilateral brisk bleeding).
  • High-risk: frail/elderly, significant comorbidity, or anticoagulated with ongoing bleeding.

Urgent ENT / suspected cancer pathway

  • Red flags for head/neck malignancy: persistent unilateral nasal obstruction/discharge, facial pain, cranial neuropathy, persistent lymphadenopathy, or visible lesion.
  • Recurrent epistaxis with anaemia or suspected bleeding disorder (consider urgent assessment; local route).

Routine ENT

  • Recurrent troublesome epistaxis despite preventative measures (moisturising/antiseptic cream + avoidance advice).
  • Unilateral recurrent bleeds where a treatable anterior bleeding point is suspected (may be suitable for cautery depending on local service).
  • Route (draft placeholder): SCI Gateway → ENT (routine) OR local ENT rapid-access clinic if available.

Who not to refer

  • Single self‑limited anterior nosebleed that stops with correct first aid and has no red flags.
  • Recurrent minor bleeds with clear vestibulitis/crusting that improve with topical antiseptic/antibiotic cream and avoidance advice.
