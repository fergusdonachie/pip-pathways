1) NICE NG84 (acute sore throat: antimicrobial prescribing)

  • Most acute sore throats resolve within ~1 week without antibiotics; management is mainly symptomatic.
  • NICE recommends using FeverPAIN or Centor to identify people more likely to benefit from antibiotics, while emphasising harms/limited benefit.
  • NICE advises reassessment at any time if symptoms worsen rapidly/significantly, or the person becomes systemically very unwell, and to consider alternative diagnoses/complications (including suppurative complications such as quinsy/deep neck space infections, and Lemierre syndrome).

2) ENT red flags (practical emergency-pattern recognition)

  • ENT emergency teaching resources emphasise: severe sore throat + severe dysphagia with little to see in the oropharynx, or hoarse/croaky voice, should raise concern for epiglottitis/supraglottitis until proven otherwise (stridor can be late).
  • Deep neck space infection can present with fever/toxicity plus neck swelling/tenderness, trismus, torticollis, drooling, stertor/stridor â†’ treat as potential airway emergency.
  • Quinsy usually requires drainage; admission decisions often hinge on ability to swallow/maintain hydration and clinical stability.

Sore throat / quinsy red flags

Background

  • Most acute sore throats (pharyngitis/tonsillitis) are self-limiting and settle within ~1 week.
  • Two things to prioritise: (1) airway/rapid deterioration (epiglottitis/deep neck infection) and (2) suppurative complications (especially quinsy).
  • Use FeverPAIN/Centor to guide antibiotic decisions only after excluding red flags.

Assessment (primary care) â†’ collapsible

    • Airway danger / epiglottitis pattern: severe sore throat with severe dysphagia (canâ†’t swallow fluids/saliva), drooling, stridor, muffled voice/hoarseness, respiratory distress, or severe pain with a normal-looking oropharynx.
    • Deep neck infection pattern: toxic appearance, neck swelling/tenderness, trismus, torticollis, drooling, stertor/stridor.
    • If any of the above â†’ same-day emergency assessment (do not delay for scoring tools).
  • Look for quinsy (peritonsillar abscess): unilateral severe sore throat  otalgia, fever, trismus, muffled hot potato voice, uvula deviation, palatal/tonsillar bulge.
  • Vitals / severity: temperature, HR, RR, SpO2; hydration; sepsis features.
  • If uncomplicated sore throat: consider FeverPAIN or Centor to estimate bacterial likelihood and guide antibiotic use.
  • Consider alternative diagnoses: glandular fever (EBV), scarlet fever, COVID/flu; and rare complications (e.g. Lemierre syndrome).

Primary care management

  • Symptomatic care (most cases): paracetamol or ibuprofen; encourage adequate fluids.
  • Antibiotics (uncomplicated sore throat): follow NICE NG84 (use FeverPAIN/Centor; consider no antibiotic  back-up  immediate depending on score and clinical context).
  • Safety-net (give explicitly): seek urgent help if symptoms worsen rapidly/significantly, if new breathing difficulty/stridor/drooling develops, if unable to swallow enough to stay hydrated, or if becoming systemically very unwell.

Who to refer

Treat airway concerns as emergencies. Do not delay referral while applying scoring tools.

Same-day emergency assessment (999/ED)

  • Any airway concern: stridor, respiratory distress, drooling, inability to swallow saliva, severe dysphagia with muffled/hoarse voice, or severe sore throat with a normal oropharyngeal exam (possible epiglottitis).
  • Suspected deep neck space infection: toxic, neck swelling/tenderness, trismus + systemic illness, torticollis, stertor/stridor.
  • Sepsis features or haemodynamic instability.

Same-day ENT / acute assessment (likely via ED unless local direct route exists)

  • Suspected quinsy (peritonsillar abscess): unilateral severe sore throat + trismus and/or muffled hot potato voice and/or uvula deviation/palatal bulge.
  • Unable to swallow enough fluid to stay hydrated.
  • Immunocompromised, significant comorbidity, or failed oral treatment with worsening.
  • Route (draft): ED â†’ ENT assessment (confirm local D&G process).

Who not to refer

  • Uncomplicated acute sore throat without red flags, able to maintain hydration, and not systemically very unwell.
