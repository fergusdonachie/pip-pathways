Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Simple otitis externa: topical drops + keep ear dry; no role for oral antibiotics unless spread beyond the canal / immunocompromised.
  • Severe canal swelling / unable to instil drops or severe pain → same‑day ENT assessment may be needed (microsuction + wick).
  • Necrotising (malignant) otitis externa / skull base osteomyelitis risk: think in older, diabetes, immunocompromised; red flags include pain out of proportion, granulation tissue/exposed bone, and cranial nerve palsy (esp facial palsy) → emergency ENT.
  • Don’t miss canal malignancy: rare, but consider if unilateral “otitis externa” not improving with appropriate topical therapy.

1) Right Decisions (GGC primary care): otitis externa

  • Simple otitis externa: keep ear dry; topical steroid/antibiotic drops suggested; no oral antibiotics for simple otitis externa.
  • Emergency ENT if significant pain or canal swollen shut (may need microsuction + wick).
  • Necrotising otitis externa can occur in immunosuppressed/older/diabetes; red flags include pain out of proportion and facial palsy → emergency ENT.
  • Consider malignancy if presumed unilateral otitis externa not improving with topical treatment.

2) Remedy (BNSSG ICB): red flags and escalation (UK pathway example)

  • Defines necrotising otitis externa as temporal bone osteomyelitis after canal infection; risk factors: older age, diabetes, immunocompromise.
  • Red flags: unremitting/disproportionate otalgia (esp overnight), systemically unwell/high fever, cranial nerve involvement (often facial palsy), granulation tissue/exposed bone at the bone‑cartilage junction.
  • Systemic antibiotics: only consider (in addition to topical) if cellulitis/spread beyond canal or immunocompromised.
  • Notes the ototoxicity caution for aminoglycoside drops when TM perforation is present; ciprofloxacin drops are generally preferred if perforation is suspected (specialist advice/local guidance).

3) Devon NHS referral guidance (UK pathway example): features prompting same‑day ENT discussion

  • Suggests necrotising otitis externa / malignancy if severe pain/headache, diabetes/older age, immunocompromise, granulation tissue at bone‑cartilage junction, facial palsy/cranial nerve impairment → same‑day ENT discussion.

Otitis externa (severe / necrotising risk)

Background

  • Otitis externa is inflammation/infection of the external ear canal causing otalgia (can be severe) ± itch ± otorrhoea ± conductive hearing loss.
  • Most cases are managed with topical treatment + self‑care. Oral antibiotics are not indicated for uncomplicated otitis externa.
  • Two things not to miss: (1) severe disease where drops can’t be delivered (may need wick/microsuction), and (2) necrotising otitis externa (skull base osteomyelitis) in high‑risk patients.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Ask about risk factors for necrotising otitis externa: older age, diabetes, immunocompromise (incl. steroids/chemo/biologics).
  • Examine: pinna tenderness, tragal pressure pain, canal oedema/discharge; try to assess if the canal is occluded (can drops reach the canal?).
  • Red flags / emergency ENT (necrotising OE / serious complication):
    • Pain out of proportion to findings or unremitting nocturnal pain
    • Facial palsy or other cranial nerve impairment
    • Granulation tissue or exposed bone at the bone–cartilage junction of the canal
    • Systemically unwell / high fever
  • Think canal malignancy (rare): unilateral presumed otitis externa not improving with appropriate topical treatment.

Primary care management

  • Self‑care: keep ear dry; avoid cotton buds/trauma; consider cotton wool with Vaseline when showering/bathing.
  • Analgesia: treat pain proactively (often severe).
  • Topical treatment: use local antimicrobial guidance (often steroid + antibiotic drops).
  • Oral antibiotics: not for uncomplicated otitis externa; consider only if cellulitis/spread beyond canal or immunocompromised (plus topical).
  • Swab: not routinely needed; consider if failure of initial therapy (e.g. persistent symptoms after ~14 days), suspected fungal infection, chronic/recurrent disease, or if referring.

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency. If worried about necrotising otitis externa, do not delay escalation for swabs or response to topical therapy.

Emergency / same‑day ENT

  • Refer/phone same‑day if any red flag features of necrotising otitis externa:
    • Pain out of proportion / unremitting nocturnal pain
    • Facial palsy or other cranial nerve impairment
    • Granulation tissue/exposed bone in canal (bone–cartilage junction)
    • Systemically unwell / high fever
  • Also consider same‑day ENT if severe pain or canal swollen shut/occluded (may need microsuction + wick to allow drops).
  • Route (draft): D&G — confirm local same‑day ENT route (ED vs SDEC vs ENT on‑call advice).

Routine ENT

  • No improvement after 3 weeks despite appropriate topical management and good self‑care.
  • Chronic/recurrent otitis externa requiring repeated topical courses over >2–3 months.
  • Concern about fungal infection needing microsuction, or canal occlusion preventing treatment (if not severe enough for same‑day).
  • Concern about canal malignancy (unilateral, not improving with appropriate treatment).
  • Route (draft): SCI Gateway → ENT (routine).

Who not to refer

  • Uncomplicated otitis externa improving with topical therapy and self‑care.
  • Mild itch‑predominant symptoms responding to self‑care/acidifying spray (if appropriate) and no red flags.
