Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Don’t treat “weight loss / night sweats” as a single pathway. Use the associated symptom/exam finding to choose the correct site-specific cancer investigation/referral (NG12 symptom chapter is the fastest way to do this).
  • Night sweats + weight loss + lymphadenopathy/splenomegaly are classic “B symptoms” for haematological malignancy; NG12 explicitly says to take fever/night sweats/weight loss into account when deciding suspected cancer referral for non‑Hodgkin’s lymphoma (and very urgent bloods for leukaemia patterns).
  • If symptoms are non‑specific and don’t fit a single USC pathway: Scotland uses Rapid Cancer Diagnostic Services (RCDS) in some areas (or GP direct access CT where not available). Threshold commonly used: new unexplained unintentional weight loss ≥5% in 3 months (or strong clinical suspicion).

1) NICE NG12 (symptom-led investigation/referral)

  • NG12 provides recommendations organised by symptom/findings and is designed for “starting from the symptom” when cancer site is unclear.
  • Examples relevant to weight loss (from the symptom chapter):
    • Upper abdominal pain + weight loss (≥55) → suspected cancer pathway referral for oesophageal/stomach.
    • Abdominal pain + weight loss (≥60) → consider urgent direct access CT (or urgent USS if CT not available) for pancreatic.
    • Abdominal pain with unexplained weight loss (≥40) → offer FIT for possible colorectal.
  • NG12 also embeds “when still uncertain, consider specialist advice” and safety-netting recommendations.

2) NICE NG12 (haematology signals inside symptom chapter)

  • Unexplained splenomegaly → consider suspected cancer pathway referral for non‑Hodgkin’s lymphoma; explicitly consider associated symptoms including fever, night sweats, shortness of breath, pruritus, weight loss.
  • Hepatosplenomegaly → consider a very urgent FBC (within 48 hours) for possible leukaemia (and escalate per results/clinical state).

3) Non-specific symptom pathways (Cancer Research UK summary of NHS Scotland RCDS criteria)

  • CRUK summarises the referral criteria used for rapid diagnostic pathways in Scotland: new unexplained unintentional weight loss (≥5% in 3 months or strong clinical suspicion); and other new unexplained constitutional symptoms for ≥4 weeks (or less if significant concern).
  • Where RCDS are not available, GP direct access CT is used in some areas (local arrangement).
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • Confirm D&G route for non-specific symptoms / RCDS (if available) vs direct access CT vs “most likely site” USC pathway.
  • Confirm the baseline “filter function” test set expected before NSS/RCDS referral (many pathways specify a standard bundle).
  • Confirm whether night sweats/weight loss with normal exam should go to medical, haematology, or NSS/RCDS in D&G, and what urgency.

Unexplained weight loss / night sweats

Background

  • Unintentional weight loss and/or night sweats are non‑specific symptoms with a wide differential (malignancy, infection, endocrine/metabolic, inflammatory, medication/substance, mental health).
  • Key principle: use the associated symptoms/exam findings to choose the correct site‑specific investigation/referral pathway (rather than a single generic rule).
  • B symptoms (night sweats, fever, weight loss) with lymphadenopathy/splenomegaly raise concern for haematological malignancy.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Confirm the symptom
    • Is weight loss unintentional? quantify (kg and % over time; weigh now and compare with prior weights if available).
    • Night sweats: severity (soaking clothes/bedding), frequency, duration; associated fever/rigors; exposures (TB), travel, HIV risk.
  • Red flags / same‑day: haemodynamic instability, severe dehydration, sepsis features, new confusion, severe hypercalcaemia symptoms, suspected DKA, significant haemoptysis, GI bleed, rapidly progressive symptoms, or strong suspicion of malignancy with acute compromise.
  • Focused history/exam to “pick a site” (examples):
    • Upper GI: dysphagia, persistent vomiting, upper abdominal pain.
    • Lower GI: change in bowel habit, PR bleeding, abdominal pain.
    • Resp: cough, breathlessness, chest pain, haemoptysis.
    • GU: haematuria, LUTS.
    • Haematology: lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, pruritus, recurrent infections, bruising.
  • Baseline tests (typical “filter function” bundle — local may vary):
    • FBC + film (if abnormal), CRP/ESR.
    • U&E/eGFR, LFTs, calcium, glucose/HbA1c.
    • TSH.
    • Ferritin/iron studies if anaemia suspected.
    • Urinalysis.
    • Chest X‑ray if respiratory symptoms or clinical concern (and per NG12 criteria).
    • Consider FIT if lower‑GI features/abdominal pain with weight loss (per NG12).

Primary care management

  • If no emergency features: complete baseline assessment/tests promptly, and arrange short‑interval review (e.g. 2–4 weeks) to reassess symptoms, weight trend and results.
  • Address reversible contributors where clear (e.g. medication side effects, uncontrolled diabetes, hyperthyroidism symptoms) but do not delay investigation if clinical concern.
  • Safety‑net: clear return instructions if rapid deterioration, new focal symptoms (e.g. dysphagia, haemoptysis, PR bleeding), persistent fever, or ongoing weight loss.

Who to refer

Pick the most likely pathway. Use NG12 symptom‑based recommendations to choose site‑specific investigation/referral where possible.

Suspected cancer pathway (USC / 2WW)

  • Refer via the appropriate site‑specific USC pathway when NG12 criteria are met (examples include dysphagia; upper abdominal pain with weight loss in older adults; persistent haemoptysis; persistent unexplained lymphadenopathy with B symptoms).

Non‑specific symptoms pathway / RCDS (if available)

  • Consider NSS/RCDS if symptoms are concerning but do not fit a single site‑specific USC pathway.
  • Typical threshold used in Scotland: new unexplained unintentional weight loss ≥5% in 3 months (or strong clinical suspicion), and/or other new unexplained constitutional symptoms.
  • Route (draft): SCI Gateway → [local NSS/RCDS service name] OR GP direct access CT (where RCDS not available).

Haematology / medical urgent assessment

  • If lymphadenopathy/splenomegaly with night sweats, fever, weight loss or concerning blood results (anaemia, thrombocytopenia, very abnormal WCC) → urgent discussion/referral per local pathway.

Who not to refer

  • If weight is stable and an alternative benign explanation is clear and symptoms resolve, manage in primary care with routine follow‑up.
  • Do not use NSS/RCDS as a default if a site‑specific USC pathway clearly applies (use the site pathway).
