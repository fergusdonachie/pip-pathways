DGRefHelp drafts / Iron deficiency anaemia

Adults with iron deficiency anaemia (IDA): confirm iron deficiency, start iron replacement, screen for coeliac disease, use NICE NG12 FIT triage for suspected colorectal cancer, and refer/arrange GI investigation when indicated (BSG 2021 + NG12 2023).
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑11

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • Confirm iron deficiency before extensive investigation (unless unstable): ferritin is the single most useful test; consider transferrin saturation if ferritin may be falsely normal (inflammation) (BSG 2021).
  • Colorectal cancer triage now uses FIT (NG12): offer quantitative FIT to guide referral in adults with iron deficiency anaemia (NG12, 2023). Refer on suspected cancer pathway if FIT ≥10 µg Hb/g faeces (NG12, 2023). If FIT not returned/negative, safety‑net and do not delay referral if strong clinical concern (NG12).
  • Men + postmenopausal women with newly diagnosed unexplained IDA often warrant upper + lower GI investigation (gastroscopy + colonoscopy; CT colonography if colonoscopy unsuitable) (BSG 2021). (Note: this is broader than NG12 FIT triage, and local pathways may combine both.)
  • Initial investigations recommended by BSG for confirmed IDA include urinalysis/urine microscopy and coeliac disease screening, plus GI endoscopy where appropriate (BSG 2021).
  • Start iron replacement promptly: BSG advises iron therapy should not be deferred while awaiting investigations unless colonoscopy is imminent. Typical first‑line oral regimens: ferrous sulphate/fumarate/gluconate; once daily; if not tolerated consider every‑other‑day dosing or alternative/IV iron (BSG 2021).

Iron deficiency anaemia (IDA) — adults: workup + referral triggers

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Suspected major haemorrhage (haematemesis/melaena/large PR bleed) or haemodynamic compromise.
  • Symptomatic severe anaemia with concerning features (e.g. syncope, chest pain, acute heart failure, severe breathlessness) → urgent assessment / ED.
  • New neurological/cardiac ischaemia symptoms possibly precipitated by anaemia.

Step 1 — confirm iron deficiency (don’t skip this)

  • FBC + indices (MCV/MCH), film if atypical.
  • Ferritin (most useful single marker). If inflammation suspected, add transferrin saturation / iron studies (BSG).
  • Baseline: U&Es, LFTs, CRP/ESR as clinically indicated; check B12/folate if mixed picture.

Step 2 — look for obvious causes (history + quick checks)

  • Bleeding: GI symptoms, PR bleeding/melaena, haematemesis; menstrual losses; haematuria.
  • Dietary factors; recent pregnancy/postpartum; blood donation.
  • Malabsorption risks: coeliac symptoms/family history; bariatric surgery; chronic diarrhoea.
  • Medications: NSAIDs/aspirin/anticoagulants (bleeding risk); PPIs (possible reduced absorption).

Step 3 — start iron replacement (don’t wait unless colonoscopy imminent)

  • Start oral iron (e.g. ferrous sulphate/fumarate/gluconate) — one tablet daily (BSG). If not tolerated: every other day or alternative oral preparation; consider IV iron if oral is contraindicated/ineffective/not tolerated (BSG).
  • Monitor response: check Hb response in the first few weeks; a brisk rise supports iron deficiency (BSG).
  • Continue treatment for ~3 months after Hb normalises to replete stores (BSG).

Step 4 — minimum initial investigations (before/alongside referral)

  • Coeliac disease screen (serology) for confirmed IDA (BSG).
  • Urinalysis / urine microscopy (BSG).
  • Consider: pregnancy test where relevant; stool testing only if diarrhoea/infective features (not routine for IDA).

Step 5 — colorectal cancer triage (NICE NG12 2023 FIT framing)

  • Offer quantitative FIT to guide referral for suspected colorectal cancer in adults with iron deficiency anaemia (NG12).
  • If FIT ≥10 µg Hb/g faeces → refer using suspected cancer pathway (NG12).
  • If FIT is not returned or <10:
    • Ensure safety‑netting (NG12).
    • Do not delay referral if strong clinical concern (e.g. abdominal mass, ongoing unexplained symptoms) (NG12).
  • Related pages: Change in bowel habit / suspected CRC; PR bleeding.

Who to refer / where to send (D&G placeholders)

  • 999 / ED: unstable/major bleeding; severe symptomatic anaemia with cardiorespiratory compromise.
  • Suspected cancer pathway (CRC): FIT ≥10 (NG12).
  • Urgent gastro / direct-access endoscopy (local route):
    • Men and postmenopausal women with new unexplained IDA often warrant bidirectional endoscopy (BSG) (local pathway may be FIT-led or direct-to-endoscopy).
    • Ongoing IDA despite treatment; recurrent IDA; or additional alarm features (weight loss, change in bowel habit, abdominal mass).
  • Gynae / women’s health: heavy menstrual bleeding or other gynaecological bleeding as the likely source (plus treat IDA in parallel).
  • Haematology: atypical blood results (pancytopenia, haemolysis), unexplained microcytosis without iron deficiency, suspected haematological malignancy, or anaemia not responding as expected.

Referral-letter minimum (suggested)

  • Hb + indices, ferritin ± transferrin saturation, platelets; trend if available.
  • Coeliac serology result; urinalysis result.
  • FIT result (value + date), and whether sample was returned (NG12).
  • Symptoms: GI (change in bowel habit, abdominal pain, weight loss, PR bleeding/melaena), gynae bleeding; any haematuria.
  • Iron therapy started (drug/dose/date) and response if rechecked.

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • Local policy for FIT in IDA: who orders, expected turnaround, and whether to start iron before FIT sample (pragmatically: start iron; but clarify if local instructions differ).
  • What to do with FIT <10 but confirmed IDA: urgent gastro clinic vs direct-to-endoscopy vs repeat FIT vs watchful waiting (and safety‑net timeline).
  • Default route for men/postmenopausal women with new unexplained IDA (FIT-first vs direct bidirectional endoscopy) and how this maps to “suspected cancer pathway”.
  • Local access to IV iron and transfusion thresholds/route for symptomatic anaemia (ED vs SDEC vs haematology).
  • Local preference for coeliac screening and any required additional tests before GI referral (e.g. repeat ferritin, CRP).
