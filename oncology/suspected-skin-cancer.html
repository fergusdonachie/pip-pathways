Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Don’t excise suspected melanoma in primary care (urgent suspected cancer referral instead).
  • USC / 2WW triggers are primarily clinical pattern recognition: change in mole (colour/size/shape), ABCDE features, new growing nodule (± pigment), persistent ulceration/bleeding/oozing (>4 weeks), keratinising/indurated lesions, suspicious nail pigmentation, immunosuppressed with an unexplained lesion.
  • Referral quality matters for triage: include measurements, symptoms (pain/tenderness/bleeding), and send a photograph where possible (local pathway‑dependent).

1) Scottish Referral Guidelines for Suspected Cancer (Skin cancers)

  • Provides practical urgent‑referral features for melanoma / SCC / high‑risk BCC, including nail lesions and immunosuppressed people.
  • Good‑practice points: do not remove lesions suspicious for melanoma in primary care; send excised specimens for histology; provide accurate description and photos (subject to governance) to support triage.

2) NICE NG12 (suspected cancer: recognition and referral)

  • NICE NG12 is the overarching UK referral framework for suspected cancer. For D&G, the actionable part is: use the suspected cancer pathway referral for lesions that are suspicious for melanoma/skin cancer, and safety‑net/review when thresholds are not met but concern persists.
  • Local pathways may split “who receives” (Dermatology vs Plastics) and whether images/dermoscopy are required for triage.
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • Confirm the exact SCI Gateway destination/service naming for USC/2WW suspected skin cancer (Dermatology vs Plastics; and whether a single “skin cancer” service exists).
  • Confirm expected photo upload method (e.g., SCI Store, clinical portal attachment) and any minimum image requirements.
  • Confirm whether D&G expects dermoscopy in primary care/triage (and what to do if not available).
  • Confirm whether any GPwSI / community dermatology pathway exists for low‑risk BCC/SCC (and what is safe to manage without USC referral).

Suspected skin cancer (melanoma / SCC / BCC)

Background

  • Aim: identify lesions needing urgent suspected cancer referral (melanoma/SCC/high‑risk BCC) and avoid inappropriate primary care excision of suspected melanoma.
  • Key tools: good history (change over time), focused examination, measurements, and good‑quality photographs for triage (follow local pathway).
  • Red flags can be pigmented (melanoma) or non‑pigmented (nodular melanoma, SCC).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: duration; change in size/shape/colour; bleeding/oozing; ulceration; pain/tenderness; itching/altered sensation; previous skin cancer; immunosuppression; sun exposure/solarium; family history melanoma.
  • Examination:
    • Measure lesion (mm), note site and surrounding inflammation.
    • Pigmented lesions: look for ABCDE features (asymmetry, border irregularity, colour irregularity, diameter increasing or >6 mm, evolving/change).
    • Nodular melanoma: new growing nodule with or without pigment.
    • SCC features: slow‑growing non‑healing or keratinising lesion with induration (thickened base) ± ulceration/bleeding.
    • Nail: new/changing pigmented line or unexplained lesion in a nail (consider subungual melanoma; haematoma tends to grow out distally).
  • Photography: include a close‑up and a contextual photo; add a ruler/scale if possible (follow local governance/pathway).
  • Dermoscopy: if trained/equipped, document findings; if not available, refer based on clinical features and do not delay.

Primary care management

  • Do NOT excise suspected melanoma in primary care (urgent suspected cancer referral instead).
  • If you excise any lesion (e.g., under an agreed local low‑risk pathway): always send the specimen for pathological examination.
  • Safety‑net: advise the patient to re‑present if the lesion changes, bleeds, ulcerates, or fails to heal; document this.

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency. Include measurements + evolution/change. Add photos where possible (local pathway dependent).

Suspected cancer pathway (USC / 2WW) — suspected skin cancer

  • Refer USC for lesions on any part of the body with one or more of:
    • Change in colour, size or shape in an existing mole.
    • ABCDE features: asymmetry; border irregularity; colour irregularity; diameter increasing or >6 mm; evolving/change.
    • New growing nodule with or without pigment.
    • Persistent (>4 weeks) ulceration, bleeding or oozing.
    • Persistent (>4 weeks) surrounding inflammation or altered sensation.
    • New or changing pigmented line in a nail, or an unexplained lesion in a nail.
    • Slow growing, non‑healing or keratinising lesion with induration (thickened base).
    • Any unexplained skin lesion in an immunosuppressed patient.
    • Biopsy‑proven melanoma, invasive SCC, or high‑risk BCC; or BCC in potentially dangerous areas (peri‑ocular, auditory meatus, major vessel/nerve).
  • Referral essentials (to support triage): site + size (mm) + duration + evolution/change; symptoms (pain/tenderness/bleeding/oozing/ulceration/altered sensation); immunosuppression/prior skin cancer; photos if possible.
  • Route (draft): SCI Gateway → DGRI → Dermatology/Plastics (USC suspected skin cancer).

Who not to refer

  • Clearly benign lesions with classic appearances (e.g., typical seborrhoeic keratosis) and no red‑flag change pattern.
  • Stable lesions without suspicious features where there is low clinical concern (safety‑net and review if change/persistence).
