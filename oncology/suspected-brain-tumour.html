NICE NG12: Suspected cancer (2015, updated 2026)

  • Adults: Consider an urgent, direct access MRI scan of the brain (or CT scan if MRI is contraindicated; to be done within 2 weeks) to assess for brain or central nervous system cancer in adults with progressive, sub-acute loss of central neurological function.
  • Children and young people: Consider a very urgent referral (for an appointment within 48 hours) for suspected brain or central nervous system cancer in children and young people with newly abnormal cerebellar or other central neurological function.

Clinical context (Red Flags)

While NG12 focuses on "progressive loss of function", other guidelines (e.g., NICE CKS, SIGN) highlight red flags that may require urgent admission or referral:

  • Headache with red flags: New onset >50, thunderclap, waking from sleep, precipitated by Valsalva, associated with vomiting/visual change/focal deficit.
  • Papilloedema (requires urgent same-day ophthalmology or medical assessment).
  • New onset seizure (adult).
  • Personality/behaviour change (confirmed by witness).

Background

  • Brain tumours are rare but serious; early diagnosis improves outcomes.
  • Symptoms depend on location and growth rate.
  • Most headaches are benign (primary headache disorders), but specific "red flags" warrant investigation.
  • Direct access MRI (or CT) is the primary investigation for adults with progressive sub-acute neurological deficits.

Assessment

History

  • Neurological deficit: Progressive weakness, sensory loss, coordination/balance issues, speech difficulty, visual field loss.
  • Headache: New onset (esp. >50), waking from sleep, worse in morning/lying down, precipitated by Valsalva, associated with vomiting.
  • Seizures: New onset focal or generalised seizure in an adult.
  • Personality/Cognitive: New confusion, memory loss, or personality change (confirmed by witness).

Examination

  • Neurological exam: Cranial nerves, limbs (tone, power, reflexes, sensation), coordination (cerebellar signs), gait.
  • Fundoscopy: Check for papilloedema (blurred disc margins).
  • Visual fields: Confrontation testing.

Primary Care Management

  • Urgent Admission/Same-day Referral:
    • Signs of raised intracranial pressure (e.g., papilloedema, reduced consciousness).
    • Acute severe headache (thunderclap) – suspect SAH.
    • Rapidly progressive neurological deficit.
  • Direct Access Imaging (USC/2WW): Refer for imaging if stable but meets criteria.
  • Do not delay referral for blood tests unless specifically indicated (e.g., infection screen if abscess suspected).

Who to refer

Adults (Direct Access Imaging / USC Referral)

  • Consider urgent direct access MRI brain (or CT if MRI contraindicated) to be done within 2 weeks for adults with progressive, sub-acute loss of central neurological function.

Children and Young People (Urgent Referral)

  • Consider very urgent referral (appointment within 48 hours) for children/young people with newly abnormal cerebellar or other central neurological function.

Who not to refer

  • Long-standing stable headaches without red flags (manage as primary headache disorder).
  • Dizziness/vertigo without focal neurological signs (consider BPPV/vestibular neuritis - see Dizziness page).
