Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Unexplained isolated node ≥2 cm persisting ≥6 weeks (or increasing) → treat as USC for assessment/biopsy via local pathway (Scottish SRG 2025).
  • Generalised lymphadenopathy (or nodes with B symptoms / hepatosplenomegaly) → USC haematology (Scottish SRG 2025; NICE NG12 aligns).
  • Do not miss “looks unwell” patterns: sepsis/airway compromise/rapidly progressive swelling, or blood film reported suspicious for acute leukaemia → same‑day/emergency routes (SRG 2025).

1) Scottish Referral Guidelines for Suspected Cancer (SRG) 2025 — Haematological cancers

  • SRG 2025 is explicit about practical thresholds for adult referral/biopsy and a baseline-test set when lymphoma is a possibility.
  • When to consider urgent clinical assessment + FBC: unexplained pallor, bruising/bleeding/petechiae, fatigue/breathlessness, recurrent infections/fever, drenching night sweats, hepatosplenomegaly, and lymphadenopathy (node ≥2 cm persisting ≥6 weeks or increasing, or generalised).
  • Lymphoma baseline tests to consider (if unexplained features): FBC, renal function, LFTs, calcium, LDH, serum protein electrophoresis, and HIV serology.
  • USC haematology (examples): generalised lymphadenopathy, especially with systemic upset (e.g. drenching night sweats/unintentional weight loss) and/or hepatosplenomegaly/splenomegaly.
  • USC “assessment/biopsy via local pathways”: unexplained isolated lymphadenopathy ≥2 cm persisting ≥6 weeks or increasing.
  • Good practice: safety‑net; repeat baseline tests if persistent concern; consider checking HIV status in non-specific symptom presentations.

2) NICE NG12 (Suspected cancer) — haematological cancers

  • NICE NG12 supports suspected-cancer-pathway referral for adults with unexplained lymphadenopathy for Hodgkin’s and non‑Hodgkin’s lymphoma (and emphasises associated systemic symptoms).
  • NG12 also contains separate referral guidance for neck lumps/head & neck cancers — clinically, cervical nodes can sit in either camp (local route decision needed).
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • For unexplained isolated lymphadenopathy ≥2 cm: confirm D&G route (USC haematology vs “lump clinic”/general surgery vs ENT for cervical nodes) and whether a direct‑to‑USS step exists before biopsy.
  • Confirm whether primary care should order baseline tests routinely (FBC/U&E/LFT/LDH/HIV) before USC referral, or whether secondary care prefers to own this.
  • Confirm whether D&G uses a Rapid Cancer Diagnostic Service (RCDS) for persistent non-specific symptoms + lymphadenopathy patterns.

Lymphadenopathy

Background

  • Lymphadenopathy is common; most is reactive/infective. The key job is to not miss malignancy (especially lymphoma/leukaemia) or serious infection.
  • In adults, unexplained isolated lymphadenopathy ≥2 cm persisting ≥6 weeks (or increasing) should be managed as urgent suspicion of cancer (USC) for assessment/biopsy via local pathway.
  • Generalised lymphadenopathy and/or B symptoms (fever, drenching night sweats, weight loss) increases concern for haematological malignancy.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • First check: is this an emergency / same day?
    • Sepsis features, airway compromise, rapidly progressive swelling, severe systemic unwellness → same-day assessment (local acute route).
    • Blood count/film reported suggestive of acute leukaemia/CML → emergency same-day pathway.
  • History: duration, growth, pain/tenderness; recent infection (URTI/dental/skin); travel/animal exposure; TB risk; sexual history; drugs (e.g. immunosuppression); autoimmune symptoms.
  • Systemic “B symptoms”: fever, drenching night sweats, unintentional weight loss; also pruritus and fatigue.
  • Examination:
    • Node size (measure), site (esp. supraclavicular), texture (hard/fixed vs rubbery/mobile), overlying skin; look for a local source of infection.
    • Check for generalised lymphadenopathy + hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Baseline tests (consider when lymphoma/leukaemia possible): FBC (± film), U&E, LFT, calcium, LDH; consider HIV serology (local consent/process).

Primary care management

  • If clearly reactive (obvious local infection) and improving: treat underlying cause + review.
  • Safety-net explicitly: document size and site; arrange review if not resolving; advise return sooner if increasing size, new nodes, or systemic symptoms.
  • Do not “watch and wait” if node meets USC criteria (below) or if generalised nodes/B symptoms/hepatosplenomegaly.

Who to refer

Emergency / same-day (local acute route)

  • Sepsis features, airway compromise, rapidly progressive swelling, severe systemic unwellness.
  • Blood count/film reported suggestive of acute leukaemia/CML.

USC (suspected cancer pathway)

  • Generalised lymphadenopathy (multiple regions), particularly with systemic upset / B symptoms and/or hepatosplenomegaly/splenomegaly → USC haematology.
  • Unexplained isolated lymphadenopathy ≥2 cm persisting ≥6 weeks or increasing in size → USC for assessment/biopsy via local pathway.

Route placeholders (draft): SCI Gateway → (1) Haematology USC (generalised/B symptoms/hepatosplenomegaly) OR (2) local “lymph node / lump assessment” pathway for isolated nodes needing biopsy.

Who not to refer

  • Small tender nodes with an obvious local infection that are resolving, with no red flags and no systemic features.
  • Isolated lymphocytosis or raised paraprotein without the additional features required for USC referral (follow local non‑USC guidance).
