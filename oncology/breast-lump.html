Evidence summary (triangulated)

  • Incidence & Nature: Breast cancer is the most common cancer in the UK, but most breast lumps (especially in younger women) are benign (fibroadenoma, cysts). Pain alone is rarely a symptom of cancer.
  • Assessment: History should cover duration, size change (menstrual cycle?), pain, nipple/skin changes, and family history. Examination involves inspection (arms raised/by side) and palpation of all quadrants/axilla.
  • Referral Urgency:
    • Immediate: Suspected breast abscess (pointing/fluctuant) or infected cyst/severe mastitis not responding to antibiotics.
    • 2WW (Suspected Cancer):
      • Aged 30+ with unexplained breast lump.
      • Aged 50+ with unilateral nipple discharge/retraction.
      • Skin changes suggestive of cancer (peau d'orange, tethering).
      • Aged 30+ with unexplained axillary lump.
    • Routine: Aged <30 with unexplained lump; severe persistent mastalgia (if failed primary care management); nipple changes not meeting 2WW criteria; new asymmetry without lump.
  • Primary Care Management: Treat lactational mastitis with flucloxacillin (or erythromycin/clarithromycin); non-lactational with co-amoxiclav (or metronidazole + erythromycin). Manage breast pain with reassurance, well-fitting bra, topical NSAIDs, and pain diary.

Background

  • Breast cancer is the most common cancer in the UK.
  • Most breast lumps are benign (fibroadenoma, cyst, etc.), especially in younger women.
  • Pain alone is rarely a symptom of breast cancer.

Assessment

  • History:
    • Duration of lump.
    • Change in size (related to menstrual cycle?).
    • Associated pain.
    • Nipple changes (discharge, inversion, rash).
    • Skin changes (tethering, peau d'orange, erythema).
    • Systemic symptoms (weight loss, bone pain).
    • Family history (breast/ovarian cancer).
  • Examination:
    • Inspection (arms by side and raised): look for asymmetry, skin dimpling, nipple changes.
    • Palpation (all quadrants, tail of Spence, axilla, supraclavicular fossa).
    • Describe lump: size, consistency (hard/soft/cystic), mobility, fixation, irregular margins.

Primary Care Management

  • Infection (Mastitis/Abscess):
    • If lactational: Flucloxacillin 500mg QDS (or Erythromycin/Clarithromycin if penicillin allergic). Encourage continued feeding/expressing.
    • If non-lactational: Co-amoxiclav 625mg TDS (cover anaerobes) or Metronidazole + Erythromycin.
    • Refer urgently if suspected abscess (fluctuant, pointing) or no response to antibiotics.
  • Breast Pain (Mastalgia) without lump:
    • Reassurance (cancer risk low).
    • Well-fitting bra (measurements).
    • Topical NSAIDs (e.g. ibuprofen gel).
    • Consider evening primrose oil (evidence limited).
    • Keeping a pain diary.

Who to refer

Immediate / Same Day

  • Suspected Breast Abscess: If pointing/fluctuant or septic (refer to General Surgery/Breast Unit depending on local pathway).
  • Infected cyst or severe mastitis not responding to treatment.

2WW (Suspected Cancer)

  • Aged 30 and over with an unexplained breast lump (with or without pain).
  • Aged 50 and over with any of the following in one nipple only:
    • Discharge.
    • Retraction.
    • Other changes of concern.
  • Skin changes suggesting breast cancer (e.g. peau d'orange, tethering).
  • Aged 30 and over with an unexplained lump in the axilla.

Routine Referral

  • Aged under 30 with an unexplained breast lump (with or without pain).
  • Severe/Persistent Mastalgia failing primary care management (usually to Breast Clinic, not 2WW).
  • Nipple changes not meeting 2WW criteria (e.g. bilateral, <50 years) but persistent.
  • Asymmetry (new or changing) without lump.
  • Family History: Refer to Family History Clinic / Genetics (check local criteria, usually via specific form).

Who not to refer

  • Cyclical breast pain alone with normal examination (manage in primary care).
  • Bilateral nipple discharge (usually benign/physiological; check prolactin/medications).
  • Transient lumpiness that resolves after menstruation (review in 4-6 weeks if unsure).
