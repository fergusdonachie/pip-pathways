Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Unexplained lymphadenopathy (especially persistent / enlarging, ≥2 cm, supraclavicular, or with B symptoms) → treat as possible lymphoma and refer via the suspected-cancer pathway (or local USC).
  • Unexplained neck lump in adults (or persistent neck lump) → consider head & neck cancer pathway; referral quality depends on describing lump features (site/size/consistency/mobility/skin changes).
  • Unexplained lump increasing in size (non-lymph node, any site) → rule-in concern for soft tissue sarcoma: urgent ultrasound (within 2 weeks) and refer if ultrasound suspicious/uncertain and concern persists.

1) NICE NG12 — the minimum actionable rules

  • Lymphoma (adults): consider a suspected cancer pathway referral for adults with unexplained lymphadenopathy (Hodgkin’s/Non‑Hodgkin’s) and factor in systemic symptoms (fever, night sweats, pruritus, weight loss; and alcohol-induced lymph node pain for Hodgkin’s).
  • Head & neck cancer: suspected cancer pathway referral is supported for persistent and unexplained neck lump (and for unexplained neck lump aged ≥45 in the laryngeal cancer section).
  • Soft tissue sarcoma: adults with an unexplained lump increasing in size → urgent direct-access ultrasound (within 2 weeks). If ultrasound suggests sarcoma (or is uncertain and concern persists) → suspected cancer pathway referral.

2) Scottish Referral Guidelines for Suspected Cancer (Right Decisions) — practical thresholds

  • Haematological cancers (adult USC criteria): consider USC for unexplained isolated lymphadenopathy if ≥2 cm and persisting for ≥6 weeks or increasing in size; and for generalised lymphadenopathy, especially with systemic upset (e.g. drenching night sweats / unintentional weight loss) or hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Head & neck SRG (context): small, mobile, rubbery lumps are more likely reactive nodes; if no concerning features, they can be followed clinically or assessed by neck ultrasound (where available). Referral quality depends on describing size/site/consistency/mobility and overlying skin changes.
What is “local-process sensitive” (D&G)
  • Which service owns “unexplained lymphadenopathy” referrals (haematology vs ENT vs general surgery) and whether there is a non-specific-symptoms / DAP route.
  • Whether primary care has direct-access ultrasound for neck lumps/soft tissue lumps (and expected timescale/reporting).
  • Whether FBC/film is required (or simply helpful) before USC referral in suspected lymphoma/haematological cancer.
This evidence summary is written for pathway design: it highlights only decision-changing points and where local policy choices sit.

New lump / lymph node

Background

  • A “new lump” could be a reactive lymph node, benign soft-tissue lesion (lipoma/cyst), or malignancy. The job is to identify who needs urgent assessment and to route to the right service.
  • Three high-yield patterns: (1) unexplained/persistent lymphadenopathy (possible lymphoma), (2) persistent unexplained neck lump (head & neck pathway), (3) unexplained lump increasing in size (possible soft-tissue sarcoma).
  • If the patient is systemically unwell or has airway/neurological compromise, use same-day/emergency routes.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: duration, change over time, infection symptoms, dental/ENT symptoms, skin lesions, travel/TB risk, animal exposure, immunosuppression, and B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss) and pruritus.
  • Examination: describe the lump (site, size, shape, consistency, mobility, tenderness, overlying skin changes) and examine for other lymphadenopathy + hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Red flags / urgent concern:
    • Neck: persistent unexplained neck lump; hoarseness/dysphagia/odynophagia; referred otalgia; oral ulcer >3 weeks; cranial nerve signs.
    • Nodes: supraclavicular node; ≥2 cm node persisting ≥6 weeks or increasing in size; generalised lymphadenopathy; B symptoms; splenomegaly.
    • Soft-tissue mass: unexplained lump increasing in size (think sarcoma) — needs urgent imaging.
  • Suggested baseline tests (do not delay referral when highly suspicious): FBC + film if concern for haematological malignancy; CRP/ESR if inflammatory picture; HIV testing where appropriate; consider LDH and calcium/renal/liver profile if lymphoma/myeloma suspicion (local practice varies).

Primary care management

  • Likely reactive node / benign lump: if clinically consistent (e.g. recent infection, small mobile rubbery node, clear benign lesion such as sebaceous cyst/lipoma) and no red flags → safety-net and review.
  • Safety-net: give clear advice to return if the lump persists beyond the agreed review window, enlarges, becomes hard/fixed, becomes supraclavicular, or systemic symptoms develop.
  • Do not treat “lymphadenopathy” with repeated empirical antibiotics in the absence of a clear bacterial focus — reassess diagnosis and consider referral/imaging.

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency. Do not delay referral if there is high clinical suspicion.

Suspected cancer / USC referral

  • Possible lymphoma / haematological cancer: unexplained lymphadenopathy (especially ≥2 cm and persisting ≥6 weeks or increasing), generalised lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, or lymphadenopathy with B symptoms/pruritus.
  • Possible head & neck cancer: persistent unexplained neck lump (with or without smoking/alcohol history).
  • Possible soft tissue sarcoma: unexplained lump that is increasing in size → urgent ultrasound within 2 weeks; refer if ultrasound suspicious/uncertain and clinical concern persists.

Same-day / emergency

  • Airway compromise/stridor, sepsis, rapidly progressive deep neck infection features, or other acute instability.

Who not to refer

  • Clear benign skin lesions (e.g. uncomplicated sebaceous cyst, typical lipoma) where local minor surgery/community pathways exist — manage or refer non-urgently as appropriate.
  • Small, mobile, tender nodes in a clear acute infection context that resolve as expected (with safety-netting).
