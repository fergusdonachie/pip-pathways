Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Confirm it’s real + persistent: recheck platelets (and review previous FBCs). Most thrombocytosis is reactive and may settle over 4–8 weeks.
  • Don’t miss cancer: thrombocytosis is a risk marker for malignancy (esp “LEGO‑C”: lung, endometrial, gastric, oesophageal, colorectal). If unexplained, assess for tumour-specific symptoms and use the matching USC pathway.
  • If no tumour-specific symptoms: both NICE NG12 and Scottish Referral Guidelines highlight urgent chest X‑ray as the default investigation for people ≥40 with thrombocytosis (and consider broader imaging/RCDS if systemic symptoms or gut feeling).

1) Scottish Referral Guidelines for Suspected Cancer (SRG) 2025 — thrombocytosis box

  • SRG emphasises thrombocytosis is usually reactive but can reflect malignancy; highlights LEGO‑C cancers (lung, endometrial, gastric, oesophageal, colorectal).
  • If unexplained thrombocytosis is identified: assess for cancer symptoms/signs and refer to tumour-specific USC if appropriate.
  • If unexplained thrombocytosis with no tumour-specific symptoms: SRG recommends arranging a chest X‑ray.
  • If unexplained thrombocytosis plus non-specific symptoms (e.g. significant weight loss) or GP “gut feeling”: consider referral for further investigation via rapid cancer diagnostic service (where available) or GP direct access imaging pathways.

2) NICE NG12 — where thrombocytosis changes action

Lung cancer (NG12):
  • Consider an urgent chest X‑ray (within 2 weeks) in people aged ≥40 with thrombocytosis.
Upper GI cancer (NG12):
  • Consider non‑urgent direct-access upper GI endoscopy in people aged ≥55 with raised platelets and upper GI symptoms such as nausea/vomiting/weight loss/reflux/dyspepsia/upper abdominal pain.
Endometrial cancer (NG12):
  • Consider direct-access ultrasound for women aged ≥55 with unexplained vaginal discharge who have thrombocytosis (among other triggers).

3) Haematology referral thresholds (example: NHS Lothian RefHelp)

  • RefHelp (not D&G) suggests haematology referral for: persistent unexplained platelets >450×109/L for ≥3 months, or acute arterial/venous occlusive event with platelets >450×109/L.
  • Lists a pragmatic primary care workup: serial FBCs, blood film, inflammatory markers (CRP/ESR), ferritin, U&Es, LFTs, LDH; and reminds reactive thrombocytosis can take 4–8 weeks to resolve.
  • Implication for D&G DGRefHelp: include a simple, “do-this-first” baseline workup + clear triggers for (a) USC/tumour-specific referral and (b) haematology referral for persistent unexplained thrombocytosis.

Raised platelets / thrombocytosis

Background

  • Thrombocytosis = raised platelet count. Most cases in primary care are reactive (infection/inflammation, iron deficiency/bleeding, recent surgery, malignancy, hyposplenism).
  • Important “can’t miss” possibilities: (1) cancer and (2) myeloproliferative neoplasm (MPN) (e.g. essential thrombocythaemia).
  • Unexplained thrombocytosis is a risk marker for malignancy (esp “LEGO‑C”: lung, endometrial, gastric, oesophageal, colorectal) — assess for tumour-specific symptoms and route appropriately.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Confirm it’s real + persistent: check previous FBCs; repeat FBC if new finding.
  • Look for reactive causes: recent infection/inflammation, recent surgery/trauma, bleeding, iron deficiency, chronic inflammatory disease, hyposplenism, malignancy.
  • Screen for cancer symptoms (LEGO‑C): cough/SOB/chest pain/weight loss; dyspepsia/upper abdominal pain/reflux/vomiting; change in bowel habit/PR bleeding/abdominal pain; postmenopausal bleeding/vaginal discharge; systemic symptoms (fatigue, anorexia, night sweats).
  • Examination: chest; abdominal mass/hepatosplenomegaly; lymph nodes; signs of chronic inflammation.
  • Baseline tests (suggested set):
    • Repeat FBC + blood film.
    • Ferritin (± iron studies if needed).
    • CRP (± ESR).
    • U&Es, LFTs (ALP may increase cancer risk when elevated alongside thrombocytosis).

Primary care management

  • Treat likely reactive causes (e.g. iron deficiency, infection/inflammation) and recheck platelets: reactive thrombocytosis may take 4–8 weeks to settle.
  • If unexplained thrombocytosis:
    • Assess for tumour-specific symptoms and use the matching USC pathway.
    • If no tumour-specific symptoms: arrange an urgent chest X‑ray (within 2 weeks) (NG12/SRG default).
    • If non-specific systemic symptoms (e.g. significant weight loss) or “gut feeling”: consider rapid cancer diagnostic / direct access imaging pathway (where available).
  • Safety‑net: document plan for repeat counts and review; advise to re-present if new cancer symptoms develop or symptoms persist/worsen.

Who to refer

Use tumour-specific USC routes where possible, and include relevant results (serial platelet counts + ferritin/CRP/LFTs + CXR if done).

Tumour-specific suspected cancer pathway (USC)

  • Refer USC if thrombocytosis is accompanied by symptoms/signs consistent with a specific cancer site (use NG12/SRG criteria).
  • NG12 examples where thrombocytosis changes action:
    • Age ≥40 with thrombocytosis → consider urgent chest X‑ray within 2 weeks (lung cancer assessment).
    • Age ≥55 with raised platelets + upper GI symptoms → consider direct-access upper GI endoscopy.
    • Women ≥55 with unexplained vaginal discharge + thrombocytosis → consider direct-access pelvic ultrasound.

Haematology

  • Refer haematology for persistent unexplained thrombocytosis (e.g. >450×109/L for ≥3 months) after reactive causes addressed/excluded.
  • Refer urgently / same-day advice if very high platelets (local threshold) or clinical concern for thrombosis/bleeding, or features suggesting an MPN (splenomegaly, erythrocytosis/leucocytosis, constitutional symptoms).
  • Route (draft placeholder): SCI Gateway → DGRI → Haematology (Advice/Outpatients).

Who not to refer

  • Single raised platelet count that normalises on repeat testing.
  • Clear reactive thrombocytosis with an identified cause (e.g. infection/inflammation, iron deficiency) that is being treated — recheck to confirm down-trend.
