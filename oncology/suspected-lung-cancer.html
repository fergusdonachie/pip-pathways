Primary care triage: when to request an urgent CXR (within 2 weeks), what CXR findings should trigger a suspected cancer pathway referral, and what to do when symptoms persist despite a normal CXR (local-process sensitive).
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • NICE NG12 separates lung cancer triage into: urgent CXR (within 2 weeks) for defined symptom combinations/signs, and suspected cancer pathway referral for people with imaging findings suggestive of malignancy (and other specified situations).
  • In primary care, the highest-yield action is to request the right test early (CXR), document smoking/asbestos exposure, and make sure there is a results-follow-up plan (safety-netting).
  • Scottish Right Decisions (NHS Highland) model provides a pragmatic operational layer: baseline blood tests (FBC/U&E/LFT/calcium) and an expectation that referrals may be returned if required results are missing (timed pathway). It also highlights escalation when symptoms persist despite a normal CXR (local variation likely).
Local-process sensitivity
  • Exact routing differs for D&G: urgent CXR access route, who can order CT chest directly, and whether a suspected lung cancer pathway referral can be made with normal CXR but persistent concerning symptoms.
  • This page keeps the NICE triggers explicit, and puts routing uncertainties into a Local agreement needed checklist.

Comparator sources used for the draft

  • NICE NG12: lung cancer (urgent CXR criteria) + mesothelioma criteria (asbestos/smoking modifies threshold).
  • Right Decisions (NHS Highland): urgent suspected lung cancer quick reference (CXR triggers; suggested baseline bloods; referral acceptance/process notes; persistent symptoms with normal CXR).

Suspected lung cancer — primary care triage (NG12 framing)

Scope

  • This is a triage / referral trigger page (not a full diagnostic guideline).
  • Adults in primary care. Use a lower threshold for urgent assessment if immunocompromised or systemically unwell.

Immediate red flags (send same day / ED)

  • Severe breathlessness/hypoxia, haemodynamic instability, sepsis, or large-volume/ongoing haemoptysis.
  • Concern for PE or ACS with instability.

Step 1 — identify people who need an urgent CXR (within 2 weeks)

  • NICE NG12: offer urgent CXR (within 2 weeks) to assess for lung cancer in people aged ≥40 if they have:
    • 2 or more of: unexplained cough, fatigue, shortness of breath, chest pain, weight loss, appetite loss, OR
    • have ever smoked and have 1 or more of the above unexplained symptoms.
  • NICE NG12: consider urgent CXR (within 2 weeks) in people aged ≥40 with any of:
    • persistent or recurrent chest infection
    • finger clubbing
    • supraclavicular lymphadenopathy or persistent cervical lymphadenopathy
    • chest signs consistent with lung cancer
    • thrombocytosis
  • Also see related pages:
    • Haemoptysis
    • Chronic cough
    • Pleural effusion (effusion is a key imaging trigger)

Step 2 — what to do with the CXR result

  • CXR suspicious for lung/pleural malignancy (e.g. mass, pleural mass, pleural effusion, slowly resolving consolidation) → suspected lung cancer pathway referral (urgent) per local process.
  • CXR shows consolidation and infection is treated:
    • ensure a repeat CXR within ~6 weeks if symptoms/signs persist or higher malignancy risk (local practice; see local agreement).
  • Normal CXR but ongoing concern:
    • do not dismiss persistent red-flag symptoms; consider escalation (CT chest / respiratory review / suspected cancer route) per local process.
    • Right Decisions (NHS Highland) model uses a trigger of >6 weeks persistent symptoms (including persistent haemoptysis in smokers/ex-smokers >40) despite normal CXR, but local pathways vary.

What to include in requests/referrals (minimum useful data)

  • Symptoms (onset/duration) + red flags (weight loss, haemoptysis, chest pain, breathlessness).
  • Smoking history (current/ex/never; pack-years if available) and asbestos exposure.
  • Observations if symptomatic: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air.
  • Examination findings (focal chest signs; lymphadenopathy; clubbing).
  • Relevant comorbidities (COPD, ILD, immunosuppression) and anticoagulant/antiplatelet use.
  • Results attached where required locally (CXR report/images; baseline bloods).

Safety-netting

  • Ensure the patient knows who will contact them with results (and when), and when/how to re-present if symptoms worsen.
  • Escalate urgently if: new/worsening breathlessness, haemoptysis, chest pain, systemic deterioration.

Local agreement needed (D&G)

Reminder
A normal CXR does not exclude lung cancer. If symptoms persist or evolve, re-assess and escalate (local route).
