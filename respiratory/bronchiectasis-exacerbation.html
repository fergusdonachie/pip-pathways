DGRefHelp drafts / Bronchiectasis exacerbation

Adults with known (or likely) bronchiectasis: recognise an acute exacerbation, send sputum, start appropriate oral antibiotics (guided by prior cultures), escalate early if severe/unwell or not improving, and ensure airway clearance advice.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • NICE antimicrobial guidance (NG117) defines an acute exacerbation as a sustained worsening from the person’s baseline, and recommends sending sputum for culture/susceptibility and offering antibiotics, using prior sputum results to guide choice where possible.
  • NICE NG117 recommends oral antibiotics first line when the person can take oral treatment and is not severely unwell, and emphasises reassessment if rapidly worsening or not improving; refer to hospital for suspected serious illness (e.g. sepsis/cardiorespiratory failure).
  • BTS bronchiectasis guideline (Thorax 2019) frames exacerbations as an acute deterioration over days with worsening cough and sputum volume/viscosity and/or increased sputum purulence (± wheeze, breathlessness, haemoptysis, systemic upset). BTS supports using previous bacteriology to target antibiotics, and commonly uses 14 days for exacerbation antibiotic courses (especially in more severe disease / Pseudomonas).

Bronchiectasis exacerbation — adults (primary care)

Scope / definition

  • This page is for adults with known or likely bronchiectasis in primary care.
  • Acute exacerbation = sustained worsening from baseline over days, typically with worsening cough and increased sputum volume/purulence (± breathlessness, wheeze, haemoptysis, systemic upset).
  • Separate pathway may be needed for: pregnancy, children, severe immunocompromise, cystic fibrosis, and patients under specialist shared-care protocols.

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Severe breathlessness, respiratory distress, hypoxia, exhaustion, cyanosis.
  • Sepsis / systemically very unwell.
  • Large-volume haemoptysis or haemoptysis with instability.
  • Concern for pneumonia with instability, cardiorespiratory failure, or another emergency diagnosis.

Same-day assessment / consider hospital referral (SDEC/AMU/ED per local process)

  • SpO2 low or new oxygen requirement; significant tachypnoea.
  • Marked comorbidity / frailty or unable to maintain oral intake.
  • New focal chest signs or high suspicion of pneumonia.
  • Failure to improve, or rapidly worsening symptoms despite antibiotics.

Assessment (what to check)

  • Confirm this is bronchiectasis‑type deterioration: change in sputum (volume/colour/purulence), increased cough, breathlessness/wheeze, fatigue/fever.
  • Obs: RR, SpO2, HR, BP, temperature. Consider NEWS2 if used locally.
  • Exclude alternatives: pneumonia, asthma/COPD exacerbation, heart failure, PE, medication side-effects.
  • Risk features for resistant organisms / treatment failure: frequent recent antibiotics, recent admission, prior resistant organisms, previous Pseudomonas culture/colonisation, severe underlying lung disease.

Investigations (do early if feasible)

  • Sputum sample for culture & susceptibility (send before/at start of antibiotics where possible).
  • CXR if suspected pneumonia or new focal signs, significant systemic illness, or diagnostic uncertainty (local route/urgency).
  • Consider FBC/CRP/U&E if systemically unwell, recurrent exacerbations, or prolonged course (local practice).

Initial management (all patients)

  • Airway clearance: encourage increased airway clearance during exacerbation (use the patient’s usual technique if trained); hydration may help. Consider prompt referral/advice from respiratory physio if available.
  • Bronchodilators if wheeze/airflow limitation or coexistent asthma/COPD (per existing plan).
  • Self-management plan: if the patient has a specialist “rescue pack”/plan, follow it (and still send sputum if possible).

Antibiotics (follow local antimicrobial guidance; use prior sputum results)

  • Offer an antibiotic for an acute exacerbation.
  • Oral first line if can take oral treatment and not severely unwell.
  • In adults without current susceptibility data (and guided by most recent sputum results), NICE NG117 lists typical first-choice oral options as: amoxicillin, doxycycline, or clarithromycin (choose per allergy/pregnancy/local policy).
  • If higher risk of treatment failure, NICE NG117 lists co‑amoxiclav as an alternative empirical option; seek advice for fluoroquinolones (due to safety restrictions) and for suspected resistant organisms.
  • Pseudomonas / resistant organism history: do not guess — use previous microbiology and/or seek specialist/microbiology advice; ensure prompt escalation pathways are clear.
  • Course length: 7–14 days depending on severity/history/response; many patients (especially more severe disease) are treated for 14 days (specialist guidance/local protocol).

Review / reassess

  • Safety-net: seek urgent help if rapidly worse, systemically very unwell, or new red flags.
  • Reassess if symptoms are not improving or the patient needs repeated courses:
    • Consider pneumonia/alternative diagnosis.
    • Check sputum result and only change antibiotic based on resistance/poor response (use narrow-spectrum where possible).
    • Seek specialist advice (consider community IV antibiotics if available).

When to refer / who should be under respiratory follow-up (principles)

  • Hospital / same-day assessment for severe illness or suspected complications (see above).
  • Seek respiratory advice/referral if:
    • Symptoms not improving with repeated antibiotic courses, or
    • Suspected/known resistant organisms (e.g. prior Pseudomonas) and no clear current plan, or
    • Frequent exacerbations (e.g. ≥3/year), recurrent admissions, or overall deterioration, or
    • Chronic colonisation with high-risk organisms (Pseudomonas, NTM, MRSA) or on long-term antibiotics (shared care).

Local agreement needed (D&G)

Reminder
Don’t miss pneumonia: new focal signs, fever/systemic illness, hypoxia or significant deterioration should lower the threshold for CXR and same-day assessment.
