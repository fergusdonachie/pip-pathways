DGRefHelp drafts / Chronic cough

Adults with cough >8 weeks: identify red flags, do baseline tests (CXR + spirometry ± FeNO), treat common “treatable traits” (don’t empirically over-treat), and refer/route appropriately (incl. lung cancer caution).
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • BTS clinical statement (Thorax 2023) recommends a systematic primary‑care assessment for cough >8 weeks: history/exam including upper airway + cardio‑resp, and baseline tests of CXR, spirometry and (where available) FeNO plus FBC. Consider sputum microbiology (incl. AFB) if productive/high-risk.
  • Before “labelling” the cough, BTS emphasises identifying and treating aggravants (ACE inhibitor, smoking) and addressing treatable traits using objective evidence or symptoms; avoid broad empirical therapy (e.g. routine PPI without reflux symptoms).
  • Red flags should trigger urgent imaging/referral. NICE NG12 specifically supports urgent CXR (within 2 weeks) for people aged ≥40 with unexplained cough when combined with other unexplained symptoms, or with a smoking/asbestos history plus at least one listed symptom.
Local-process sensitivity
  • Exact routes differ for: urgent CXR, access to spirometry/FeNO, and the threshold for suspected lung cancer pathway referral when the CXR is normal but symptoms persist.
  • This page provides a safe decision structure + a local‑agreement checklist to localise for D&G.

Comparator sources used for the draft

  • British Thoracic Society Clinical Statement (Thorax 2023): approach to chronic cough in adults (primary‑care assessment, red flags, treatable traits).
  • NICE NG12 (Suspected cancer: recognition and referral): lung/pleural cancer recommendations (urgent CXR criteria; suspected cancer pathway referral triggered by CXR findings and other criteria).

Chronic cough (>8 weeks) — adults (primary care)

Scope / definition

  • Chronic cough = cough lasting >8 weeks.
  • Separate pathway needed for children, pregnancy, and significant immunocompromise (lower threshold for same‑day assessment).

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Severe breathlessness, hypoxia, respiratory distress, or exhaustion.
  • Haemoptysis with instability or large-volume ongoing bleeding.
  • Chest pain with collapse, or concern for PE with instability.
  • Sepsis features or markedly unwell patient.

Urgent / suspected cancer pathway red flags (act urgently; same day or 2WW/urgent CXR per local process)

  • Haemoptysis (see haemoptysis page).
  • Systemic features: unexplained weight loss, fever, night sweats, anorexia, marked fatigue.
  • Persistent hoarseness or concerning dysphagia symptoms.
  • Recurrent/persistent chest infections or persistent focal chest signs.
  • Age ≥40 with risk factors (current/ex‑smoker or asbestos exposure) and unexplained cough (especially if with other NG12 symptoms).

Step 1 — core history / examination

  • Character/timing: dry vs productive; triggers; nocturnal; post‑viral; episodic vs daily; duration.
  • Associated symptoms: breathlessness, wheeze, chest pain, heartburn/regurgitation, post‑nasal drip/rhinitis, voice change, fever, weight loss.
  • Exposures: smoking/vaping; occupational (incl. asbestos); household mould; travel/TB exposure.
  • Medication: ACE inhibitor (and when started); other drugs that may irritate airways.
  • Examination: upper airway (nose/throat), chest, cardiovascular system; consider lymph nodes.

Step 2 — baseline tests in primary care (do early)

  • CXR (particularly if unexplained, persistent symptoms, or any red flags).
  • Spirometry (with bronchodilator reversibility if indicated).
  • FeNO if available (helps identify eosinophilic airway inflammation / likely steroid response).
  • FBC (consider eosinophils; anaemia/infection clues).
  • Sputum culture if productive; consider TB testing/AFB where risk features (local process).

Step 3 — remove aggravants

  • ACE inhibitor: stop/switch if possible (can take weeks to resolve).
  • Smoking/vaping: offer cessation support; advise it is a common driver of chronic cough.

Step 4 — treat “treatable traits” (avoid blind empirical treatment)

  • Airway disease (asthma/COPD/airflow limitation): optimise inhaled therapy per local guidance.
  • Possible eosinophilic airway disease: consider a 1‑month inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) trial if supportive evidence (e.g. raised FeNO and/or eosinophils; or clear asthma symptoms/wheeze).
  • Rhinosinusitis / upper airway symptoms: trial intranasal steroid and saline irrigation/douching if symptoms/signs consistent.
  • Reflux: trial PPI only if reflux symptoms (e.g. heartburn/regurgitation); focus on lifestyle measures where appropriate.
  • Anxiety / cough hypersensitivity context: explain cough hypersensitivity; consider non‑pharmacological strategies and address contributing anxiety/depression where present.

When to refer / where to send

  • Suspected lung cancer pathway referral / urgent imaging if:
    • Red‑flag features (above), or
    • Abnormal CXR suspicious for malignancy, or
    • NICE NG12 criteria for urgent CXR are met (and act on the result promptly).
  • Routine respiratory referral if:
    • Cough persists despite: removal of aggravants + baseline tests + targeted treatment of identified traits, or
    • Recurrent productive cough / bronchiectasis suspicion, abnormal spirometry needing specialist input, or
    • Diagnostic uncertainty.
  • ENT consideration if prominent throat symptoms/voice change suggest laryngeal pathology and CXR/spirometry are reassuring (local route).

Safety‑netting

  • Seek urgent help if: new haemoptysis, worsening breathlessness, chest pain, fever/systemic decline, weight loss, or any red flag develops.
  • Ensure a results-follow-up plan for CXR/spirometry/FeNO/bloods, with a clear review date.

Local agreement needed (D&G)

Reminder
Chronic cough is common, but don’t normalise it: do baseline tests early and keep a low threshold to escalate if red flags or abnormal CXR.
