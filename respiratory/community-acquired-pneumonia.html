DGRefHelp drafts / Community-acquired pneumonia (adults)

Adult CAP: confirm likely pneumonia clinically, assess severity (CRB‑65 + judgement), start antibiotics promptly, and decide community vs hospital/SDEC.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (NICE-aligned)
  • Use clinical judgement plus CRB‑65 to stratify severity in adults with suspected community-acquired pneumonia (CAP).
  • CAP is higher risk when there is hypoxia, sepsis, confusion, hypotension, or significant comorbidity/frailty.
  • Start antibiotics as soon as a diagnosis is established. In hospital, NICE targets starting within 4 hours of presentation.
  • Microbiological testing is generally reserved for moderate/high severity or when there are additional indications (e.g. suspected sepsis, risk factors for legionella).
  • Follow-up CXR is not routine after inpatient pneumonia, but is considered at ~6 weeks for people with lung cancer risk factors (e.g. smokers, age >50), persisting symptoms, or weight loss.
Antibiotics: what this draft does (and does not) do

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) — adults (primary care)

Definition / scope

  • Acute illness (typically <21 days) with symptoms/signs consistent with pneumonia, acquired outside hospital.
  • Applies to adults; separate pathways needed for children, pregnancy, and immunocompromise.

Immediate red flags (same-day assessment / ED)

  • SpO2 low (especially <92% on air, or any cyanosis) or signs of respiratory failure.
  • Sepsis concern: hypotension, new confusion, lactate concern, mottling, not passing urine, rapidly deteriorating.
  • CRB‑65 high severity (score 3–4).
  • Unable to maintain oral intake, repeated vomiting, or cannot cope at home.
  • Alternative diagnosis suspected that is time-critical (e.g. PE, ACS, pneumothorax), or diagnostic uncertainty in a sick patient.

Assess severity (CRB‑65 + judgement)

  • CRB‑65 (1 point each):
    • Confusion (new disorientation)
    • Respiratory rate ≥30/min
    • Blood pressure: systolic <90 or diastolic ≤60
    • Age ≥65
  • Interpretation (typical):
    • Score 0: usually low severity → consider community management if safe.
    • Score 1–2: moderate severity → consider hospital/SDEC/virtual-ward depending on clinical judgement and support.
    • Score 3–4: high severity → urgent hospital assessment/admission.
  • Upgrade risk even with low CRB‑65 if: significant hypoxia, frailty, immunosuppression, major comorbidity (COPD/heart failure/CKD), or poor social support.

Assessment (primary care)

  • History: fever/rigors, cough, sputum, pleuritic pain, breathlessness, haemoptysis, duration, sick contacts/travel, aspiration risk; comorbidities and immunosuppression.
  • Observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air (document if on oxygen), mental state.
  • Examination: work of breathing, focal chest signs, signs of heart failure/volume overload; calf swelling/pain if PE risk.
  • Investigations (community): none routinely mandated; consider CXR if diagnosis uncertain, severe symptoms, poor response, or cancer risk features.

Initial management (community)

  • Start antibiotics when clinical diagnosis of CAP is made (do not delay unnecessarily).
  • Antibiotic choice: use local antimicrobial guidance (typical pattern: amoxicillin for low severity; doxycycline/clarithromycin if penicillin allergy; broader cover for comorbidity/moderate severity — local policy).
  • Safety-net: seek urgent help if worsening breathlessness, persistent fever, confusion, unable to eat/drink, or not improving within ~3 days.
  • Consider smoking cessation advice and review pneumococcal/flu vaccination status opportunistically.

Who to refer / admit

  • ED / acute medical assessment: high severity (CRB‑65 3–4), hypoxia, sepsis concern, inability to cope, significant diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Consider SDEC / hospital-at-home / virtual ward (if available): moderate severity (CRB‑65 1–2) with stable obs and support, or borderline cases needing CXR/bloods/short interval review.
  • Routine respiratory is usually not appropriate for acute CAP unless complications or persistent symptoms suggest alternative diagnosis (local pathway decision).

Who not to refer (usually)

  • Low severity CAP (CRB‑65 0) with normal/acceptable obs, able to cope at home, and clear safety-netting.

Referral letter / triage metrics (if referring)

  • CRB‑65 components (explicitly state confusion? RR? BP? age?), and your overall severity judgement.
  • SpO2 on air (and whether on oxygen), RR, HR, BP, Temp.
  • Duration of illness; main symptoms; pleuritic pain; haemoptysis.
  • Comorbidities, frailty, immunosuppression; aspiration risk.
  • Any tests done (CXR if obtained; ECG if chest pain; swabs if relevant).
  • Antibiotics given (drug + dose + start time) and response so far.
  • Home situation / support / ability to manage oral meds/fluids.
