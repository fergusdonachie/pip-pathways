DGRefHelp drafts / Pleural effusion

CXR-confirmed pleural effusion: assess severity and red flags, do safe baseline tests, and route to same-day assessment / pleural clinic / suspected cancer pathway as appropriate.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • In primary care, pleural effusion work is mostly triage + safe baseline assessment; definitive investigation (usually ultrasound-guided aspiration with pleural fluid analysis and targeted imaging) sits in secondary care pleural services.
  • First decide: unstable / very symptomatic? (hypoxia, significant respiratory distress, sepsis) → same-day hospital assessment.
  • An unexplained unilateral pleural effusion should be treated as potentially serious until proven otherwise (malignancy, pleural infection, PE, TB) and routed for prompt specialist assessment.
  • NICE NG12 includes pleural-disease signals for mesothelioma (urgent CXR for adults ≥40 with chest signs compatible with pleural disease; suspected cancer pathway referral if CXR suggests mesothelioma).
Local-process sensitivity
  • Routes vary: SDEC/acute medicine vs respiratory/pleural clinic vs radiology “direct to USS + aspiration”.
  • Access to same-day CXR, urgent ultrasound, and who can aspirate pleural fluid differs by health board.
  • This page is drafted to be safe even if the D&G routing is not yet agreed; see the Local agreement needed checklist.

Comparator sources used for the draft

  • NICE NG12 (Suspected cancer: recognition and referral): lung and pleural cancers section (mesothelioma: urgent CXR criteria; suspected cancer pathway referral for CXR suggesting mesothelioma).
  • British Thoracic Society (BTS) Pleural Disease Guideline (2023): pleural disease guidance including investigation/management pathways for undiagnosed unilateral pleural effusion (high-level principles used; local implementation varies).
Scope note
This is a primary care triage/referral draft. It is not a pleural-procedures guide.

Pleural effusion — adults (primary care triage)

Definition / scope

  • Pleural effusion = abnormal fluid in the pleural space (usually detected on CXR/ultrasound).
  • Applies to adults. Separate pathways needed for children, pregnancy, and significant immunocompromise.
  • Assumes effusion is suspected clinically and/or seen on imaging; if not yet imaged, start with CXR (and consider same-day assessment if unwell).

Immediate red flags (send same day: ED / SDEC / acute assessment)

  • Respiratory compromise: severe breathlessness, exhaustion, cyanosis, SpO2 low on air, new confusion.
  • Sepsis / pleural infection concern: fever, rigors, pleuritic chest pain, toxic appearance, rising inflammatory markers (if available), or CXR suggesting parapneumonic effusion/empyema.
  • Haemodynamic instability or syncope.
  • Massive effusion with significant symptoms or mediastinal shift on imaging (if reported).
  • Possible PE with instability (pleuritic pain + breathlessness ± haemoptysis ± collapse).

Step 1 — confirm + characterise (what you can safely do in primary care)

  • CXR: confirm effusion; note unilateral vs bilateral; any mass/pleural thickening/collapse/consolidation.
  • Observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air.
  • History:
    • Time course; pleuritic pain; cough/fever; weight loss/night sweats; haemoptysis.
    • Heart failure features (orthopnoea/PND, ankle oedema).
    • VTE risk factors (recent surgery/immobility, previous VTE, malignancy, oestrogen therapy).
    • TB risk (exposure, travel, immunosuppression).
    • Asbestos exposure (mesothelioma risk).
  • Examination: reduced chest expansion, reduced breath sounds, stony dullness; signs of HF; lymphadenopathy.

Step 2 — baseline tests (before referral, if stable and this doesn’t delay)

  • Bloods (suggested): FBC, U&E, LFT, CRP (if infection suspected).
  • Consider NT‑proBNP if HF is plausible and diagnosis uncertain (local availability).
  • Do not delay same-day assessment for bloods if the patient is significantly symptomatic/unwell.

Step 3 — likely cause patterns (to guide routing)

  • Heart failure (often bilateral): dyspnoea, oedema, raised JVP, cardiomegaly; consider HF workup and treatment; refer/admit if new severe HF or hypoxia.
  • Parapneumonic effusion / empyema: febrile, pleuritic pain, pneumonia features; needs same-day assessment for antibiotics +/- drainage.
  • Malignancy (often unilateral): weight loss, persistent symptoms, haemoptysis, pleural thickening, large recurrent effusion; prompt pleural/resp assessment.
  • PE: pleuritic pain + breathlessness; may have small effusion; follow suspected PE pathway (local).
  • TB: systemic symptoms + risk factors; pleural effusion can be a presenting feature; needs specialist assessment and microbiology liaison.

Who to refer / where to send

  • Same-day hospital assessment (ED/SDEC) for: red flags above, suspected empyema/parapneumonic effusion, hypoxia, severe symptoms, or diagnostic uncertainty in a sick patient.
  • Respiratory / pleural service (urgent) for:
    • Unexplained unilateral effusion (especially if new),
    • effusion with pleural thickening or suspicious CXR features,
    • recurrent effusion, or
    • effusion where aspiration/biopsy is likely needed (most unilateral effusions).
  • Suspected cancer pathway referral (lung/pleural; urgent) if:
    • CXR has findings that suggest mesothelioma (NICE NG12), or
    • CXR is otherwise suspicious for lung/pleural malignancy (local pathway).

What to include in the referral

  • Symptoms and timeline; pleuritic pain; fever; weight loss/night sweats; haemoptysis.
  • Smoking and asbestos exposure history (if known).
  • VTE risk factors and PE features (if present).
  • Observations incl. SpO2 on air.
  • CXR report (attach/copy); baseline bloods (if done).

Safety-netting

  • Seek urgent care if worsening breathlessness, chest pain, fever/rigors, haemoptysis, syncope, or SpO2 drops.
  • If managed as HF in community, arrange close follow-up and reassess if not improving promptly.

Local agreement needed (D&G)

Reminder
Do not attempt pleural aspiration in primary care unless you are trained, equipped, and working in an appropriate environment with imaging guidance and monitoring.
