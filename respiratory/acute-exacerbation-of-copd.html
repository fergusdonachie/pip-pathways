AECOPD: assess for respiratory failure/pneumonia, document obs (esp. SpO2), optimise bronchodilators, give a short steroid course, and use antibiotics only when bacterial features suggest benefit.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (NICE-aligned)
  • Many acute exacerbations are not bacterial; antibiotics should be targeted to clinical features suggesting likely bacterial infection (not automatic).
  • Prioritise severity assessment: hypoxia/respiratory failure, sepsis, pneumonia/PE/heart failure in the differential.
  • Systemic steroids reduce recovery time and treatment failure in AECOPD; NICE COPD guidance includes: offer oral prednisolone 30 mg daily for 5 days when corticosteroids are needed for an exacerbation.
  • NICE antimicrobial guidance (NG114) provides example antibiotic choices and emphasises: review response within 2–3 days; if sputum culture taken, only change antibiotic based on susceptibilities when symptoms are not improving.
  • Oxygen can be harmful if given inappropriately in COPD; use a target saturation approach and escalate for suspected hypercapnic respiratory failure.
Localisation needed
  • Antibiotic regimens must align to D&G antimicrobial guidance (formulary/resistance vary); NICE options are a safe default but may not be the local standard.
  • SDEC/respiratory assessment access and community oxygen/ABG processes vary; keep local routes explicit.

Comparator sources used for the draft

  • NICE NG115 (COPD in over 16s): exacerbation management principles; short-course oral prednisolone recommendation.
  • NICE NG114 (AECOPD: antimicrobial prescribing): when to consider antibiotics, and example antibiotic options/duration.
  • BTS Emergency Oxygen guideline (2017; with ongoing updates noted by BTS): target saturation strategy and oxygen safety in hypercapnic risk.

Acute exacerbation of COPD (AECOPD) — primary care

Definition / scope

  • Sustained worsening of COPD symptoms from the person’s usual stable state (typically increased breathlessness, cough, and/or sputum volume/purulence).
  • Always consider alternative diagnoses and complications (pneumonia, PE, heart failure, pneumothorax, ACS, sepsis).

Immediate red flags (same-day assessment / ED)

  • SpO2 low (especially <92% on air, or falling vs usual baseline) or severe breathlessness / exhaustion / cyanosis.
  • New confusion, drowsiness, or signs of hypercapnia (CO2 retention concern).
  • Sepsis concern: hypotension, persistent tachycardia, rigors, mottling, very unwell.
  • Chest pain, syncope, haemoptysis, unilateral pleuritic pain (PE/ACS/pneumothorax differential).
  • Unable to cope at home (cannot speak in sentences, cannot manage ADLs, poor support) or unable to maintain oral intake.
  • Suspected pneumonia (focal chest signs, high fever, pleuritic pain, significant hypoxia) — follow CAP pathway.

Assessment (primary care)

  • Observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air (document if on oxygen), mental state; consider NEWS2 if used locally.
  • History: baseline COPD severity (MRC dyspnoea), usual SpO2 (if known), change in symptoms; sputum volume/colour; fever; pleuritic pain; VTE risk; heart failure symptoms; inhaler adherence; recent antibiotics/oral steroids; previous admissions/NIV/ICU.
  • Examination: work of breathing, wheeze/air entry, focal signs, peripheral oedema, signs of DVT, hydration.
  • Community investigations: none mandated; consider CXR/ECG if pneumonia/heart failure/ACS/PE is possible or diagnostic uncertainty in a sick patient.

Initial management (community) — if safe to treat at home

  • Bronchodilators: increase use of SABA (± SAMA) using inhaler + spacer; consider nebuliser only if already prescribed/available and appropriate.
  • Oral corticosteroid: consider if significant increase in breathlessness interfering with daily activities; NICE option: prednisolone 30 mg daily for 5 days (avoid prolonged courses).
  • Antibiotics: do not give routinely. Consider if bacterial features (especially sputum purulence plus increased volume/thickness and/or increased breathlessness) and/or higher risk of complications. Use local antimicrobial guidance. (NICE NG114 examples: amoxicillin / doxycycline / clarithromycin for 5 days.)
  • Oxygen: if hypoxic and oxygen is being given in the community, use a target saturation approach and escalate urgently if CO2 retention risk is suspected (local policy).
  • Safety-net / follow-up: review if not starting to improve within 48–72 hours, or earlier if worsening. Give clear ED triggers (worsening breathlessness, drowsiness, new confusion, chest pain, unable to cope).

Who to refer / admit

  • ED / acute medical assessment: any red flag above; suspected respiratory failure/hypercapnia; severe hypoxia; sepsis; suspected pneumonia needing assessment; inability to cope at home.
  • Consider SDEC / rapid-access respiratory / hospital-at-home (if available locally): borderline cases needing CXR, bloods/ABG, IV therapy, or short-interval observation; frequent exacerbations or significant comorbidity/frailty.

Who not to refer (usually)

  • Mild/moderate AECOPD with stable observations, no red flags, and safe home circumstances with clear follow-up/safety-netting.

Referral letter / triage metrics (if referring)

  • SpO2 on air (and whether on oxygen), RR, HR, BP, Temp, mental state; NEWS2 if used.
  • Baseline COPD severity (MRC), usual SpO2 if known; prior NIV/ICU; number of exacerbations/admissions in last 12 months.
  • Sputum: volume/colour change; fever; pleuritic pain; chest pain; haemoptysis.
  • Comorbidities (HF/IHD/CKD/diabetes), frailty, immunosuppression, smoking status.
  • Medications: inhalers (incl. ICS), home oxygen, rescue pack use; what you have started (bronchodilators/steroids/antibiotics with doses + start time).
  • Any tests done (CXR/ECG) and findings.
  • Home situation/support and ability to manage at home.
