Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (BTS/SIGN-aligned)
  • Acute asthma severity assessment should not rely on wheeze: a silent chest, exhaustion or altered consciousness are high-risk signs.
  • Use a structured severity classification (moderate / acute severe / life-threatening / near-fatal) based on clinical features + PEF and SpO2.
  • Give high-dose inhaled SABA promptly (via MDI + spacer where possible); start oral corticosteroids early in acute attacks.
  • Patients with acute severe or life-threatening features generally need same-day ED/acute assessment (and many will need nebulised therapy, oxygen, IV therapy and/or ABGs).
  • Oxygen: use a target saturation approach (asthma usually targets 94–98%) and escalate if deteriorating.
Localisation needed
  • How primary care accesses nebulisers, oxygen, peak-flow meters and emergency drugs varies — keep the local operational pathway explicit.
  • SDEC / rapid-access respiratory services and ambulance thresholds are local; this draft uses standard BTS/SIGN severity triggers as a default.

Acute asthma (adult) — primary care / out-of-hospital

Definition / scope

  • Acute worsening of asthma symptoms (wheeze, breathlessness, chest tightness, cough) requiring additional reliever therapy.
  • This page is for adults. (Children/pregnancy: use age-/setting-specific guidance.)
  • Always consider differentials, especially if atypical: anaphylaxis, pneumonia, pneumothorax, PE, heart failure, upper airway obstruction / inducible laryngeal obstruction.

Immediate red flags (call 999 / ED)

  • Life-threatening features (any):
    • Unable to speak, confusion/drowsiness, collapse, exhaustion, cyanosis.
    • Silent chest / very poor respiratory effort.
    • SpO2 <92% (or falling despite treatment).
    • PEF <33% of best/predicted (if measurable).
  • Acute severe asthma features (treat + same-day ED assessment):
    • PEF 33–50% of best/predicted.
    • RR ≥25/min or HR ≥110/min.
    • Unable to complete sentences in one breath.
  • Near-fatal asthma is suspected if rising CO2 / respiratory failure is likely (typically requires ABGs and critical care level assessment).

Assessment (do quickly; repeat after treatment)

  • Observations: RR, HR, BP, Temp, SpO2; mental state; work of breathing; ability to speak.
  • Peak expiratory flow (PEF): record best of 3 if feasible; compare to personal best or predicted; document %.
  • History: previous ICU/ventilation/near-fatal attack; current inhalers and adherence; recent oral steroids; triggers (viral, allergen, NSAIDs); smoking/vaping; comorbid COPD; pregnancy (if relevant).
  • Look for alternate diagnosis: chest pain/pleurisy, fever, unilateral reduced air entry, signs of anaphylaxis (urticaria, angioedema, hypotension), calf pain/haemoptysis.

Immediate treatment (primary care / out-of-hospital)

  • High-dose inhaled SABA: give promptly.
    • Preferred: salbutamol MDI + spacer (repeated doses as per local emergency protocol).
    • If severe and MDI/spacer not feasible: nebulised salbutamol (if available) while awaiting ambulance.
  • Oral corticosteroid early: if acute attack needing reliever escalation or any severe features — typical adult dosing is prednisolone 40–50 mg daily for 5 days (local protocol/formulary).
  • Oxygen (if available): target SpO2 94–98%. Escalate urgently if hypoxic or deteriorating.
  • Add ipratropium (usually nebulised) for acute severe/life-threatening asthma if available locally and awaiting transfer.
  • Antibiotics: not routine for asthma attacks; consider only if clear evidence of bacterial infection.

Disposition

  • ED / acute medical assessment: any acute severe or life-threatening features; poor response to initial bronchodilator; recurrent symptoms soon after treatment; significant comorbidity/frailty; social concerns or inability to self-manage.
  • Consider home management only if clearly mild/moderate, improves quickly with inhaled SABA, observations stable (including SpO2), and there is a reliable plan for follow-up and escalation.

Aftercare if managed in the community

  • Review within 24–48 hours (earlier if any concern).
  • Check inhaler technique and adherence; ensure an asthma action plan.
  • Optimise preventer therapy and arrange routine asthma review (consider MART/ICS-formoterol strategy if used locally).
  • Safety-net: worsening breathlessness, reduced speech, fainting, blue lips, SpO2 falling, or needing reliever more frequently than every 4 hours → seek urgent help / call 999.

Referral letter / triage metrics (if referring)

  • Severity classification (moderate / acute severe / life-threatening) and what features are present.
  • Observations (RR/HR/BP/Temp/SpO2) and mental state.
  • PEF: absolute value + % best/predicted, and response to treatment.
  • Treatments given + times (SABA doses/route; ipratropium; oxygen; steroid dose/time).
  • Past risk history: ICU/ventilation, previous near-fatal attack, recent oral steroids, previous admissions.
  • Trigger/differential concerns (anaphylaxis, pneumonia, pneumothorax, PE).
