DGRefHelp drafts / Pneumothorax

Primary care recognition + immediate actions: identify tension/unstable patients for blue-light transfer, provide safe first steps, and refer stable suspected spontaneous pneumothorax urgently for same-day assessment.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis with immediate life-threatening potential: do not delay transfer for imaging if the patient is unstable.
  • In primary care, management is primarily recognition + triage + safe immediate actions. Definitive interventions (aspiration/chest drain) sit in ED/acute care.
  • BTS pleural disease guidance (2023) supports a more personalised approach for spontaneous pneumothorax; however, in community settings with limited monitoring/imaging, a low threshold for same-day hospital assessment remains appropriate.
  • RCEMLearning notes that stable ED patients should generally have urgent CXR before “blind” needle decompression; but in a peri-arrest/unstable patient with suspected tension pneumothorax, urgent decompression is indicated, and absence of a ‘hiss’ does not exclude tension (cannula too short/kinked/blocked).
Local-process sensitivity
  • Routes vary (ED vs SDEC/acute medicine vs respiratory) and whether ambulance transfer is mandated for all suspected pneumothorax.
  • This draft is conservative for safety; refine the Local agreement needed items once D&G routing is confirmed.

Comparator sources used for the draft

  • British Thoracic Society (BTS) Pleural Disease Guideline (2023): spontaneous pneumothorax management principles and pathways.
  • RCEMLearning (Spontaneous pneumothorax module): tension pneumothorax management notes (stable imaging-first; do not rely on “hiss”).
Scope note
This is a primary care triage draft (recognise + route). It is not a procedural guide.

Pneumothorax — adults (primary care recognition + immediate actions)

Definition / scope

  • Pneumothorax = air in the pleural space causing partial/complete lung collapse.
  • Primary spontaneous: no known underlying lung disease (often young, tall, thin, smokers).
  • Secondary spontaneous: underlying lung disease (e.g. COPD, fibrosis) — usually higher risk of deterioration.
  • Traumatic/iatrogenic: after injury or procedure — manage as higher-risk; same-day ED/acute care.

Immediate red flags (999 / blue-light to ED)

  • Suspected tension pneumothorax (treat as emergency):
    • Severe respiratory distress, hypoxia, cyanosis, exhaustion, or reduced consciousness.
    • Haemodynamic compromise: hypotension, tachycardia, shock, collapse/syncope.
    • Tracheal deviation is a late/unreliable sign; do not wait for it.
  • Any unstable patient with sudden pleuritic chest pain + breathlessness (consider pneumothorax alongside PE/ACS/aortic pathology).
  • Significant comorbidity or reduced physiological reserve (advanced COPD, ILD, frailty) with acute breathlessness.

First actions in primary care (while arranging transfer)

  • ABC + observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air; consider ECG if chest pain.
  • Oxygen: give supplemental oxygen if hypoxic or in significant distress (do not delay transfer).
  • Analgesia: adequate pain relief for pleuritic pain (e.g. NSAID/paracetamol if suitable) — but not at the expense of transfer speed.
  • Do not send the patient to radiology/for outpatient CXR if they are unstable or significantly breathless.
  • Needle decompression / emergency procedures: only if you are trained, equipped, and the patient is peri-arrest/unstable with suspected tension pneumothorax. (In stable patients, urgent imaging in ED is usually preferred.)

How to recognise a likely pneumothorax (stable patient)

  • Symptoms: sudden unilateral pleuritic chest pain + acute breathlessness (may be mild); dry cough.
  • Signs: reduced breath sounds on one side, hyperresonance, reduced chest expansion; tachycardia; low SpO2 (may be normal in small primary spontaneous pneumothorax).
  • Risk markers:
    • Underlying lung disease (COPD/ILD), older age, significant smoking history.
    • Recent chest trauma/procedure (incl. central line, biopsy).

Where to send / who to refer

  • ED (999 if needed) for: suspected tension/unstable, hypoxia, severe breathlessness, syncope, trauma/iatrogenic, or significant comorbidity (secondary spontaneous likely).
  • Same-day hospital assessment (ED/SDEC) for: suspected spontaneous pneumothorax even if stable — to confirm on imaging and decide conservative management vs aspiration/drain (per BTS guidance and local pathways).
  • Do not manage a new suspected pneumothorax solely in the community unless an explicit local pathway exists that provides rapid imaging + senior review.

What to include in the referral / handover

  • Time of onset; symptoms severity; chest pain characteristics; triggers (cough/trauma/procedure).
  • PMH: COPD/ILD/asthma; smoking; previous pneumothorax; pregnancy (separate pathway needed).
  • Observations including SpO2 on air; response to oxygen; ECG if done.
  • Exam findings: side, breath sounds, respiratory effort.

Safety-netting (if stable and awaiting assessment)

  • Seek urgent help immediately if worsening breathlessness, chest pain, dizziness/syncope, or blue lips.
  • Avoid flying/scuba diving until assessed and advised (and for a period after resolution per specialist advice).

Local agreement needed (D&G)

Reminder
Tension pneumothorax can deteriorate rapidly. If in doubt and the patient is unwell, treat as emergency and transfer.
