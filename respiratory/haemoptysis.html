DGRefHelp drafts / Haemoptysis

Coughing up blood: confirm true haemoptysis, identify life‑threatening bleeding, do baseline assessment/tests, and route for urgent imaging / suspected cancer pathway when indicated.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • First decide if this is true haemoptysis (lower respiratory tract) vs pseudohaemoptysis (epistaxis/post‑nasal bleed) vs haematemesis.
  • Identify life‑threatening haemoptysis (airway compromise, hypoxia, haemodynamic instability, rapid ongoing bleeding) → 999 / ED.
  • If stable, the most useful initial test is usually a CXR. NICE NG12 includes haemoptysis as a red‑flag symptom for lung cancer assessment/referral pathways.
  • Scottish Right Decisions (NHS Highland) pathway emphasises urgent CXR for unexplained haemoptysis and specifies baseline bloods (FBC/U&E/LFT/calcium) to be available for timed lung‑cancer referral acceptance.
Local-process sensitivity
  • Exact routing differs: direct access CXR vs radiology request, and what is required to trigger/accept a suspected lung cancer pathway referral.
  • This page provides a safe decision structure + a local agreement checklist (so it can be rapidly localised for D&G).

Comparator sources used for the draft

  • NICE NG12 (Suspected cancer: recognition and referral): lung cancer section (urgent CXR; suspected cancer pathway referral for unexplained haemoptysis age ≥40).
  • Right Decisions (NHS Highland): Urgent suspected lung cancer quick reference (urgent CXR for unexplained haemoptysis; baseline blood tests; referral triggers incl persistent haemoptysis in smokers/ex‑smokers >40 even with normal CXR).

Haemoptysis (coughing up blood) — adults (primary care)

Definition / scope

  • Haemoptysis = blood expectorated from the lower respiratory tract.
  • Applies to adults. Separate pathways needed for children, pregnancy, and significant immunocompromise (lower threshold for same‑day assessment).

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Airway compromise, stridor, inability to clear secretions, or rapidly increasing bleeding.
  • Respiratory compromise: severe breathlessness, cyanosis, very low SpO2 on air, exhaustion.
  • Haemodynamic instability: syncope, hypotension, tachycardia, shock, ongoing large‑volume bleeding.
  • High-risk context: known lung cancer, significant underlying lung disease with deterioration, recent major haemoptysis, or on anticoagulants with significant bleeding.
  • PE concern with instability (pleuritic chest pain + breathlessness + haemoptysis + collapse).

Step 1 — confirm the source

  • Rule out pseudohaemoptysis: recent epistaxis, blood in mouth on waking, blood only when blowing nose.
  • Rule out haematemesis: nausea/retching, coffee‑ground material, melaena, known GI bleed risk.
  • If unclear and the patient is unwell → treat as high risk and arrange urgent assessment.

Step 2 — baseline assessment (stable patient)

  • History:
    • Amount/character (streaks vs teaspoons vs clots), frequency, duration, triggers.
    • Associated: fever, pleuritic pain, breathlessness, weight loss, night sweats, hoarseness, chest pain.
    • Infection/TB risk: exposure, travel, immunosuppression.
    • VTE risk: recent surgery/immobility, previous VTE, malignancy, oestrogen therapy.
    • Smoking history; occupational exposure (e.g. asbestos).
    • Medication: anticoagulants/antiplatelets; recent dose changes.
  • Observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air, mental state.
  • Examination: ENT/oral cavity, chest exam, signs of consolidation/effusion, calf swelling/tenderness, lymphadenopathy.

Step 3 — investigations in primary care

  • CXR: arrange urgently if unexplained haemoptysis, recurrent haemoptysis, or cancer risk features.
  • Bloods (suggested baseline): FBC, U&E, LFT, calcium; consider clotting (especially if anticoagulants/bleeding tendency) and CRP if infection suspected.
  • Sputum (if productive): consider culture; consider TB testing if risk features (local process).

Likely causes (common patterns)

  • Infection (acute bronchitis/pneumonia): fever, purulent sputum, pleuritic pain, focal chest signs.
  • Malignancy: persistent/recurrent haemoptysis, weight loss, smoker/ex‑smoker, abnormal CXR.
  • Pulmonary embolism: pleuritic chest pain + breathlessness ± haemoptysis (consider risk factors; follow PE pathway).
  • Bronchiectasis/COPD: chronic sputum, recurrent infections; exacerbations can bleed.
  • Upper airway bleed (pseudohaemoptysis): epistaxis/post‑nasal bleed.

Who to refer / where to send

  • Suspected lung cancer pathway referral (urgent) if:
    • Unexplained haemoptysis age ≥40 (NICE NG12), or
    • CXR suspicious (mass/effusion/slowly resolving consolidation), or
    • Persistent symptoms >6 weeks despite normal CXR (Right Decisions model; local process may vary), especially in smokers/ex‑smokers.
  • Same-day hospital assessment for: ongoing bleeding, physiological compromise, PE concern needing urgent workup, or diagnostic uncertainty in a sick patient.
  • Routine respiratory referral may be appropriate for recurrent small-volume haemoptysis with benign/known cause once cancer/serious causes excluded (local process).

Safety-netting (stable, community-managed)

  • Call 999 / attend ED if: bleeding increases, new/worsening breathlessness, chest pain, dizziness/syncope, or SpO2 drops.
  • If treated as infection: review if not improving within 48–72 hours, or sooner if worsening.
  • Ensure follow-up plan for results (CXR and bloods) and clear escalation advice.

Local agreement needed (D&G)

Reminder
If there is any physiological compromise or ongoing bleeding, this is not a “routine referral” scenario — send same day.
