DGRefHelp drafts / Lead poisoning / lead exposure

Suspected lead exposure (children, pregnancy, adults): when to test, what to do with results, and who to involve (public health / occupational health / NPIS).
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑13

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key points that change actions
  • There is no known safe blood lead concentration in children; the mainstay of management is identify and remove the source.
  • UK guidance uses a pragmatic “investigate exposure” threshold of ≥0.1 μmol/L (≥2 μg/dL) in children/pregnancy, and a “public health intervention” threshold of ≥0.24 μmol/L (≥5 μg/dL) for children <16 and pregnant women (England; similar level is used across UK nations).
  • For non‑pregnant adults, UK public health intervention commonly starts at ≥0.48 μmol/L (≥10 μg/dL) (England); occupational exposure is managed under HSE / occupational health.
  • Clinical symptoms are often absent until higher levels (classically >1.45 μmol/L / 30 μg/dL), but low-level exposure can still cause harm (esp neurodevelopment).
  • Use whole blood lead concentration for diagnosis; do not use hair/nail tests. Use EDTA tube for sampling (UKHSA).
Who to call
  • NPIS/TOXBASE (registered NHS users) for detailed clinical management and chelation advice; NPIS phone: 0344 892 0111 (health professionals).
  • Public health / Health Protection Team for source investigation and mitigation when the intervention concentration is met (exact route differs by UK nation/board).

Comparator sources used for the draft

  • UKHSA/GOV.UK (2024 update): clinical intervention thresholds; sampling/testing notes; symptoms and sources; NPIS/TOXBASE signposting.
  • UKHSA/GOV.UK: environmental/public health intervention thresholds and roles (public health intervention concentrations; home sources, water/paint, multi-agency response).
  • UKHSA LEICSS annual report (2023): confirms surveillance/intervention threshold and the “no safe level” framing.
This evidence summary is written for pathway design: it highlights decision‑changing thresholds and where local policy choices sit.

Lead poisoning / lead exposure (suspected) — primary care

Background

  • Lead exposure is usually chronic and may be asymptomatic; there is no known safe blood lead concentration in children.
  • Main treatment is identify and remove the exposure source (paint dust/renovation, imported cosmetics/spices/remedies, water/pipework, occupational/hobby exposure).
  • Use whole blood lead concentration (EDTA tube). Do not use hair/nail testing.

Assessment (primary care)

  • When to suspect / test
    • Children (esp 1–4 years): pica/hand-to-mouth, old/renovated housing, unexplained abdominal pain/constipation/headache/irritability, developmental or behavioural concerns, anaemia, faltering growth.
    • Pregnancy: any plausible exposure (paint stripping/renovation, occupational, imported products).
    • Adults: compatible symptoms (abdominal pain/constipation, neuropathy, fatigue/cognitive symptoms) or known exposure (occupational/hobby).
  • Exposure history: old/renovated housing; sanding/heat stripping; peeling paint; imported spices/remedies (kohl/surma); ceramics; shooting/fishing weights; battery/recycling; stained glass; “take-home” dust on clothes.
  • Tests
    • Whole blood lead concentration (EDTA tube).
    • FBC (anaemia). Consider iron studies (iron deficiency increases susceptibility/uptake).
    • Consider U&E/LFTs/Calcium as clinically indicated (systemic effects; pregnancy).
    • If accidental ingestion of a lead-containing object is suspected: consider abdominal X‑ray (local process).
  • Units: 5 μg/dL ≈ 0.24 μmol/L; 10 μg/dL ≈ 0.48 μmol/L (UKHSA conversion).

Primary care management (by blood lead concentration)

  • <0.1 μmol/L (<2 μg/dL): reassure; repeat only if ongoing exposure suspected.
  • ≥0.1 to <0.24 μmol/L (2–<5 μg/dL): increased exposure vs general population → identify/mitigate source; address iron/calcium deficiency; consider vulnerable household contacts.
  • ≥0.24 μmol/L (≥5 μg/dL) in child <16 or pregnant: inform public health (HPT) for source investigation + monitoring; repeat testing per advice.
  • Adults (non‑pregnant): public health involvement commonly at ≥0.48 μmol/L (≥10 μg/dL) in England; confirm local nation/board process. Consider occupational route (HSE/occupational health).
  • Symptomatic / very high level: urgent same‑day assessment; discuss with NPIS/toxicology (TOXBASE) re admission/chelation thresholds.

Who to refer / where to send

  • Same-day hospital / ED: significant symptoms (abdominal colic, vomiting, neuro symptoms), suspected encephalopathy/seizure, haemodynamic instability, or clinician concern.
  • NPIS / TOXBASE: advice on confirmed/high blood lead and chelation decisions (NPIS 0344 892 0111 for health professionals; TOXBASE for registered NHS users).
  • Public health / Health Protection Team for source investigation at intervention concentrations:
    • Child <16: ≥0.24 μmol/L (≥5 μg/dL).
    • Pregnant woman: ≥0.24 μmol/L (≥5 μg/dL).
    • Adult (non‑pregnant): ≥0.48 μmol/L (≥10 μg/dL) (England threshold; confirm local process).
  • Occupational exposure: route via occupational health / HSE (Control of Lead at Work).

Safety-netting

  • Return urgently / attend ED if worsening abdominal pain/vomiting, confusion, weakness, seizures, or any acute deterioration.
  • Document a plan for results handling + repeat test interval (public health/toxicology advice if elevated).
