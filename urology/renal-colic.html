Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Do not miss “infected obstruction”: renal colic + features of infection/sepsis is a surgical/urology emergency (same‑day).
  • Imaging standard: NICE NG118 recommends urgent (within 24 hours) low‑dose non‑contrast CT for adults with suspected renal colic (pregnancy: USS instead of CT).
  • Analgesia ladder: NSAID first line; if contraindicated/insufficient → IV paracetamol; then consider opioids. (Do not use antispasmodics.)

1) NICE NG118 (renal & ureteric stones — assessment and management)

  • Imaging (adults): offer urgent (within 24 hours) low‑dose non‑contrast CT for suspected renal colic. Pregnancy: ultrasound instead of CT.
  • Pain management: offer NSAID by any route first line. If contraindicated/insufficient: offer IV paracetamol; consider opioids if still contraindicated/insufficient. Do not offer antispasmodics.
  • Medical expulsive therapy: consider an alpha blocker for distal ureteric stones <10 mm (off‑label note in NG118).

2) Practical primary care triage patterns (what changes actions)

  • Red flags for same‑day assessment (common across NHS pathways):
    • fever/rigors or sepsis features (especially with obstruction)
    • uncontrolled pain/vomiting despite initial analgesia
    • AKI, anuria, or rising creatinine / hyperkalaemia concern
    • single kidney / transplanted kidney
    • pregnancy
    • diagnostic uncertainty / concern for alternative emergency (e.g., AAA, peritonitis)
  • Urine dip: haematuria is common but not universal; absence does not exclude stones.
  • If urine suggests infection (nitrites/leukocytes, infective symptoms): send MSU and treat, but do not delay escalation if obstructed/infected kidney suspected.

Renal colic

Background

  • Renal colic = suspected ureteric/renal stone causing acute flank/loin pain. Two things not to miss: (1) infected obstruction and (2) an alternative abdominal emergency (e.g., AAA).
  • If the patient is unwell (fever/rigors/sepsis), has AKI/anuria, or pain/vomiting is uncontrolled → same‑day assessment (obstructed infected kidney is an emergency).
  • NICE NG118: adults with suspected renal colic should have urgent (within 24 hours) low‑dose non‑contrast CT (pregnancy: ultrasound instead of CT).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Typical history: sudden severe flank/loin pain (often colicky/waves), may radiate to groin; nausea/vomiting; urinary frequency/urgency; haematuria may occur.
  • Red flags / same‑day:
    • fever/rigors or sepsis features (especially with flank pain)
    • uncontrolled pain or persistent vomiting despite initial analgesia/antiemetic
    • AKI, anuria, or severe renal impairment
    • single kidney / transplanted kidney
    • pregnancy
    • diagnostic uncertainty (concern for AAA/peritonitis/other surgical abdomen)
  • Obs/exam: record vitals; abdominal exam; check for peritonism; consider AAA risk if older/vascular risk.
  • Urine: dipstick for blood; if nitrites/leukocytes or infective symptoms → send MSU and treat as UTI/pyelonephritis but do not delay escalation if unwell/obstructed.
  • Bloods (if available / before referral): U&E/creatinine, FBC ± CRP; pregnancy test if relevant.

Primary care management

  • Analgesia (NICE NG118):
    • NSAID first line (any route) if safe.
    • If NSAID contraindicated/insufficient: IV paracetamol (usually requires secondary care setting).
    • If still inadequate: consider opioid analgesia.
    • Do not use antispasmodics for renal colic.
  • Hydration: normal oral fluids (aim pale urine); avoid “force‑drinking” as it does not flush the stone and may worsen pain.
  • Safety‑net: return/seek urgent help if fever/rigors, worsening pain, persistent vomiting, reduced urine output, or feeling generally unwell.
  • If a stone is passed: if retrievable, store dry in a sterile pot for analysis (local process).

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency and the correct local acute route.

Same‑day assessment (ED / acute pathway / on‑call surgery/urology)

  • Suspected renal colic with any red flags:
    • fever/rigors or sepsis features (obstructed infected kidney = emergency)
    • uncontrolled pain/vomiting
    • AKI/anuria or severe renal impairment
    • single kidney / transplant
    • pregnancy
    • diagnostic uncertainty / concern about AAA/peritonitis
  • Imaging standard (adults): urgent low‑dose non‑contrast CT within 24 hours (pregnancy: ultrasound).
  • Route (draft): ED / SDEC / on‑call surgical team (confirm local route).

Routine urology (non‑urgent)

  • Recurrent stone formers, atypical/recurrent symptoms, or metabolic risk factors where urology follow‑up is needed (local criteria).
  • Include: imaging report(s), renal function, urinalysis, infection history, and analgesia needs.

Who not to refer

  • Uncomplicated suspected renal colic with no red flags and symptoms resolving, once appropriate imaging and follow‑up plan is in place (local process dependent).
  • Non‑stone musculoskeletal flank pain once alternative diagnoses are assessed and symptoms improve.
