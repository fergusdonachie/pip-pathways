Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Do not ignore visible haematuria (even if it is painless, intermittent, or there are "benign" explanations such as anticoagulation).
  • Suspected cancer pathway (urology): NICE NG12 — age ≥45 with unexplained visible haematuria (no UTI) OR visible haematuria that persists/recurs after successful UTI treatment → suspected cancer pathway referral (bladder/renal).
  • UTI is a common masking problem (esp. older women): SRG emphasises urine culture for all haematuria and avoiding repeated UTI treatment without reassessment if haematuria persists.

1) NICE NG12 (urological cancers — haematuria)

  • Bladder cancer (suspected cancer pathway): refer if age 45+ with unexplained visible haematuria without UTI, or visible haematuria that persists/recurs after successful UTI treatment. Also refer if age 60+ with unexplained non‑visible haematuria plus dysuria or raised WCC.
  • Renal cancer (suspected cancer pathway): refer if age 45+ with unexplained visible haematuria without UTI, or visible haematuria that persists/recurs after successful UTI treatment.
  • Recurrent/persistent unexplained UTI (age 60+): consider non‑urgent referral for bladder cancer.
  • Prostate cancer overlap: NG12 says consider PSA + DRE in people with LUTS or erectile dysfunction or visible haematuria; refer on age‑threshold PSA or malignant-feeling prostate.

2) Scottish Referral Guidelines / Right Decisions (Bladder & kidney cancer)

  • Urine culture should be obtained if a person presents with haematuria (visible or non-visible).
  • Under 45: a single episode of visible haematuria (no UTI) does not meet USC criteria; consider imaging or alternative urology pathway depending on local guidance.
  • Notes groups at risk of diagnostic delay (e.g., older women with UTIs; recurrent UTIs).
  • Suggests seeking urology advice if recurrent haematuria after recent negative investigations (does not need USC).

3) Practical primary care synthesis (what changes actions)

  • Always confirm haematuria is urinary (vaginal bleeding, rectal bleeding, beetroot/dyes; myoglobin/haemolysis) and document whether it is visible vs dipstick-only.
  • For suspected UTI, treat based on symptoms + culture guidance, but recheck if visible haematuria persists or recurs after treatment.
  • Visible haematuria with red flags (clot retention, inability to pass urine, unstable patient) needs urgent acute assessment, not routine referral pathways.
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • Confirm the exact SCI Gateway destination/service naming for suspected urological cancer referral (bladder/renal) and the local direct-to-USOC vs generic "Urology" route.
  • Confirm whether D&G expects any pre-referral tests beyond urine culture (e.g., repeat MSU, U&Es/eGFR, FBC, PSA in men with LUTS, urine ACR for suspected renal cause).
  • Confirm whether primary care should request any imaging (e.g., renal tract USS) before routine urology referral for <45s / low-risk scenarios.

Visible haematuria

Background

  • Visible haematuria (blood seen in urine) is a urological red flag until proven otherwise.
  • Do not attribute visible haematuria to anticoagulation alone.
  • Common differentials: UTI, stones, benign prostatic disease, (less commonly) glomerular/renal disease, and urological malignancy.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Confirm it is urinary: exclude vaginal bleeding, rectal bleeding, food dyes; ask about timing (start/end of stream), clots, pain.
  • Red flags / same‑day assessment:
    • Haemodynamic instability, sepsis features, uncontrolled pain.
    • Inability to pass urine / clot retention.
    • Single kidney / immunosuppression + systemic illness (use judgement).
  • Symptoms that guide differentials:
    • Dysuria/frequency/urgency → consider UTI (but still safety‑net haematuria).
    • Colicky flank pain → consider stone.
    • LUTS (hesitancy/nocturia/poor flow) in men → consider prostate/BPH; consider PSA + DRE per NG12.
    • Proteinuria, oedema, hypertension → consider glomerular/renal cause (may need renal pathway).
  • Tests (baseline):
    • Urine dip (document blood, protein, nitrites, leucocytes).
    • Send urine culture for all haematuria (visible or non-visible).
    • If visible haematuria is ongoing/significant: consider FBC, U&Es/eGFR (local preference).

Primary care management

  • If UTI likely: treat per local antimicrobial guidance and send culture.
  • Safety‑net tightly: visible haematuria that persists or recurs after treatment counts towards suspected cancer referral criteria (age thresholds apply).
  • Advise to seek urgent help if unable to pass urine, worsening pain, fever/rigors, or heavy bleeding/clots.

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency and the correct service destination. If in doubt, treat visible haematuria as high significance and seek advice.

Suspected cancer pathway (urology)

  • NICE NG12: refer using a suspected cancer pathway for bladder/renal cancer if age ≥45 and either:
    • Unexplained visible haematuria without UTI, or
    • Visible haematuria that persists or recurs after successful treatment of UTI.
  • Route (draft): SCI Gateway → DGRI → Urology → Suspected cancer pathway (bladder/renal).

Other / non-USC referral or advice (local pathways)

  • Age <45 with visible haematuria (no UTI): consider routine urology or imaging based on local guidance.
  • Recurrent haematuria after recent negative investigations: consider urology advice (does not usually need USC).

Who not to refer

  • Do not delay referral by repeatedly treating presumed UTI if visible haematuria persists or recurs.
  • If visible haematuria is clearly explained by a transient benign cause and resolves completely, manage case-by-case with safety‑netting (local guidance applies).
