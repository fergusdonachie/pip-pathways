Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Don’t miss sepsis / complicated pyelonephritis: NICE NG111 says refer to hospital if acute pyelonephritis has symptoms/signs of serious illness (e.g. sepsis), and consider referral if dehydrated/unable to keep fluids/meds down, pregnant, or higher risk of complications (structural/functional abnormality, diabetes, immunosuppression).
  • Recurrent UTI isn’t just “more antibiotics”: NICE NG112 recommends referral / specialist advice for men (male GU system), recurrent upper UTI, recurrent lower UTI where cause is unknown, pregnancy, and suspected cancer (per NG12).
  • Cancer safety-net branch: NICE NG12 includes non‑urgent referral for bladder cancer in people aged 60+ with recurrent or persistent unexplained UTI (and separate haematuria triggers).

1) NICE NG111: acute pyelonephritis (antimicrobial prescribing) — what changes actions

  • Send urine for culture before antibiotics (midstream sample) for adults (and under‑16s per paeds UTI guideline).
  • Safety net / reassess: symptoms should start improving within 48 hours of antibiotics; reassess if worsening at any time or not improving by 48h.
  • Refer to hospital if any symptoms/signs suggest a more serious illness/condition (example given: sepsis).
  • Consider referral / specialist advice if significantly dehydrated / unable to take oral fluids/medicines, pregnant, or higher‑risk for complications (structural/functional abnormality, diabetes, immunosuppression).

2) NICE NG112: recurrent UTI (prevention) — who needs specialist input

  • Defines recurrent UTI as including lower UTI and upper UTI (acute pyelonephritis).
  • Refer / seek specialist advice for further investigation and management in:
    • men (and trans women / non-binary people with a male GU system) ≥16
    • recurrent upper UTI
    • recurrent lower UTI when underlying cause is unknown
    • pregnancy
    • suspected cancer (follow NG12)
    • anyone with gender reassignment surgery with structural alteration of the urethra

3) NICE NG12: recurrent unexplained UTI in older adults (bladder cancer trigger)

  • Consider non‑urgent referral for bladder cancer in people aged 60+ with recurrent or persistent unexplained UTI.
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • Confirm the D&G acute route for suspected pyelonephritis needing same‑day assessment: ED vs SDEC/ambulatory vs on‑call medicine/urology (and whether direct admission is ever appropriate).
  • Confirm local criteria for “complicated UTI” that should be discussed with urology (e.g., known stone disease, urinary retention/obstruction, catheter-associated, post‑urological procedure).
  • Confirm whether primary care should request any baseline tests before urgent referral (e.g., U&E/creatinine, CRP, blood cultures) vs leaving all tests to the acute pathway.
  • Confirm preferred SCI Gateway destinations/service names for: urology (recurrent UTI in men/structural cause) and urology suspected cancer (if using local variants of NG12 criteria).
This evidence summary is written for pathway design: it highlights only decision‑changing points and where local policy choices sit.

Recurrent UTI / pyelonephritis red flags

Background

  • Recurrent UTI includes recurrent lower UTI and recurrent upper UTI (acute pyelonephritis).
  • Two things not to miss: (1) sepsis/complicated pyelonephritis and (2) an underlying cause (structural/functional problem, stone/obstruction, cancer red flags).
  • NICE NG111: acute pyelonephritis with serious illness (e.g. sepsis) → hospital assessment; consider referral if dehydrated/unable to take oral fluids/meds, pregnant, or higher risk of complications.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Differentiate lower UTI vs pyelonephritis:
    • Lower UTI: dysuria, frequency/urgency, suprapubic discomfort; usually no systemic features.
    • Pyelonephritis: fever/rigors, flank/loin pain or CVA tenderness, nausea/vomiting, systemic unwell.
  • Red flags / same‑day assessment:
    • features of sepsis or becoming systemically very unwell
    • unable to keep down oral fluids/antibiotics (vomiting) or significantly dehydrated
    • pregnancy
    • concern for obstruction/complication: known stone disease, anuria/oliguria, urinary retention, new AKI, single kidney/transplant
    • immunosuppression or significant comorbidity (e.g. diabetes) with systemic symptoms
    • severe uncontrolled pain, diagnostic uncertainty, or failure to improve
  • Recurrent UTI pattern: check organism history (relapse vs reinfection), triggers (sex/contraception), post‑coital symptoms, vaginal symptoms, and any urinary symptoms between infections.
  • Tests:
    • Urine dip may support diagnosis but do MSU for culture before antibiotics in suspected pyelonephritis (NICE NG111).
    • If recurrent UTI: send MSU during symptomatic episodes (ideally before antibiotics) to confirm infection/organism.
    • If unwell / red flags: record observations; consider bloods (FBC, U&E/creatinine ± CRP) if this does not delay urgent referral (local process).

Primary care management

  • If suspected pyelonephritis and stable for community treatment:
    • Send MSU for culture before antibiotics (NICE NG111).
    • Start oral antibiotics per local antimicrobial guidance.
    • Safety‑net: symptoms should start improving within 48 hours; seek help sooner if worsening or systemically unwell (NICE NG111).
  • Recurrent UTI prevention (overview):
    • Start with behavioural/self‑care measures; consider vaginal oestrogen in peri/post‑menopause if appropriate (NICE NG112).
    • If needed: targeted single‑dose prophylaxis for identifiable triggers or methenamine hippurate / daily antibiotic prophylaxis per NG112 (often needs shared decision making and sometimes specialist advice).

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency and the correct local route. Do not delay acute referral for extra tests.

Same‑day assessment (ED / acute pathway / SDEC)

  • Suspected acute pyelonephritis with any red flags:
    • symptoms/signs of serious illness (e.g. sepsis) — refer to hospital (NICE NG111)
    • significant dehydration or unable to take oral fluids/medicines (NICE NG111)
    • pregnancy (NICE NG111)
    • higher risk of complications (structural/functional abnormality, diabetes, immunosuppression) with systemic symptoms (NICE NG111)
    • concern for obstruction/AKI/single kidney/transplant
  • Route (draft): ED / SDEC / acute medical assessment (confirm local route).

Urology / specialist advice (recurrent UTI workup)

  • Refer / seek specialist advice for recurrent UTI in (NICE NG112):
    • men (male GU system) ≥16
    • recurrent upper UTI (pyelonephritis)
    • recurrent lower UTI when the underlying cause is unknown
    • pregnancy
    • after gender reassignment surgery with structural alteration of the urethra
  • Include: MSU culture history (organisms/resistance), antibiotic courses, triggers, any imaging, and relevant comorbidities.

Cancer safety‑net branch (older adults)

  • Age 60+ with recurrent/persistent unexplained UTI → consider non‑urgent referral for bladder cancer (NICE NG12).
  • If visible haematuria triggers are present, follow the haematuria pathway (separate DGRefHelp page).

Who not to refer

  • Uncomplicated lower UTI responding to treatment, without red flags or concerning pattern.
  • Recurrent UTI with an established prevention plan and good response (review in primary care; re-refer if pattern changes).
