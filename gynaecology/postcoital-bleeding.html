Key design points
  • PCB is common; many cases have no identifiable cause and may resolve spontaneously, but the GP priority is to avoid missing cervical/vaginal/vulval cancer or significant cervicitis/PID.
  • If the appearance of the cervix is suspicious for cervical cancer, guidance consistently supports urgent suspected cancer / urgent colposcopy referral. A smear is not required before referral, and a previous negative smear should not delay referral.
  • The NHS Cervical Screening Programme emphasises: people with symptoms consistent with cervical cancer (e.g. PCB or persistent unexplained discharge) are not suitable candidates for screening; after excluding common causes (infection/contraception), they should be referred for specialist assessment.
  • For a normal-looking cervix and no vulval/vaginal lesion, several UK pathways advise: test/treat infection risk (esp. chlamydia/gonorrhoea), review hormonal contraception, treat ectropion if persistent and appropriate; if symptoms persist and are troublesome, refer routinely for gynaecology assessment (and sometimes pelvic ultrasound first, depending on local pathways).
  • Age-threshold urgency varies between pathways (some recommend urgent secondary care review for persistent PCB in >40); local policy should decide the default urgent vs routine threshold when exam is normal.
Local (D&G): routing confirmed
  • Normal exam + persistent/troublesome PCB → refer to gynaecology (general) for triage (not direct symptomatic colposcopy).
  • TVUS → decision/request handled via gynae triage (not expected as a GP pre-referral requirement).
  • Likely cervicitis/STI → default route is sexual health.

Comparator pathways used for the draft

  • NHS Cervical Screening Programme colposcopy guidance (2025 update): symptoms (incl PCB) are not an indication for screening; refer for specialist assessment when cancer suspected; urgent colposcopy if suspicious cervix/symptoms consistent with cervical cancer.
  • Macmillan (NG12 summary): urgent referral within 2 weeks if cervix appearance is consistent with cervical cancer; smear not required and should not delay referral.
  • Devon Formulary (South & West) PCB guidance: suspicious cervix → USCP/colposcopy; otherwise treat causes (infection/ectropion/foreign body/polyp) and refer routinely if persistent/troublesome; suggests TVUS for normal exam but persistent symptoms (local pathway).
  • Right Decisions (NHS Scotland) PCB guidance: urgent referral for suspicious cervix; different age thresholds/targets reflect Scottish service model (useful as a comparator, not necessarily transferable).

Background

  • Postcoital bleeding (PCB) is bleeding after sex.
  • Common causes include cervicitis/STIs, ectropion, polyps, hormonal contraception, vaginal atrophy/trauma, and (less commonly) malignancy.
  • Key priority: identify a suspicious cervix/vaginal or vulval lesion and refer urgently via suspected cancer pathway.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: timing/frequency/persistence; intermenstrual or postmenopausal bleeding; discharge; pelvic pain; dyspareunia; contraception/HRT; pregnancy risk; anticoagulation; STI risk; cervical screening history (up to date?).
  • Examination: speculum (cervix + vagina) and external vulva. Document whether appearance is benign vs suspicious (ulceration, induration, friability, fungating mass).
  • Tests: pregnancy test if relevant; chlamydia/gonorrhoea NAATs if risk; consider BV/trich/candida as appropriate; smear if due and feasible (do not delay urgent referral if cervix suspicious).
  • If bleeding is heavy or prolonged: consider FBC (anaemia) and other AUB work-up as clinically indicated.

Primary care management

  • If suspicious cervix/vaginal/vulval lesion: do not delay—refer via urgent suspected cancer pathway (smear not required before referral).
  • Treat identifiable causes: manage cervicitis/STIs per local guidance; review/change hormonal contraception if relevant; manage ectropion/atrophy/trauma; remove foreign body if trained/equipped.
  • If cervix/vagina/vulva appear normal and tests are negative: reassure that many cases settle; safety-net and arrange review if persistent/troublesome.

Who to refer

Key principle: the speculum findings drive triage. Make the cervix/vagina/vulva description explicit; include infection testing/treatment already done.

Urgent suspected cancer

  • Cervix appearance consistent with cervical cancer, or suspicious vaginal/vulval lesion (e.g., ulcerated/indurated/fungating/friable mass).
  • Do not delay urgent referral for a smear; a previous negative smear should not delay urgent referral if the cervix is suspicious.

Routine gynaecology

  • Persistent/troublesome PCB despite management and a normal (non-suspicious) examination.
  • Cervical polyp or ectropion needing secondary care treatment.
  • Recurrent symptoms with no clear cause.
  • Route (D&G): SCI Gateway → DGRI → Gynaecology → General (Routine) for triage.
  • TVUS pre-referral: not expected as a GP pre-referral requirement; gynae triage to request if needed.
  • D&G note: no direct “symptomatic colposcopy” route for PCB with a normal (non-suspicious) exam; reserve urgent suspected cancer / urgent colposcopy for a suspicious cervix or high-concern symptom pattern.
  • Local routing locked in (2026‑02‑15). Checklist retained for record: PCB — local operational decisions (D&G).

Referral letter: required information (triage metrics)

  • Symptoms: PCB frequency/duration; any intermenstrual bleeding; any postmenopausal bleeding; discharge; pelvic pain.
  • Examination: cervix appearance (normal vs suspicious); contact bleeding; ectropion; visible polyp; cervicitis/discharge; vaginal/vulval findings.
  • Cervical screening: last test date/result; whether up to date (note: negative result does not exclude cancer if cervix suspicious).
  • Pregnancy risk/contraception/HRT; anticoagulation.
  • Swab results and treatments given (CT/GC NAAT, BV/trich/candida if tested).

Who not to refer

  • Single episode PCB with a normal (non-suspicious) examination and an identifiable benign cause that resolves with treatment, with appropriate safety-netting.
