DGRefHelp drafts / Recurrent miscarriage

Assessment and referral criteria for recurrent pregnancy loss (3+ miscarriages, or 2+ with risk factors).
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑17

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key points for a GP-facing pathway page
  • Definition (RCOG 2023): Recurrent miscarriage is defined as 3 or more confirmed first-trimester pregnancy losses (non-consecutive counts, but traditionally 3 consecutive for referral).
  • When to refer: Standard threshold is after 3 miscarriages. However, clinicians may refer after 2 miscarriages if there is clinical suspicion of pathology (e.g., age ≥38–40, late miscarriages, or known risk factors).
  • Second trimester loss: One or more second-trimester miscarriages (12–24 weeks) warrants investigation (often via preterm birth/obstetric clinic rather than fertility).
  • Investigations: Main yield is from Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS) testing, Thyroid function, and sometimes Genetic Karyotyping (products of conception or parental). Progesterone support may be offered in future pregnancies if there is a history of bleeding and prior miscarriage.

Primary care role

  • Supportive care: Acknowledge the psychological impact. Signpost to support charities (e.g., Tommy's, Miscarriage Association).
  • Optimisation: BMI (target 19–30), smoking cessation, limit alcohol/caffeine.
  • Pre-conception: Folic acid (400mcg or 5mg if high BMI/diabetes); Vitamin D.
  • Referral: To Recurrent Miscarriage Clinic (RMC) or Gynaecology, depending on local setup.
Local (D&G)
  • Confirm exact referral threshold (strict 3 vs 2+).
  • Confirm where APS testing is done (GP vs Specialist Clinic) — often best left to clinic to ensure timing is correct (not during pregnancy or acute thrombosis).
  • Confirm destination: "Recurrent Miscarriage Clinic" vs General Gynae.

Recurrent miscarriage

Background

  • Recurrent miscarriage is defined by RCOG (2023) as 3 or more first-trimester pregnancy losses. (Traditionally defined as consecutive, but guidelines now recognize non-consecutive patterns; referral thresholds usually prioritize 3 losses).
  • Risk increases with maternal age and number of previous losses.
  • Modifiable risk factors: maternal age (delay), BMI (obesity/underweight), smoking, alcohol, and uncontrolled thyroid/diabetes.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: dates/gestations of all pregnancies; histology/karyotype if known; partner details; medical history (thrombosis, SLE, thyroid, diabetes).
  • Examination: BMI; BP; pelvic exam only if indicated (e.g. by concurrent symptoms).
  • Initial Tests: FBC, TFTs, HbA1c (if risk factors).
  • Note: Antiphospholipid (APS) screening is usually coordinated by the specialist clinic (must be >6 weeks post-pregnancy and not pregnant).

Primary care management

  • Optimise health: aim for BMI 19–30; stop smoking; limit alcohol/caffeine.
  • Supplements: Folic acid 400mcg (5mg if BMI >30/diabetes) and Vitamin D 10mcg daily.
  • Support: acknowledge distress; signpost to charities (e.g., Tommy's, Miscarriage Association).
  • Next pregnancy: usually offered early reassurance scan (6–8 weeks) via Early Pregnancy Unit.

Who to refer

  • Recurrent first-trimester miscarriage: women with 3 or more confirmed miscarriages.
  • Consider after 2 miscarriages if: age ≥38–40 (check local criteria) or significant clinical concern (e.g. known autoimmune, history of infertility).
  • Second-trimester loss: women with one or more mid-trimester losses (12–24 weeks) — refer for investigation (often Obstetric/Preterm pathway).

Referral letter: required information

  • Dates and gestations of all losses.
  • Any histology/karyotype results.
  • BMI, smoking, medical history.

Who not to refer

  • Women with fewer than 3 miscarriages (unless ≥38y or specific risk factors).
  • Biochemical pregnancies (very early loss) may not be counted for clinic criteria (confirm locally).
