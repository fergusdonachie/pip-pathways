PID: recognise (low threshold), exclude pregnancy/surgical emergency, treat empirically, arrange 72h review, and know when to admit / refer.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑09

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • Clinical diagnosis is imprecise; guidance supports a low threshold for empiric treatment once pregnancy is excluded, because delaying therapy increases risk of infertility, ectopic pregnancy and chronic pelvic pain.
  • Typical symptoms/signs: recent onset bilateral lower abdominal pain + cervical motion tenderness/adnexal tenderness ± abnormal bleeding, discharge, dyspareunia, fever.
  • Test for chlamydia + gonorrhoea (and ideally M. genitalium if available) but negative tests do not exclude PID.
  • Baseline actions in primary care: pregnancy test, assess for surgical emergency, start antibiotics, advise no unprotected sex until completion, and arrange review at 72 hours (earlier if worsening).
  • Consider admission/IV antibiotics for severe disease (e.g. fever >38°C, peritonism, suspected tubo-ovarian abscess), pregnancy, diagnostic uncertainty (cannot exclude appendicitis/ectopic/ovarian torsion), or no improvement after oral therapy.
  • Partner management matters: current partner(s) should be contacted for testing, and many pathways recommend empiric cover for male partners (e.g. doxycycline) alongside sexual health follow-up.

Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) (primary care)

Background

  • PID = ascending infection from cervix → endometritis/salpingitis ± tubo-ovarian abscess/peritonitis.
  • Common pathogens: chlamydia, gonorrhoea; mixed vaginal flora/anaerobes; M. genitalium can be a cause.
  • Key principle: if suspicion is reasonable and pregnancy excluded, treat empirically (do not wait for swab results).

Red flags / same-day assessment

  • Possible ectopic pregnancy (missed period/positive test, unilateral pain, collapse, shoulder tip pain) → urgent assessment.
  • Surgical abdomen / diagnostic uncertainty (appendicitis, ovarian torsion/rupture) → ED / surgical & gynae assessment.
  • Severe clinical disease: fever >38°C, peritonism, severe pain/vomiting, systemic unwell, suspected pelvic peritonitis.
  • Suspected tubo-ovarian abscess (marked tenderness, pelvic mass, very unwell) → admit/urgent imaging.
  • Pregnancy (PID uncommon but higher maternal/fetal risk) → same-day specialist assessment; parenteral therapy usually advised.
  • Unable to tolerate oral antibiotics or concern re adherence/safeguarding → consider admission.

Assessment (primary care)

  • History: onset/duration; bilateral lower abdominal pain; dyspareunia; abnormal bleeding (IMB/PCB/menorrhagia); discharge; fever; vomiting; pregnancy risk; IUD insertion in last 4–6 weeks; STI risk.
  • Examination: abdominal tenderness/peritonism; speculum (discharge, cervicitis); bimanual (cervical motion tenderness/adnexal tenderness); consider pelvic mass.
  • Tests:
    • Pregnancy test (do first).
    • NAAT for chlamydia/gonorrhoea; add M. genitalium if available locally.
    • Consider HIV/syphilis testing as per sexual health pathways.
    • CRP/ESR may support diagnosis but are non-specific; use clinically.

Primary care management (uncomplicated / mild–moderate)

  • Start antibiotics immediately once pregnancy excluded and suspicion is reasonable.
  • Analgesia; rest if severe symptoms.
  • Sex: avoid unprotected sex until patient and partner(s) have completed treatment and follow-up.
  • Safety-net: return urgently if worsening pain, fever, vomiting, faint/collapse, new peritonism.

Suggested antibiotic regimen (check local antimicrobial guidance)

  • First-line outpatient (BASHH): IM ceftriaxone 1 g stat plus doxycycline 100 mg BD 14 days plus metronidazole 400 mg BD 14 days.
  • Alternatives exist (including quinolones/moxifloxacin) but are usually second line due to resistance and serious adverse-effect warnings; use local guidance / sexual health advice.
  • If M. genitalium positive, BASHH recommends moxifloxacin (specialist/sexual health input advised).

Review and follow-up

  • Review at 72 hours (earlier if moderate/severe symptoms). If no improvement → consider admission, imaging, and/or alternative diagnosis; switch to parenteral therapy where appropriate.
  • Consider review at 2–4 weeks to confirm symptom resolution, adherence, partner management completed, and to re-test if indicated.

Who to refer / admit

  • Same day / admit: severe disease, pregnancy, suspected tubo-ovarian abscess, cannot exclude surgical emergency, inability to tolerate oral therapy, or no improvement at 72 hours.
  • Sexual health/GUM: for partner notification, STI testing/treatment, and where available M. genitalium testing and management.
  • Routine gynae: persistent pelvic symptoms after treated PID where alternative pathology suspected (e.g. endometriosis, chronic pelvic pain) — local pathway decision.

Who not to refer (usually)

  • Clinically improving uncomplicated PID managed as outpatient, with arranged 72h review and sexual health follow-up/partner management.

Referral letter / triage metrics (if referring)

  • Pregnancy test result and LMP.
  • Symptoms: onset, severity, fever/vomiting, bleeding/discharge, dyspareunia.
  • Exam: peritonism? cervical motion tenderness? adnexal tenderness/mass?
  • Swabs: chlamydia/gonorrhoea (and M. genitalium if done) — date + results if available.
  • Treatment started: antibiotics + start date/time; response at 72h if known.
  • IUD in situ? inserted within last 4–6 weeks?
