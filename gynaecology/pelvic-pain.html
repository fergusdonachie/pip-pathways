Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design decisions for the DGRefHelp page
  • Start by separating acute pelvic pain (same-day/surgical/ectopic risk) from chronic pelvic pain.
  • Always consider pregnancy (test if any possibility), PID, and ovarian torsion/rupture in acute presentations.
  • For suspected endometriosis: NICE NG73 emphasises recognising symptom patterns, offering initial management, and referring when needed; a normal exam/scan does not exclude endometriosis.
  • For suspected PID: BASHH recommends a low threshold for empiric treatment; pregnancy test + STI testing; consider admission for severe disease / abscess / pregnancy / inability to exclude surgical emergency.
  • DGRefHelp pages should explicitly state what secondary care needs in the referral letter (pattern, exam, tests, red flags, what has been tried) because it affects triage and clinic destination.

NICE NG73 (endometriosis: awareness + referral principles)

  • Endometriosis can present with chronic pelvic pain, dysmenorrhoea, dyspareunia and cyclical GI/urinary symptoms; consider it even if imaging is normal.
  • Initial management in primary care typically includes analgesia and hormonal options (tailored to fertility intentions), and referral when symptoms are severe, persistent, or impacting quality of life despite initial measures.

BASHH PID guideline (2019): threshold to treat + when to admit

  • Symptoms/signs are not specific; maintain a low threshold to treat PID empirically when clinical features fit and pregnancy is excluded.
  • Admission/IV therapy should be considered for severe disease (e.g., fever >38°C, pelvic peritonitis, suspected tubo-ovarian abscess), pregnancy, inability to exclude surgical emergency, or no response to oral therapy.
  • Testing for chlamydia/gonorrhoea and (where available) M. genitalium is recommended; absence of infection does not exclude PID.
Local variation to highlight (D&G)
  • Confirm local access model for pelvic ultrasound (direct access vs secondary care ordering) and expected timing for suspected endometriosis/chronic pelvic pain.
  • Confirm whether D&G has direct-to pathways (e.g., endometriosis clinic, chronic pelvic pain clinic, hysteroscopy clinic) and what referral metrics determine triage.

Pelvic pain

Background

  • Pelvic pain can be acute (hours–days) or chronic (≥3–6 months). Start by ruling out urgent causes.
  • Always consider pregnancy/ectopic risk, PID, ovarian torsion/rupture, appendicitis and urinary causes in acute pain.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: onset + severity; unilateral vs bilateral; cyclical relation to periods; dysmenorrhoea; dyspareunia; bowel/urinary symptoms (esp cyclical); discharge; fever; bleeding (IMB/PCB/PMB); contraception; pregnancy risk; STI risk; past endometriosis/PID/surgery.
  • Examination: abdo exam; pelvic exam where appropriate (cervical motion tenderness, adnexal tenderness/mass, discharge, cervical lesion).
  • Tests: pregnancy test if any possibility; urinalysis ± culture; STI NAATs if indicated; consider FBC/CRP if infection suspected.
  • Consider imaging: pelvic USS if pelvic mass suspected, severe persistent symptoms, or to assess fibroids/ovarian pathology (per local access model).

Primary care management

  • Acute severe pain / red flags: urgent assessment (same day).
  • Suspected PID: low threshold for empiric antibiotics once pregnancy excluded; take STI tests; safety-net and review (often 72h for moderate/severe).
  • Suspected endometriosis/chronic pelvic pain: start analgesia + consider hormonal suppression if appropriate (aligned with fertility wishes); document impact and prior treatments.

Who to refer

Key principle: referral letter content affects triage (gynae vs sexual health vs surgical vs urology; and which clinic). Include the metrics needed.

  • Same-day / emergency: suspected ectopic pregnancy; suspected torsion/ruptured cyst; peritonism; sepsis; uncontrolled pain; haemodynamic compromise.
  • Urgent gynae: suspected pelvic mass; persistent severe symptoms; concerning bleeding pattern; suspected abscess; failure to improve after initial PID treatment.
  • Routine gynae: suspected endometriosis/chronic pelvic pain impacting quality of life despite initial measures; recurrent symptoms; dyspareunia; cyclical GI/urinary symptoms suggestive of endometriosis.

Referral letter: required information (triage metrics)

  • Pain pattern: acute vs chronic; cyclical vs non-cyclical; unilateral/bilateral; severity; impact on work/sleep/sex.
  • Associated symptoms: dysmenorrhoea; dyspareunia; bowel/urinary symptoms (esp cyclical); bleeding pattern; discharge; fever.
  • Pregnancy risk + pregnancy test result.
  • Exam findings (including speculum findings, cervical motion/adnexal tenderness, masses).
  • Investigations done and results: urinalysis/culture; STI NAATs; FBC/CRP if done; ultrasound report if performed.
  • Treatments tried: analgesia, hormonal options, PID antibiotics and response; contraception/fertility wishes.
  • Past history: endometriosis, PID, abdominal/pelvic surgery, IUD insertion in last 4–6 weeks (PID risk window).

Who not to refer

  • Mild symptoms with clear benign explanation and good response to initial treatment may be managed in primary care with safety-netting and review.
