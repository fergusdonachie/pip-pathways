DGRefHelp drafts / Vulval dermatoses

Management of Lichen Sclerosus (LS), Lichen Planus (LP), and other vulval skin conditions. Includes when to refer for biopsy.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑17

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • Lichen Sclerosus (LS): Common, chronic inflammatory condition. Hallmark is itch + white plaques/atrophy ("cigarette paper" skin). Can cause scarring/resorption. Small risk of SCC (<5%).
  • Management of LS: Potent topical steroid (Clobetasol propionate 0.05% ointment) is the gold standard for induction and maintenance. Ointments are preferred over creams (less stinging, better barrier).
  • Lichen Planus (LP): Less common, often involves vagina (erosive LP) and mouth. Can cause scarring/adhesions. Often requires specialist management.
  • Biopsy: Required if diagnosis is uncertain, failure to respond to treatment, or suspicion of malignancy (VIN/SCC).
  • General care: Use soap substitutes (emollients), avoid irritants, wear cotton underwear.

BASHH 2014 / BAD Guidelines for Lichen Sclerosus

  • Diagnosis is often clinical but biopsy recommended if atypical or uncertain.
  • First-line Rx: Ultrapotent steroid (Clobetasol propionate 0.05%) daily for 1-3 months then taper to maintenance (e.g., 1-2 times/week).
  • Long-term follow-up: Historically lifelong specialist follow-up; now often discharged to GP for annual review if stable, with rapid re-access if flare/changes.
Local (D&G)
  • Referral destination: General Gynae or Dermatology? (Often Gynae for vulval, Derm for complex/multi-site). Usually "Vulval Clinic" if available, or General Gynae.
  • Confirm if GPs can initiate Clobetasol for suspected LS before referral (usually yes to relieve symptoms, but biopsy may be needed for confirmation).

Vulval dermatoses (Lichen Sclerosus/Planus)

Background

  • Lichen Sclerosus (LS): Chronic inflammatory condition causing itch, soreness, and white plaques/atrophy. Can lead to scarring (loss of labia minora, burying of clitoris) and has a small risk (<5%) of squamous cell carcinoma (SCC).
  • Lichen Planus (LP): Autoimmune condition affecting skin/mucosa. "Erosive" LP affects the introitus/vagina (unlike LS) and causes pain/burning/discharge. Oral involvement is common.
  • Eczema/Dermatitis: Often due to irritants (soaps, urine) or allergy. Causes itch/redness/excoriation.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: Itch (often nocturnal in LS), pain/soreness, dyspareunia, bleeding/fissuring. Check for autoimmune history (thyroid, vitiligo). Ask about irritants (washing habits, incontinence).
  • Examination:
    • LS: Pallor (ivory white plaques), atrophy (thin "crinkled" skin), purpura/ecchymosis, fissures, loss of architecture. Vagina is never involved.
    • LP: Erythematous erosions at introitus (glazed red), vaginal synechiae/scarring, white reticulate pattern (Wickham's striae). Check mouth for oral LP.
  • Red flags: Unexplained lump, ulcer, or persistent bleeding (suspected cancer).

Primary care management

  • General measures (All):
    • Avoid soap/bubble bath/wipes. Use emollient soap substitute (e.g., Hydromol, Dermol 500) for washing.
    • Wear loose cotton underwear; avoid tight clothing.
    • Use barrier ointment (e.g., Vaseline) before swimming/exercise if irritant.
  • Suspected Lichen Sclerosus:
    • Potent topical steroid is required (do not use weak steroids).
    • Start: Clobetasol propionate 0.05% (Dermovate) ointment (not cream). Apply thinly OD (night) for 1 month, then alternate days for 1 month, then twice weekly.
    • Review at 3 months: Symptom control? Architecture stable? If not improving, REFER.
    • Note: Lifelong maintenance (1-2x/week) is often needed to prevent scarring/cancer risk.
  • Eczema/Dermatitis: Moderate potent steroid (e.g., Betnovate / mometasone) ointment daily for 7-14 days + regular emollients.

Who to refer

Include description of lesions and treatment response.

  • Urgent Suspicion of Cancer: Unexplained vulval lump, ulceration, or bleeding.
  • Routine Referral (Gynae/Derm):
    • Diagnostic uncertainty (biopsy required).
    • Suspected Lichen Planus (vaginal involvement requires specialist care).
    • Failure to respond to potent steroid treatment (compliance check first).
    • Progressive scarring/adhesions despite treatment.
    • Children with vulval symptoms (paediatric referral).

Referral letter: required information

  • Symptoms and duration.
  • Description of lesions (white plaques, erosions, scarring, etc.).
  • Treatments tried (name, strength, formulation, duration) and response.
  • Any incontinence or irritant exposure.

Who not to refer

  • Stable Lichen Sclerosus on maintenance treatment with no active symptoms or architectural change (can be managed in primary care with annual review).
  • Mild irritant dermatitis that resolves with emollients/soap avoidance.
