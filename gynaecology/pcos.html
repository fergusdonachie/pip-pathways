DGRefHelp drafts / PCOS (polycystic ovary syndrome)

Primary care: diagnose (and exclude mimics), manage common symptoms, and know when to refer. Includes triage/referral metrics and safety-net thresholds.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑09

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key points for a GP-facing pathway page
  • Diagnosis in adults is usually based on Rotterdam criteria (2 of 3): oligo/anovulation; clinical/biochemical hyperandrogenism; polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound — after excluding other causes.
  • PCOS is often a metabolic risk state: screen/monitor for type 2 diabetes risk, cardiovascular risk factors and obesity; address sleep apnoea and mental health where relevant.
  • Symptom-driven management is typical in primary care: cycle control/endometrial protection, hirsutism/acne, and fertility goals.
  • Red flags for alternative diagnoses: rapid virilisation/rapidly progressive hirsutism, or markedly raised androgens → urgent specialist assessment (often endocrinology).

Diagnosis (adults) — practical summary

  • Rotterdam criteria (need 2 of 3): (1) polycystic ovaries on scan (e.g., ≥12 follicles or ovarian volume >10 cm³ — definitions vary by scan technology), (2) oligo/anovulation, (3) clinical and/or biochemical hyperandrogenism.
  • Exclude mimics (depending on presentation): thyroid disease, hyperprolactinaemia, premature ovarian insufficiency, congenital adrenal hyperplasia, Cushing’s, androgen-secreting tumour.
  • Adolescents: diagnostic criteria differ; avoid over-diagnosis soon after menarche. If needed, a pragmatic rule used in pathways is: wait >2 years after menarche and require BOTH hyperandrogenism and persistent irregular cycles (ultrasound morphology is less helpful).

Typical baseline investigations (primary care / community gynae pathways)

  • Androgen profile where indicated: total testosterone, SHBG, calculate free androgen index (FAI).
  • Rule-out tests commonly used: TSH, prolactin, FSH (± LH/oestradiol depending on local pathway/presentation).
  • If testosterone markedly high: further adrenal/androgen workup (e.g., DHEA-S, androstenedione, 17‑hydroxyprogesterone) and consider urgent endocrinology.

Long-term risk / screening (common themes)

  • Diabetes risk: consider regular HbA1c screening (some pathways advise annual HbA1c).
  • Cardiovascular risk factors: manage BP, lipids and weight; consider QRISK assessment where appropriate.
  • Endometrium: prolonged oligomenorrhoea/amenorrhoea increases endometrial hyperplasia risk — aim for ≥4 withdrawal bleeds/periods per year using CHC/progestogens/LNG‑IUS as appropriate.
  • Pregnancy: higher gestational diabetes risk; ensure appropriate screening in pregnancy.
  • Wellbeing: consider mood, eating disorder risk, body image and psychosexual impact.
Local (D&G)
  • Default routing not yet confirmed for: suspected PCOS diagnosis queries, menstrual symptoms needing specialist input, and ovulation induction/fertility support.
  • If uncertain, refer to General Gynae clinic; consider endocrine referral if androgen red flags.

PCOS (polycystic ovary syndrome)

Background

  • PCOS is associated with irregular or absent ovulation, hyperandrogenic symptoms (e.g., hirsutism/acne) and increased metabolic risk.
  • Adult diagnosis is usually based on Rotterdam criteria (need 2 of 3) after excluding other causes: oligo/anovulation; clinical/biochemical hyperandrogenism; polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: cycle pattern (frequency/duration; IMB); acne/hirsutism onset and progression; fertility goals; weight change; sleep apnoea symptoms; mood/eating disorder risk; family history of diabetes/CVD; meds (androgens/anabolics).
  • Examination: BMI, BP; hyperandrogenism features; look for virilisation / rapid progression.
  • Baseline tests (typical): total testosterone; SHBG (calculate FAI); TSH; prolactin; FSH (± LH/oestradiol depending on pathway). Consider pregnancy test if amenorrhoea; consider HbA1c for metabolic risk.
  • Exclude other causes depending on presentation: thyroid disease, hyperprolactinaemia, premature ovarian insufficiency, CAH, Cushing’s, androgen-secreting tumour.

Primary care management

  • Lifestyle/metabolic: weight management support; address BP/lipids; consider periodic HbA1c screening (some pathways: annual).
  • Endometrial protection (oligo/amenorrhoea): aim for ≥4 bleeds/year with CHC (if eligible), cyclical progestogen (e.g., medroxyprogesterone 10 mg OD 14 days every 2–3 months), or LNG‑IUS.
  • Hirsutism: discuss hair removal; consider CHC options (some pathways suggest co‑cyprindiol or drospirenone-containing CHC if appropriate); consider eflornithine (Vaniqa) for facial hirsutism (trial ~4 months).
  • Acne: manage per acne guidance; CHC may help; refer dermatology if severe or refractory.
  • Metformin: consider particularly if higher metabolic risk (e.g., BMI >30 kg/m² or diabetes risk factors) if used locally; titrate (GI side effects).
  • Fertility goals: optimise weight/comorbidities; follow local infertility referral criteria; consider early referral if prolonged anovulation or other infertility risks.

Who to refer

  • Endocrinology (consider urgent): virilisation or rapidly progressive hirsutism; testosterone >5 nmol/L or >2× upper limit of normal; abnormal adrenal androgen profile (suspect CAH/Cushing’s/adrenal/ovarian tumour).
  • Gynaecology: persistent abnormal bleeding; prolonged amenorrhoea with abnormal endometrium/endometrial thickness; diagnostic uncertainty; symptoms not controlled with primary care management; fertility/ovulation induction as per local pathway.
  • Dermatology: acne not responding to standard management or requiring specialist therapy.

Referral letter: suggested information (triage metrics)

  • Symptoms + duration; cycle pattern; severity of hirsutism/acne; fertility goals; abnormal bleeding.
  • BMI, BP; comorbidities; smoking; current contraception/medications; prior treatments tried.
  • Key results (with units/ranges): testosterone, SHBG, FAI; TSH; prolactin; FSH (± LH/oestradiol); HbA1c if done.
  • Ultrasound results (if done), and endometrial thickness if abnormal bleeding/prolonged amenorrhoea.
  • Red flags present/absent: virilisation; rapid progression.

Who not to refer

  • Typical symptoms responding to primary care management with no red flags.
  • Do not refer for “polycystic ovaries on ultrasound” alone without supportive symptoms/biochemistry (confirm local criteria).
