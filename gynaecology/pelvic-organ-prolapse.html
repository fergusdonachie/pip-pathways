Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key points for a GP-facing pathway page
  • Definition: Symptomatic descent of one or more pelvic organs (uterus, bladder, bowel) into the vagina.
  • Conservative first: NICE NG123 emphasizes supervised pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT) for at least 3–4 months for symptomatic mild/moderate prolapse.
  • Pessaries: Effective non-surgical option. Can be managed in primary care or community clinics.
  • Surgery: Reserved for those who fail conservative measures, have severe (procidentia) prolapse, or decline pessaries.

NICE NG123 (Urinary incontinence and pelvic organ prolapse)

  • Assess for red flags (pelvic masses, postmenopausal bleeding).
  • Offer supervised PFMT (protocol of at least 16 weeks) as first-line for symptomatic stage 1–2 prolapse.
  • Consider vaginal oestrogen for signs of vaginal atrophy.
  • Discuss pessaries as an alternative to surgery or while waiting.

Pelvic Organ Prolapse

Background

  • Pelvic organ prolapse (POP) is the symptomatic descent of pelvic organs into the vagina. It is common (affecting ~1 in 10 women over 50).
  • Symptoms include a dragging sensation, feeling of a lump/bulge, and associated urinary or bowel symptoms.

Red Flags (Urgent/2WW)

  • Suspected cancer (2WW): Unexplained palpable mass in or at the entrance to the vagina (distinct from simple prolapse), pelvic mass, ascites, or postmenopausal bleeding.
  • Urgent: Urinary retention or ureteric obstruction (renal failure) due to severe prolapse.

Assessment (primary care)

  • History: sensation of bulge/lump, dragging/heaviness, urinary symptoms (incontinence, hesitancy, recurrent UTIs), bowel symptoms (obstructed defecation), sexual dysfunction.
  • Examination:
    • Perform SIMS speculum exam (left lateral) to assess compartment (anterior/cystocele, posterior/rectocele, apical/uterine).
    • Ask patient to cough/bear down to assess extent of descent.
    • Check for vaginal atrophy or ulceration.
    • Exclude pelvic masses (bimanual exam).

Primary care management

  • Lifestyle: Weight loss (if BMI >30), smoking cessation, treat constipation, avoid heavy lifting/high-impact exercise.
  • Pelvic Floor Muscle Training (PFMT): First-line for symptomatic mild/moderate prolapse. Refer to pelvic health physiotherapy or encourage supervised programme (min. 16 weeks).
  • Topical Oestrogen: Prescribe if vaginal atrophy is present (unless contraindicated).
  • Pessaries: Consider fitting a ring pessary if skills available, or refer to community pessary clinic. Review every 6 months.

Who to refer

  • Routine Gynaecology:
    • Symptomatic prolapse failing conservative management (PFMT/pessary).
    • Stage 3 or 4 prolapse (procidentia) where conservative management is unsuitable or declined.
    • Pessary complications (e.g., erosion, pain) or failure to retain pessary.
    • Patient preference for surgery after counselling on risks/benefits.
  • Urogynaecology (if specialized): Complex cases, previous repair failure, or significant urinary incontinence co-morbidity.
