Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key points (NICE NG23)
  • In otherwise healthy people aged ≥45 with typical symptoms, diagnose perimenopause/menopause clinically (do not use routine labs).
  • FSH testing is generally only for age 40–45 with symptoms (incl cycle change), early menopause (age 40–44), or premature ovarian insufficiency (POI) (<40).
  • HRT is first-line for bothersome vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes/night sweats) where no contraindication.
  • Menopause-specific CBT is an option for vasomotor symptoms (adjunct to HRT, or when HRT is contraindicated or not wanted).
  • Genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM): vaginal oestrogen is effective; serious adverse effects are very rare; symptoms often recur on stopping, but can restart.

Recognising perimenopause/menopause (practical)

  • Perimenopause (≥45): new vasomotor symptoms + any menstrual cycle change → clinical diagnosis (no labs needed).
  • Menopause (≥45): no period ≥12 months (and not using hormonal contraception) → clinical diagnosis.
  • After hysterectomy: menopause is identified by symptom pattern (for example vasomotor symptoms).
  • It can be difficult to identify menopause in people taking hormonal treatments (for example for HMB) — treat individually.

What not to do (NICE)

  • Do not use AMH, inhibin A/B, oestradiol, antral follicle count, ovarian volume to identify menopause/perimenopause in ≥45.
  • Do not use FSH to identify menopause in people using combined hormonal contraception or high-dose progestogen.
  • Do not routinely offer SSRI/SNRI or clonidine as first-line treatment for vasomotor symptoms alone.

First-line management themes (primary care)

  • Shared decision making about symptom priorities, HRT benefits/risks, and alternatives; review and adjust.
  • Contraception: perimenopause ≠ no fertility. Ensure contraception advice (FSRH over-40 guidance).
  • Bone and muscle health: discuss bone health; encourage physical activity/strength maintenance.
  • Bleeding on HRT: manage via the dedicated pathway (triage thresholds differ by regimen/risk). See: Unscheduled bleeding on HRT.

DGRefHelp draft (D&G) — Menopause

Background

  • Menopause/perimenopause commonly occurs in mid-life, but can occur earlier (including after surgery/medical treatment).
  • Symptoms vary in type and duration: cycle change, vasomotor symptoms, mood change, musculoskeletal symptoms, sexual difficulties, and genitourinary symptoms (vaginal dryness/urinary symptoms).
  • Priority is symptom control, health promotion (bone/muscle, cardiovascular risk factors), contraception advice, and identifying people who need specialist input (early menopause/POI, complex comorbidity, malignancy red flags).

Assessment (primary care)

  • Symptoms: vasomotor (frequency/severity), sleep, mood, cognitive symptoms, joint/muscle pain, sexual function, GSM symptoms (dryness, dyspareunia, recurrent UTI-type symptoms).
  • Menstrual/bleeding pattern: changes in cycle length/regularity; bleeding on HRT if applicable (see bleeding-on-HRT page).
  • Contraception: current method; pregnancy risk; whether hormonal contraception may mask bleeding pattern.
  • Risk factors / contraindications to HRT: past VTE, cardiovascular disease/stroke, active liver disease, migraine with aura (route considerations), breast cancer history, unexplained vaginal bleeding.
  • Baseline observations: BP, BMI; consider CVD risk factor review and bone health discussion.

Diagnosis — when to test

  • Age ≥45 with typical symptoms: diagnose perimenopause/menopause clinically (no routine labs).
  • FSH can be considered to confirm menopause only in:
    • age 40–45 with symptoms (incl cycle change),
    • early menopause age 40–44,
    • POI (<40; needs specialist framing).
  • Do not use FSH to diagnose menopause if on combined hormonal contraception or high-dose progestogen.

Primary care management

  • Vasomotor symptoms: offer HRT if appropriate. If HRT is contraindicated or not wanted, consider menopause-specific CBT.
  • GSM: offer vaginal oestrogen (cream/gel/tablet/pessary/ring) via shared decision making. Explain serious adverse effects are very rare; symptoms often recur when stopped; can restart if needed.
  • Do not routinely use SSRI/SNRI or clonidine as first-line for vasomotor symptoms alone.
  • Health promotion: discuss bone health; encourage physical activity/strength maintenance; address smoking/alcohol and weight where relevant; signpost to reputable resources.
  • Contraception: provide contraception advice / signpost to FSRH over‑40 guidance.

Who to refer

  • Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI) / suspected menopause <40 → specialist assessment (typically gynae/endocrine per local route).
  • Early menopause (40–44) with significant distress or complex management needs.
  • Symptoms not controlled after reasonable primary-care management trials, or complex comorbidity/contraindications where specialist input is needed.
  • Personal history of breast cancer and menopause-associated symptoms → specialist pathway (usually oncology/breast + menopause expertise; local route to agree).
  • Unexplained vaginal bleeding or bleeding on HRT that meets referral thresholds → use bleeding pathways:
    • Unscheduled bleeding on HRT
    • Postmenopausal bleeding
    • Abnormal uterine bleeding

Safety-net / when to reassess urgently

  • New, heavy, persistent, or postcoital bleeding (especially >45 or postmenopausal) → assess for malignancy risk and use the relevant bleeding pathway.
  • New unilateral breast lump, nipple change, or other concerning breast symptoms → urgent breast pathway.
  • New severe headache/neurological symptoms, chest pain, shortness of breath, unilateral leg swelling while on systemic HRT → urgent assessment for thromboembolic/cardiovascular events.
