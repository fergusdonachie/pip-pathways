DGRefHelp drafts / Vulval symptoms

Vulval itch/pain/skin change/lump (incl Bartholin’s cyst/abscess): primary care approach + red flags + what secondary care needs in the referral letter.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • Vulval pruritus/pain/skin change is common; common causes in secondary care clinics include dermatitis/eczema, candidiasis, lichen sclerosus and lichen planus.
  • Symptoms are non-specific. A standard gynae history is often insufficient; include irritants/allergens, other skin sites, meds, autoimmune history, and relevant cervical cytology/smoking/immune deficiency if VIN suspected.
  • Biopsy rule (RCOG GTG58): biopsy is required if failure to respond to treatment or suspicion of VIN/cancer; VIN is a histological diagnosis; biopsy atypical/suspicious areas.
  • Vulval lump/ulcer/bleeding: NICE NG12 includes a suspected cancer pathway referral trigger for unexplained vulval lump, ulceration or bleeding.
  • Bartholin’s cyst/abscess: most can be managed conservatively if mild; antibiotics are generally not indicated unless cellulitis/offensive discharge; Word catheter is commonly first-line for surgical management; consider biopsy/excision pathway in women >40 (risk of malignancy is rare but higher in this group).
  • DGRefHelp “Who to refer” should emphasise the referral letter content that supports triage (symptom pattern, exam, prior treatments, suspected diagnosis, lesion description/location, and whether a Bartholin mass is present).

RCOG Green-top Guideline No. 58 (2011): Vulval skin disorders

  • Sets an evidence-based framework for initial assessment and when to refer to specialist vulval services.
  • Highlights importance of examining the whole vulval/perianal region and considering systemic dermatoses (mouth/skin/scalp etc. where relevant).

Bartholin’s cyst/abscess (Right Decisions GGC primary care guideline as an example pathway)

  • Summarises pragmatic management including: conservative care for mild/no infection; antibiotics only if cellulitis/offensive discharge; Word catheter first-line procedure; marsupialisation for recurrence or preference.
  • Notes biopsy may be indicated in women over 40 years due to increased risk of Bartholin gland carcinoma (rare).
Local (D&G)
  • Referral destination: General Gynae clinic for persistent vulval symptoms / suspected dermatoses.
  • Confirm local route for suspected vulval cancer (USOC) and for Bartholin’s abscess needing Word catheter/marsupialisation.
  • Confirm whether clinical photographs are acceptable/desired and by what route (governance).

Vulval symptoms

Background

  • Vulval symptoms are common and include itch (pruritus), pain/soreness, dyspareunia and visible skin change or lumps.
  • Common causes include dermatitis/eczema, candidiasis, lichen sclerosus, lichen planus, Bartholin’s cyst/abscess and vulval intraepithelial neoplasia (VIN).
  • Key priority: identify lesions requiring biopsy / urgent assessment (possible VIN/cancer) and treat reversible causes while minimising irritants.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: symptom type (itch/pain/burning), duration, severity, nocturnal itch, triggers; discharge; dyspareunia; urinary symptoms; previous thrush treatments; irritants/allergens (soaps, wipes, pads, lubricants); other skin sites; autoimmune history; smoking; immunosuppression; cervical cytology history if VIN suspected.
  • If a lump: exact site (e.g. posterior introitus 4/8 o’clock = Bartholin region), size, tenderness, fluctuation, overlying skin change/ulceration, discharge/bleeding, duration and whether recurrent.
  • Examination: inspect vulva + perianal skin; speculum if discharge/PCB/cervix concern; consider full skin/oral exam if lichen planus/psoriasis suspected.
  • Tests: consider vaginal swab / candida testing if discharge; STI NAATs if risk; consider diabetes/thyroid testing only if clinically indicated; if Bartholin abscess is draining, consider a swab (and STI tests if risk).

Primary care management

  • Stop irritants: avoid perfumed soaps/wipes; use emollient as soap substitute; cotton underwear; avoid over-washing.
  • Treat likely infection where appropriate (e.g., candidiasis) and reassess response.
  • Bartholin’s cyst/abscess (initial):
    • Mild symptoms/no cellulitis: analgesia + warm baths/sitz baths; safety-net.
    • Antibiotics are generally not indicated unless cellulitis/offensive discharge/systemic features (follow local antimicrobial guidance).
    • Avoid repeated needle aspiration/incision and drainage in primary care if your local pathway expects Word catheter / marsupialisation (recurrence is higher with simple drainage).
  • If symptoms persist or there is a focal lesion, do not keep repeating empiric thrush treatment—escalate for specialist assessment/biopsy if indicated.

Who to refer

Include the triage metrics: lesion description + exact location + treatments tried + risk factors.

  • Suspected cancer pathway: unexplained vulval lump, ulceration or bleeding → urgent pathway per NICE NG12 + local process.
  • Routine / specialist vulval service: persistent symptoms despite initial measures; suspected lichen sclerosus/lichen planus; recurrent symptoms with visible skin change.
  • Biopsy principle (VIN/cancer): biopsy is required if failure to respond to treatment or suspicion of VIN/cancer; VIN is a histological diagnosis and atypical/suspicious areas should be biopsied.
  • Bartholin’s cyst/abscess needing procedure: severe pain/large abscess/recurrent disease → refer/arrange acute gynae assessment per local pathway (Word catheter commonly first-line; marsupialisation for recurrence).
  • Bartholin’s mass in women >40: consider referral for biopsy/excision pathway (rare malignancy risk; follow local gynae guidance).

Referral letter: required information (triage metrics)

  • Symptoms: itch/pain/burning; duration; severity; nocturnal symptoms; impact on sex/urination.
  • Lesion description: exact location (diagram/clock-face), size, colour/pigment change, ulceration, fissuring, induration, bleeding, discharge; whether focal vs diffuse.
  • If Bartholin region: cyst vs abscess features; size; tenderness; recurrence; any discharge/cellulitis; pregnancy status if relevant.
  • Exam performed: vulval + perianal; speculum findings if done.
  • Treatments tried and response (incl repeated antifungals / steroids / emollients / antibiotics).
  • Risk factors: smoking; immunosuppression; past abnormal cervical cytology/HPV history if relevant; age >40 if Bartholin mass.
  • Results: swabs/NAATs if done; diabetes/thyroid tests if relevant.

Who not to refer

  • Clearly explained transient symptoms that respond promptly to appropriate first-line management can be managed in primary care with safety-netting.
  • Small, asymptomatic Bartholin’s cyst can usually be managed conservatively with safety-netting (follow local pathway).
