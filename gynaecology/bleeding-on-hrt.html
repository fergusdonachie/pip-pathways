Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑09

Evidence summary (triangulated)

BMS 2024 “headlines” that change GP actions
  • Initial assessment should document bleeding pattern + exact HRT regimen + cancer risk factors, and include exam (abdo/pelvic) and relevant investigations (e.g., cervical screening if due/eligible, swabs if indicated, BMI).
  • Risk factors (endometrial cancer): major includes BMI ≥40 and Lynch/Cowden; minor includes BMI 30–39, diabetes, PCOS.
  • Progestogen adequacy matters: ensure monthly progestogen dose is proportionate to oestrogen dose; minimum progestogen days per month for sequential regimens (NET/MPA 10 days; micronised progesterone 12 days).
  • When to investigate (low-risk): if bleeding is within 6 months of starting HRT or persists within 3 months of a dose/prep change, BMS supports adjusting HRT/progestogen for a total of up to 6 months before escalating (if no cancer risk factors and bleeding not heavy/prolonged/daily).
  • TVUS thresholds (if uniform and fully visualised): ≤4 mm for continuous combined HRT; ≤7 mm for sequential HRT → low cancer risk; adjust HRT and review. Above these thresholds → urgent endometrial assessment pathway.
Why this matters for local services
  • BMS notes referral pressure increases when low-risk women are referred without attempting regimen optimisation; a clear DGRefHelp page can reduce unwarranted variation.
  • DGRefHelp should specify what to include in the referral so gynae can triage (regimen details, progestogen exposure, BMI/risk factors, bleeding pattern, TVUS details if done).

Unscheduled bleeding on HRT

Background

  • Unscheduled bleeding on HRT = bleeding on a regimen intended to be bleed-free (continuous combined), or bleeding outside the expected withdrawal bleed on sequential HRT.
  • A settling phase is common (up to 6 months after starting HRT or within 3 months of a regimen change).
  • Priorities: exclude non-uterine causes (cervix/vagina) and identify people needing urgent endometrial assessment.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Bleeding pattern: timing relative to HRT start/change; heavy/prolonged/daily vs light/spotting; recurrence/persistence.
  • HRT regimen details: continuous combined vs sequential; exact products + doses; route (oral/transdermal); progestogen type/dose/days per month; adherence (missed progestogen).
  • Risk factors: BMI (major: ≥40; minor: 30–39), diabetes, PCOS; hereditary syndromes (Lynch/Cowden) if known.
  • Examination/tests: abdominal + pelvic exam where appropriate; speculum if indicated; ensure cervical screening is up to date/eligible; consider swabs if discharge; document BMI.

Primary care management

  • If low cancer-risk and bleeding is within 6 months of starting HRT or within 3 months of a change: consider HRT/progestogen adjustments for a total of up to 6 months (BMS), provided bleeding is not heavy/prolonged/daily.
  • Check adherence and whether a combined preparation would reduce missed progestogen.
  • Consider vaginal oestrogen if atrophic findings.

Who to refer

Include the triage metrics. Secondary care needs exact regimen + progestogen exposure + risk factors + TVUS details if available.

  • Urgent assessment / imaging / gynae triage if: heavy/prolonged/daily bleeding; major risk factors; persistent bleeding despite appropriate regimen adjustments; concerning exam findings.

TVUS thresholds (BMS 2024) — if endometrium uniform + fully visualised

  • ccHRT: ET ≤4 mm → low risk; adjust HRT and review.
  • sHRT: ET ≤7 mm → low risk; adjust HRT and review.
  • ccHRT: ET >4 mm or sHRT: ET >7 mm → urgent pathway for endometrial assessment (biopsy and/or hysteroscopy).
  • If endometrium incompletely visualised → treat as abnormal and refer per local pathway.

Referral letter: required information (triage metrics)

  • Bleeding pattern + timing relative to HRT start/change; heavy/prolonged/daily?
  • Exact HRT regimen (products, doses, route, start dates) + progestogen exposure (days/month) + adherence.
  • BMI and risk factors (diabetes, PCOS; Lynch/Cowden if known).
  • Exam findings incl speculum/cervix; cervical screening status.
  • If done: TVUS report (ET, uniformity, focal lesions/polyps, adnexal findings).
  • What adjustments have already been tried (and response).

Who not to refer

  • Low-risk unscheduled bleeding within the expected settling phase may be managed with regimen optimisation and review (per BMS), provided bleeding is not heavy/prolonged/daily and there are no major risk factors.
