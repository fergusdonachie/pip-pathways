Evidence summary (triangulated)

Related pages
  • Postcoital bleeding (if PCB is the main symptom).
  • Symptoms suggestive of cervical cancer (PCB/discharge) — referral & colposcopy (if cervix/vagina/vulva looks suspicious or symptoms concerning despite up-to-date screening).
  • Heavy menstrual bleeding (if the main issue is heavy/prolonged bleeding with regular cycles).
  • Postmenopausal bleeding (if established postmenopausal).
Key pathway design points
  • Abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB) is a symptom group; priority is to identify who needs urgent exclusion of malignancy (endometrial/cervical) versus who can start primary care management.
  • NICE NG88 (HMB) gives clear triggers for considering endometrial pathology (risk factors / treatment failure) and recommends targeted sampling in the context of hysteroscopy (not blind biopsy for HMB).
  • Local DGRefHelp pages should make explicit what information secondary care needs for triage (age, BMI, bleeding pattern, PCB/IMB, tamoxifen, PCOS/obesity, exam findings, treatments tried).

NICE NG88 (used here for endometrial pathology risk framing)

  • Risk features for endometrial pathology that should prompt escalation include: persistent intermenstrual/irregular bleeding; infrequent heavy bleeding in obese women or those with PCOS; tamoxifen use; and treatment failure.
  • Investigation choice should be guided by history/exam: hysteroscopy for suspected submucosal fibroid/polyp/endometrial pathology; ultrasound for palpable uterus/pelvic mass/inconclusive exam.
Local agreement needed
  • Confirm the local D&G thresholds and referral routes for postcoital bleeding, abnormal cervix findings, and suspected endometrial pathology in pre/perimenopausal women (these are often board-specific).
  • Confirm whether D&G expects primary care to arrange ultrasound before gynae referral in specific scenarios (vs gynae arranging).

Abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB)

Related pages

Background

  • Includes intermenstrual bleeding (IMB), postcoital bleeding (PCB), irregular/unpredictable bleeding and persistent change in bleeding pattern (non-pregnant).
  • Priorities: exclude pregnancy where relevant; consider infection/cervical pathology; identify those needing urgent exclusion of endometrial/cervical malignancy.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: bleeding pattern (IMB/PCB, frequency, duration, volume), pain, discharge, contraception/HRT, cervical screening history, pregnancy risk, anticoagulants, tamoxifen.
  • Risk factors (endometrial pathology): age >45 with new/increased bleeding; persistent IMB/irregular bleeding; obesity/BMI >30; PCOS; tamoxifen; treatment failure.
  • Examination: speculum exam if PCB/discharge or cervix concern; bimanual/pelvic exam if pain/pressure symptoms or structural cause suspected.
  • Tests: pregnancy test if relevant; FBC if heavy/prolonged bleeding; STI testing if risk/discharge; consider TSH only if clinically indicated.

Primary care management

  • Treat contributory causes where identified (e.g., STI/cervicitis).
  • If low risk of structural/histological abnormality, consider primary care medical management (see HMB page for options) + safety-netting.
  • If structural cause suspected (pelvic mass/uterus palpable), arrange ultrasound as per local process.

Who to refer

Include the metrics secondary care needs for triage (bleeding pattern, risk factors, exam findings, tests done, treatments tried).

  • Suspected cervical cancer: abnormal cervix/visible lesion, contact bleeding with suspicious cervix → urgent pathway / colposcopy route per local process (state findings clearly).
  • Suspected endometrial pathology: persistent IMB/irregular bleeding with risk factors (age >45, obesity/PCOS, tamoxifen) or treatment failure → gynaecology for further assessment (often hysteroscopy-based).
  • Structural abnormality suspected (uterus palpable/pelvic mass) or abnormal scan findings → gynaecology referral with imaging report.

Referral letter: required information (triage metrics)

  • Age, BMI (if known), parity; pregnancy risk and pregnancy test result if relevant.
  • Bleeding pattern: IMB/PCB/irregular; duration; severity; impact; any anaemia symptoms.
  • Cervical screening history (date/result) and speculum findings (normal vs lesion/contact bleeding).
  • Risk factors: PCOS/obesity, tamoxifen, anticoagulation, family history (if relevant).
  • Treatments tried and response (incl CHC/progestogens/LNG‑IUS where relevant).
  • Results: FBC; STI tests if done; ultrasound report if performed.

Who not to refer

  • Low-risk bleeding patterns with no risk factors and normal examination may be managed initially in primary care with safety-netting (confirm locally).
