Key points
  • People with symptoms suggestive of cervical cancer (e.g. postcoital bleeding or persistent vaginal discharge not explained by infection/other causes) are not suitable candidates for cervical screening as the management route; they should have symptomatic specialist assessment.
  • If the appearance of the cervix is suspicious (or symptoms are strongly consistent with cervical cancer), the NHS Cervical Screening Programme guidance supports referral to colposcopy and being seen within 2 weeks.
  • A smear is not required before referral when cancer is suspected; a previous negative smear should not delay referral.
  • If common causes have been excluded (infection/contraception), refer to a gynaecologist experienced in cervical disease; they may refer on for symptomatic colposcopy outside the screening programme if cancer is suspected.

Why this page exists

  • In practice, PCB/discharge patients may be misdirected towards “do a smear” rather than symptom-led assessment/referral. This page aims to keep the rule simple: symptoms ≠ screening.

Symptoms suggestive of cervical cancer (PCB / persistent unexplained discharge)

Related pages
  • Postcoital bleeding
  • Abnormal uterine bleeding
  • Heavy menstrual bleeding
  • Postmenopausal bleeding
  • Cervical polyps

Principle

  • Cervical screening is for asymptomatic people. If symptomatic (e.g. PCB or persistent unexplained discharge), manage as a symptomatic problem, not a screening pathway.

Assessment (primary care)

  • History: PCB frequency/duration/persistence; discharge; intermenstrual or postmenopausal bleeding; pelvic pain; dyspareunia; contraception/HRT; pregnancy risk; anticoagulation; STI risk; cervical screening history (up to date?).
  • Examination: speculum (cervix + vagina) and vulva. Document cervix appearance explicitly: normal/benign vs suspicious (ulceration, induration, friable/fungating lesion, abnormal bleeding on contact with suspicious appearance).
  • Tests (as indicated): pregnancy test; chlamydia/gonorrhoea NAATs (and treat/partner management per local guidance); consider BV/trich/candida where appropriate.

Immediate actions

  • Suspicious cervix (or symptoms strongly consistent with cervical cancer): refer via urgent suspected cancer pathway / urgent colposcopy (do not delay for smear).
  • Not suspicious exam: exclude/treat common causes (infection, contraception-related bleeding, ectropion, trauma/atrophy) and safety-net. If persistent/troublesome, refer per local pathway (typically routine gynae).

Who to refer

Triage is driven by the speculum findings. If the cervix looks suspicious, use urgent suspected cancer / urgent colposcopy (do not delay for smear). If the exam is not suspicious, exclude/treat common causes and refer routinely if symptoms persist.

Urgent suspected cancer

  • Appearance of cervix consistent with cervical cancer.
  • Suspicious vaginal/vulval lesion (separate suspected cancer pathway as appropriate).
  • Do not delay referral for a smear. A previous negative smear should not delay referral if the cervix is suspicious.
  • Route (draft placeholder): SCI Gateway → DGRI → Gynaecology (suspected cancer / urgent colposcopy).

Routine gynaecology

  • PCB or persistent unexplained discharge with a non-suspicious exam after common causes have been excluded/treated, and symptoms persist.

Referral letter: required information (triage metrics)

  • Symptoms: PCB frequency/duration; discharge; any IMB/PMB; pelvic pain.
  • Speculum findings: cervix appearance (normal vs suspicious) + contact bleeding; ectropion/polyp/cervicitis; vaginal/vulval findings.
  • Infection testing and treatment: CT/GC NAAT results; other swabs if done; antibiotics given; pregnancy test result if relevant.
  • Cervical screening: last test date/result; whether up to date (note: negative result does not exclude cancer if cervix suspicious).

Who not to refer

  • Single episode PCB with an identifiable benign cause that resolves and a normal (non-suspicious) exam, with safety-netting.
