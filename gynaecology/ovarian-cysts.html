Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Postmenopausal incidental simple cyst ≤3 cm (unilateral, unilocular, simple): RCOG consensus update says no routine follow‑up (safety‑net for symptoms).
  • Symptoms suggest ovarian cancer: NICE NG12 gives the primary care cascade (CA‑125 → USS → suspected cancer referral if USS suggests cancer).
  • D&G local rule (draft assumption): postmenopausal incidental cyst >3 cm (or not clearly simple) → refer to general gynae and arrange CA‑125 at the same time so results are available for triage.

1) RCOG Green‑top Guideline No. 34 (postmenopausal cysts)

  • Scope: postmenopausal ovarian cysts; aims to reduce unnecessary surgery/testing while identifying who needs general gynae vs gynae oncology.
  • Dec 2025 consensus amendment (RCOG/RCR supported by BGCS/BSUR): postmenopausal women with unilateral, unilocular simple ovarian cysts ≤3 cm on imaging do not require routine follow‑up.
  • The public web page is a summary; the clinical algorithm is referenced as being in the PDF (page 6).

2) NICE NG12 (suspected ovarian cancer: symptoms → tests → referral)

Symptoms that trigger primary care testing (especially age ≥50; persistent/frequent, ~>12×/month):
  • persistent abdominal distension (“bloating”)
  • early satiety and/or loss of appetite
  • pelvic or abdominal pain
  • increased urinary urgency and/or frequency
  • also consider: unexplained weight loss, fatigue, change in bowel habit
  • new IBS-type symptoms in women ≥50 are a red flag (IBS rarely presents first time at this age)
NG12 primary care testing cascade (ovarian):
  • If exam shows ascites and/or pelvic/abdominal mass (not obviously fibroids) → suspected cancer pathway referral.
  • Otherwise, if symptoms suggest ovarian cancer → measure CA‑125 in primary care.
  • If CA‑125 ≥35 IU/mL → arrange abdominal + pelvic ultrasound.
  • If USS suggests ovarian cancer → suspected cancer pathway referral.
  • If CA‑125 normal (<35), or CA‑125 ≥35 with normal USS → look for other causes; safety‑net and reassess if symptoms persist/worsen.

3) Wider trawl / local pathway variation (what differs in practice)

  • Beyond the RCOG ≤3 cm “no follow‑up” rule, local pathways often add pragmatic thresholds for follow‑up (e.g., 1–5 cm schedules) and may embed risk scoring (RMI/IOTA/O‑RADS) for complex masses.
  • Right Decisions (GGC primary care): emphasises TVUS; uses CA‑125 (0–35) and RMI I with a cut‑off of 200; includes follow‑up schedules for 1–5 cm simple cysts (if CA‑125 normal) and escalation by symptoms/size/features. Source: Ovarian cysts in post‑menopausal women (GGC).
  • Right Decisions (NHS Borders acute gynae): includes pragmatic size bands for premenopausal simple cysts (e.g., <5 cm no routine follow‑up; 5–7 cm interval USS; >7 cm consider surgery) and advises CA‑125 for all postmenopausal ovarian masses with RMI >200 as a high‑risk threshold. Source: Ovarian cysts/masses management (NHS Borders).
  • Implication for D&G DGRefHelp: keep the high‑authority ‘≤3 cm simple postmenopausal = no routine follow‑up’ branch; explicitly state the local D&G rule for what happens above that (rather than importing another board’s threshold table).
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • Confirm the exact SCI Gateway destination/service naming for suspected ovarian cancer (USOC) vs urgent vs routine general gynae.
  • Confirm whether D&G uses any risk scoring model (RMI/IOTA/O‑RADS) for indeterminate/complex masses, and where it is calculated (radiology vs gynae triage).
  • Confirm whether any radiology‑led follow‑up exists (vs gynae owning follow‑up once referred).
  • Confirm whether CA‑125 should be ordered for all postmenopausal incidental cysts >3 cm (current draft assumes yes, ordered alongside referral).

Ovarian cysts

Background

  • Ovarian cysts are common; most are benign (functional cysts are common premenopausally; incidental cysts are also seen postmenopausally).
  • Two things not to miss: (1) acute complication (torsion/rupture/haemorrhage) and (2) ovarian cancer (especially postmenopausal and/or persistent symptom pattern).
  • Postmenopausal, unilateral unilocular simple cysts ≤3 cm do not require routine follow‑up (safety‑net).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • How do they present?
    • Acute pain (sudden severe unilateral pelvic pain ± vomiting) → think torsion/rupture/haemorrhage.
    • Persistent/vague abdominal or pelvic symptoms → consider ovarian cancer symptom pattern.
    • Incidental cyst on imaging (scan for something else) → decide if safe to manage vs refer.
  • Red flags / same‑day: peritonism, sepsis features, haemodynamic instability, uncontrolled pain, suspected torsion → urgent assessment via acute gynae/surgical pathway.
  • Symptoms suggesting ovarian cancer (esp ≥50; persistent/frequent): bloating/distension, early satiety/appetite loss, pelvic/abdominal pain, urinary urgency/frequency (also consider weight loss, fatigue, change in bowel habit; new IBS symptoms ≥50).
  • Examination: abdominal exam for mass/ascites; consider pelvic exam where appropriate.
  • Tests (essentials):
    • If symptoms suggest ovarian cancer: CA‑125.
    • If CA‑125 ≥35 → arrange abdo/pelvic ultrasound.
    • If CA‑125 normal (<35), or CA‑125 ≥35 with normal USS → assess other causes; safety‑net and review if symptoms persist/worsen.
    • Premenopausal: pregnancy test if relevant.
  • Interpret the scan description:
    • Simple (unilocular, thin‑walled, no solid parts/papillae; no ascites; unilateral) vs
    • Complex/indeterminate (solid areas, papillary projections, multilocular with concerning features, ascites, bilateral lesions).

Primary care management

  • OK to manage in primary care:
    • Postmenopausal: incidental unilateral unilocular simple cyst ≤3 cm and asymptomatic → reassure; no routine follow‑up; safety‑net for symptoms.
    • Premenopausal: clinically well with clearly simple cyst and no red flags → reassure + safety‑net (follow local follow‑up policy if scan recommends repeat).
  • What to do while awaiting referral/clarification: analgesia; document symptom pattern; ensure results (CA‑125/USS) are available to the receiving service when requested.

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency. Include CA‑125 if indicated so results are available.

Suspected cancer pathway (USOC)

  • Refer USOC if any of the following:
    • Ascites and/or pelvic/abdominal mass on examination (not obviously fibroids), or
    • Ultrasound suggests ovarian cancer, or
    • Persistent ovarian cancer symptom pattern with concerning findings (use judgement + safety‑net).
  • Tests: follow NG12 cascade (CA‑125 → if ≥35 then USS). Do not delay referral if exam/USS is already strongly suspicious.
  • Route (draft): SCI Gateway → DGRI → Gynaecology (USOC / suspected ovarian cancer).

Urgent gynaecology

  • Refer urgent if:
    • Postmenopausal cyst with significant symptoms, or
    • Complex/indeterminate mass with concerning features but not clearly meeting USOC criteria.
  • Tests: arrange CA‑125 if postmenopausal (or if symptoms suggest cancer) so result is available.
  • Route (draft): SCI Gateway → DGRI → Gynaecology → General (Urgent).

Routine gynaecology

  • D&G local rule for this page (draft assumption): postmenopausal incidental cyst >3 cm (or not clearly simple) → refer routine gynae and arrange CA‑125 at the same time.
  • Premenopausal symptomatic cysts persisting despite initial measures, or cysts with uncertainty/persistence → consider routine referral.
  • Route (draft): SCI Gateway → DGRI → Gynaecology → General (Routine).

Who not to refer

  • Postmenopausal, asymptomatic unilateral unilocular simple cyst ≤3 cm.
  • Premenopausal, clinically well with clearly simple cyst and no red flags (manage conservatively + safety‑net).
