DGRefHelp drafts / Urinary incontinence (women)

Primary care: categorise (stress / urgency-OAB / mixed), do the minimum sensible workup (urine dip + exam where indicated), start first-line conservative care (PFMT / bladder training / lifestyle), and know when to refer.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑09

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key points for a GP-facing pathway page
  • At first assessment, categorise urinary incontinence as stress, urgency urinary incontinence/overactive bladder (OAB), or mixed; in mixed, treat the predominant symptom first (NICE NG123).
  • Initial baseline: urine dipstick for blood/glucose/protein/leukocytes/nitrites; treat/confirm UTI only when appropriate (NICE NG123).
  • Use a 3-day bladder diary in initial assessment of urinary incontinence/OAB (NICE NG123).
  • First-line conservative management:
    • Stress (or mixed): supervised pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT) for ≥3 months (NICE NG123).
    • Urgency/OAB (or mixed): bladder training for ≥6 weeks (NICE NG123) + lifestyle measures.
  • If medicines are used for OAB: counsel that benefit may take ≥4 weeks; review at 4 weeks; consider anticholinergic burden/cognition risk; consider beta‑3 agonist options if antimuscarinics unsuitable/ineffective (NICE NG123 + TA guidance).
  • Consider referral to specialist services for red-flag patterns (pain, voiding difficulty/retention, suspected fistula, neurological disease, faecal incontinence, pelvic mass), or after failed/tolerated conservative + medicine trials (NICE NG123).

Assessment themes (primary care)

  • Type of symptoms: stress (cough/laugh/exertion), urgency/OAB (urgency +/- frequency/nocturia), mixed (predominant symptom).
  • Contributors: pregnancy/childbirth, menopause/atrophy, obesity, constipation, chronic cough, high caffeine, meds (diuretics), mobility/cognitive impairment.
  • Examination (when indicated): abdominal and pelvic exam if needed to exclude pelvic mass/prolapse; check for atrophic changes if GSM suspected; assess pelvic floor muscle contraction before supervised PFMT (digital assessment recommended by NICE).
  • Tests: urine dip in all; MSU culture guided by symptoms + dip results (NICE NG123). Consider PVR (bladder scan) if voiding dysfunction symptoms or recurrent UTI; use bladder diary (≥3 days).
  • Do not routinely: imaging (CT/MRI/US except residual), cystoscopy, pad tests, or urodynamics prior to primary surgery when straightforward stress UI demonstrated (NICE NG123; specialist decision).

Conservative management (first-line)

  • Stress / mixed: supervised PFMT ≥3 months; typical programme: ≥8 contractions, 3 times/day; continue if beneficial (NICE NG123).
  • Urgency/OAB / mixed: bladder training ≥6 weeks; consider adding medicine if bladder training insufficient and frequency troublesome (NICE NG123).
  • Lifestyle: reduce caffeine (OAB); fluid optimisation if too high/low; weight loss if BMI >30; manage constipation; address cough/smoking (NICE NG123).
  • GSM/atrophy: consider vaginal oestrogen if OAB symptoms + GSM (NICE NG123).
  • Containment products: not a treatment; can be used as coping strategy pending definitive care, adjunct, or long-term only after treatment options explored; ensure annual review (NICE NG123).
Local (D&G)
  • Likely destinations vary by local service: continence nurse / pelvic floor physio / urogynaecology / urology. This page includes a Local agreement needed list to avoid blocking drafting.

Urinary incontinence (women)

Background

  • Categorise as stress, urgency urinary incontinence/overactive bladder (OAB), or mixed; in mixed, treat the predominant symptom first.
  • First-line is usually conservative management (PFMT / bladder training + lifestyle).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Symptoms: stress (leak with cough/laugh/exertion), urgency/OAB (urgency +/- frequency/nocturia), mixed (predominant symptom). Document impact.
  • Baseline tests: urine dipstick in all (blood, glucose, protein, leukocytes, nitrites). MSU culture guided by symptoms + dip results.
  • Bladder diary: ask for ≥3 days (include work/leisure days).
  • Examination: abdominal/pelvic exam when needed to exclude pelvic mass/prolapse/other pathology; consider GSM/atrophy. Assess pelvic floor muscle contraction before supervised PFMT.
  • Consider post-void residual (bladder scan): if voiding dysfunction symptoms or recurrent UTI.
  • Do not routinely: imaging (except residual), cystoscopy, pad tests, or urodynamics in straightforward primary stress UI prior to surgery (specialist decision).

Primary care management

  • Lifestyle: reduce caffeine (OAB); fluid optimisation; weight loss if BMI >30; constipation management; address chronic cough/smoking.
  • Stress (or stress-predominant mixed): supervised PFMT for ≥3 months (typical: ≥8 contractions, 3 times/day). Continue if beneficial.
  • Urgency/OAB (or urgency-predominant mixed): bladder training for ≥6 weeks. If insufficient benefit and frequency is troublesome, consider adding an OAB medicine alongside bladder training (counsel: benefits may take ≥4 weeks; consider anticholinergic burden/cognitive risk).
  • GSM/atrophy: consider vaginal oestrogen if OAB symptoms plus genitourinary symptoms/signs of menopause (check contraindications and local formulary).
  • Containment products: coping strategy pending treatment; adjunct; or long-term only after treatment options explored (review at least annually if long-term).

Who to refer

  • Consider specialist referral (continence/urogyn/urology depending on local model) if: persisting bladder/urethral pain; voiding difficulty/retention; suspected fistula; suspected neurological disease; associated faecal incontinence; pelvic mass; previous continence surgery; previous pelvic cancer surgery; previous pelvic radiotherapy.
  • Suspected cancer referral (NG12/local pathway): haematuria or recurrent/persistent unexplained UTI.
  • OAB: if medicines tried but not successful or not tolerated → secondary care for further options.
  • Stress UI: if conservative management fails and patient wants to discuss surgery → refer for specialist assessment and shared decision-making.

Referral letter: suggested information (triage metrics)

  • Category + predominant symptom; duration; impact; triggers; nocturia; pad use.
  • Urine dip + MSU (if done); haematuria present/absent; recurrent UTI history.
  • 3‑day bladder diary summary (frequency, volumes, urgency and incontinence episodes; fluids/caffeine).
  • Exam findings if performed: prolapse present/absent; pelvic mass excluded; GSM/atrophy signs; pelvic floor contraction.
  • Treatments tried: PFMT (duration, supervised?), bladder training duration, lifestyle changes; medicines tried and adverse effects.
  • Red flags / relevant history: voiding difficulty/retention symptoms; neurological symptoms; faecal incontinence; previous pelvic surgery/radiotherapy/continence surgery.

Who not to refer

  • Uncomplicated stress/urgency/mixed urinary incontinence without red flags: start conservative management in primary care with planned review.
