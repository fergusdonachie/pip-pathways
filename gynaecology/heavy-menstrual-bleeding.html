Key decision points (for a GP-facing pathway page)
  • Start with impact on quality of life (NG88), then assess for structural vs histological pathology risk.
  • Baseline tests: FBC for all; TSH only if clinically indicated (NG88 + NHS Scotland pathway).
  • First-line treatment for many people with no obvious structural cause: LNG‑IUS, or (if unsuitable/declined) tranexamic acid / NSAIDs / CHC / cyclical progestogen (NG88; mirrored in the national pathway).
  • Imaging/investigation choices: offer ultrasound when uterus is palpable abdominally / pelvic mass suspected / exam inconclusive (NG88); consider hysteroscopy and targeted biopsy when endometrial pathology risk is higher (NG88).

NHS Scotland CfSD HMB pathway (v1)

  • Pragmatic “one page” primary care algorithm, explicitly including: FBC; TSH only if indicated; and high‑risk features prompting concern for endometrial pathology (e.g., persistent intermenstrual/postcoital bleeding, PCOS with obesity, tamoxifen use, age >45 with increased bleeding).
  • Includes treatment options commonly used in primary care and flags when to consider ultrasound and/or referral for hysteroscopy/biopsy/colposcopy/gynae.

NICE NG88 (HMB)

  • NG88 emphasises that the aim is to improve quality of life (not just quantify blood loss).
  • FBC for all; do not test thyroid function unless symptomatic for thyroid disease; consider coagulation disorder testing if HMB since menarche with personal/family history.
  • Investigation selection is guided by symptoms/risk: hysteroscopy for suspected submucosal fibroid/polyp/endometrial pathology; ultrasound for possible larger fibroids or pelvic mass; TVUS preferred for suspected adenomyosis.
Local variation to highlight (D&G)
  • If D&G uses a local “direct-to” service (e.g., community gynaecology, one‑stop clinic, hysteroscopy triage) or specific ultrasound access arrangements, that should be explicit in “Who to refer” and “Referral letter: required info”.
  • Where secondary care triage tools are used (e.g., BMI, age bands, treatment failures, suspected mass), the pathway should state the metrics needed on the referral.

Heavy menstrual bleeding

Related pages

Background

  • Heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB) is defined by the woman’s perception that bleeding is excessive and affects quality of life.
  • Assess for features suggesting structural causes (fibroids/polyp/mass) or histological risk (endometrial pathology).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: bleeding pattern; impact on life; pelvic pain/pressure; persistent intermenstrual bleeding; postcoital bleeding; fertility wishes; contraception; previous treatment; comorbidities.
  • High‑risk features (endometrial pathology consideration): persistent intermenstrual or postcoital bleeding; PCOS with obesity; age >45 with increased bleeding; tamoxifen use; treatment failure.
  • Examination: pelvic exam if symptoms/risk suggest pelvic mass/uterine abnormality or before LNG‑IUS; consider speculum if postcoital bleeding/discharge.
  • Tests: FBC for all; TSH only if clinically indicated; consider coagulation disorder testing if HMB since menarche plus personal/family history of bleeding disorder.

Primary care management

  • If low risk of structural/histological abnormality: start treatment without initial imaging/invasive investigation.
  • Most effective option (when appropriate): LNG‑IUS.
  • If LNG‑IUS declined/unsuitable: consider tranexamic acid during menses; NSAIDs; combined hormonal contraception (without a pill‑free week as per the national pathway); or cyclical oral progestogen (e.g. days 5–26).
  • Consider depot progestogen contraception (e.g., DMPA/Sayana Press) where appropriate.
  • When to consider ultrasound: uterus palpable abdominally, pelvic mass suspected, or exam inconclusive/difficult.

Who to refer

Key principle: include the metrics secondary care needs for triage (risk factors + what has been tried).

  • Histological abnormality suspected (high‑risk features or treatment failure): consider referral for hysteroscopy ± endometrial sampling (service dependent).
  • Structural abnormality suspected (uterus palpable abdominally / pelvic mass): arrange ultrasound and refer as indicated.
  • Scan findings: suspicious pelvic mass → urgent gynae referral; fibroids >3 cm → gynae referral (first‑line medical options may still help while awaiting review).
  • Postcoital bleeding / cervix concern: consider colposcopy pathway as locally indicated; state speculum findings clearly.

Referral letter: required information (triage metrics)

  • Age, BMI (if known), parity; smoking status if relevant to treatment choice.
  • Bleeding pattern: duration; flooding/clots; intermenstrual bleeding; postcoital bleeding; impact on quality of life.
  • Pelvic symptoms: pain/pressure; urinary/bowel symptoms; any mass symptoms.
  • Current contraception; fertility wishes.
  • Risk factors: PCOS + obesity; tamoxifen; anticoagulation; family/personal bleeding disorder history.
  • Treatments tried and duration (LNG‑IUS / TXA / NSAIDs / CHC / progestogens) and response.
  • Examination findings (including speculum) and FBC result; TSH if done for clinical indication.
  • If imaging performed: ultrasound report details (fibroid size/location, endometrial appearance, adnexal findings).

Who not to refer

  • Many women with HMB and no related symptoms suggesting structural/histological abnormality can start first‑line medical management in primary care.
