Vulval lump at posterior introitus: conservative care vs antibiotics vs drainage (Word catheter / marsupialisation), red flags, and referral-letter triage metrics.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • Most symptomatic Bartholin’s cysts/abscesses can be managed conservatively or with a simple drainage procedure; the GP focus is: exclude unusual/suspicious lesions, treat infection/cellulitis when present, and arrange definitive drainage when needed.
  • Antibiotics are not routinely required for uncomplicated cysts/abscesses; they are typically reserved for cellulitis/offensive purulent discharge/systemic features or when spontaneous drainage occurs and infection is suspected.
  • If drainage is needed, many UK hospital pathways recommend Word balloon catheter as first-line procedural management (often under local anaesthetic) to create a temporary fistula and reduce recurrence.
  • Marsupialisation is generally reserved for recurrent abscess, failed Word catheter, complex/unusual cases, or patient preference (often under GA).
  • Consider biopsy/histology in older patients (commonly cited threshold: >40) because Bartholin gland malignancy is rare but more likely with increasing age / atypical features.
Local (D&G): likely decisions needed
  • Where primary care should route patients needing drainage (e.g. same-day gynae assessment unit vs general gynae triage vs ED), and typical timescales.
  • Local antibiotic choices (and whether to advise STI screen routinely after resolution in sexually active patients).
  • Whether D&G applies an age/atypical-features rule for biopsy (often >40) and whether that is done at the time of drainage.

Bartholin’s cyst/abscess

Background

  • Bartholin’s glands sit at the posterior vestibule (either side of the vaginal opening). Duct obstruction can cause a cyst (often less painful); infection can cause an abscess (usually very painful).
  • Many cases settle with conservative measures, but persistent/painful abscesses often need drainage.

Assess (primary care)

  • Symptoms: unilateral vulval lump/pain, discharge, difficulty walking/sitting, dyspareunia; fever/systemic upset.
  • Exam: confirm location (posterior introitus/labia); look for cellulitis; look for atypical/suspicious features (ulceration, induration, irregular mass, fixed lesion, bleeding).
  • Rule out differentials: vulval abscess elsewhere, hidradenitis, infected epidermoid cyst, vulval malignancy, genital ulcer disease.
  • STI risk: consider CT/GC NAATs if risk (and consider advising sexual health screen after resolution in sexually active patients as per local practice).

Red flags / urgent assessment

  • Systemic illness, rapidly spreading cellulitis, immunosuppression, severe pain with inability to mobilise/urinate, or concern for necrotising infection.
  • Atypical lesion (especially age >40, new persistent mass, ulceration/induration/fixity) → needs specialist assessment ± biopsy/histology.

Primary care management (while awaiting definitive treatment)

  • Analgesia (e.g. paracetamol/ibuprofen if suitable).
  • Warm baths/sitz baths and local care; avoid irritants (bubble bath/talcum/lotions).
  • Antibiotics: not routinely needed for uncomplicated cyst/abscess; consider if cellulitis/offensive purulent discharge/systemic features, or if abscess is draining and infection suspected (use local antimicrobial guidance).
  • Do not attempt needle aspiration or simple I&D as a definitive approach unless local pathway explicitly recommends it (higher recurrence than fistula-forming procedures).

Who to refer (and likely destination)

  • Suspected complex/unusual/atypical mass (esp. >40) → gynaecology for assessment ± biopsy/histology.
  • Abscess requiring drainage (severe pain, persistent/enlarging despite conservative care) → gynaecology for Word catheter (often outpatient/LA) or marsupialisation depending on recurrence/complexity.
  • Recurrent abscess or failed Word catheter → consider marsupialisation (service-dependent).

Referral letter: required information (triage metrics)

  • Age; pregnancy status; immunosuppression/diabetes/anticoagulants.
  • Side (left/right), size estimate, duration, recurrence history; pain severity; any spontaneous drainage.
  • Exam: presence/extent of cellulitis; whether lesion is typical vs atypical/suspicious; any ulceration/induration/fixity.
  • Systemic features (fever), ability to mobilise/urinate.
  • STI risk and any swabs performed; antibiotics already given and response.
