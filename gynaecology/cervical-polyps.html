DGRefHelp drafts / Cervical polyps

Incidental vs symptomatic cervical polyp: assess for cervix/endometrium red flags, when primary care removal is reasonable, and what secondary care needs in the referral letter.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑09

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • Cervical polyps are common and usually benign; the main GP priority is to avoid missing cervical cancer (suspicious cervix) and to recognise when it may be an endometrial polyp (base not seen / appears to originate from canal/uterus).
  • If a polyp is incidental and asymptomatic and the cervix otherwise looks benign, many pathways advise no referral unless the appearance is concerning.
  • If symptomatic (IMB/PCB/blood-stained discharge) or the polyp is large / base not visible, most pathways advise routine gynae referral (or removal if appropriately trained in primary care) and send for histology.
  • If the cervix looks fungating/ulcerated/indurated (or otherwise suspicious), refer via urgent suspected cancer route.
  • For referral triage, secondary care mainly needs: symptom pattern, speculum findings (incl “suspicious?”), smear history, and whether the polyp base is visible (cervical vs possible endometrial origin).
Local (D&G): unknowns to confirm
  • Whether D&G prefers general gynae vs any direct colposcopy referral criteria for “cervical polyp + bleeding”.
  • Whether primary care polypectomy is supported (training/governance, histology handling, acceptable technique).

Comparator pathways used for the draft

  • Devon Formulary guidance includes: urgent suspected cancer if suspicious/fungating mass; routine gynae if symptomatic and removal impracticable, base not visible (possible endometrial polyp), size >1 cm, or confirmed endometrial polyp; suggests primary care removal by trained clinicians with histology.
  • BNSSG Remedy emphasises: asymptomatic incidental polyps usually do not need referral; suspicious appearance → 2WW; colposcopy capacity constraints → polyps/bleeding generally to gynaecology unless colposcopy specifically indicated.

Cervical polyps

Background

  • Cervical polyps are common and usually benign.
  • They may be incidental or cause intermenstrual bleeding (IMB), postcoital bleeding (PCB) or blood-stained discharge.
  • Key priority: identify a cervix/lesion that is suspicious for malignancy and ensure appropriate urgent referral.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: symptoms (IMB/PCB/discharge), duration and persistence; pain; pregnancy risk; contraception/HRT; anticoagulation; cervical screening history (last test date/result; up to date?).
  • Examination: speculum exam to assess cervix and polyp; note whether appearance is benign vs suspicious (ulceration, induration, friability, fungating mass).
  • Key observations: approximate size; whether base is visible (if not, consider endometrial polyp); contact bleeding; discharge; cervical ectropion/cervicitis/other lesions.
  • Swabs/tests: STI NAATs if risk; pregnancy test if relevant; smear if due and feasible (do not delay urgent referral if suspicious).

Primary care management

  • If suspicious cervix/lesion: do not attempt removal—refer via urgent suspected cancer route.
  • If asymptomatic and clearly benign: offer reassurance and monitoring (local policy may vary).
  • If symptomatic and clearly benign: consider removal if you are trained, equipped and can send tissue for histology; otherwise refer routinely to gynaecology.

Who to refer

Key principle: the referral letter content affects triage (urgent suspected cancer vs routine gynae; cervix vs possible endometrial origin). Include the metrics secondary care needs.

Urgent suspected cancer

  • Cervix/lesion suspicious for malignancy (e.g., fungating/ulcerated/indurated mass, irregular friable cervix).

Routine gynaecology

  • Symptomatic polyp where primary care removal is impracticable.
  • Polyp base not visible (possible endometrial polyp).
  • Large polyp (e.g., >1 cm) or recurrent/persistent symptoms.

Referral letter: required information (triage metrics)

  • Symptoms: IMB/PCB/discharge; duration; persistence; any postmenopausal bleeding.
  • Speculum findings: cervix benign vs suspicious; polyp size/appearance; contact bleeding; discharge.
  • Is the base of the polyp visible? (If not, possible endometrial polyp.)
  • Cervical screening: last smear date/result; whether up to date.
  • Pregnancy risk / contraception / HRT; anticoagulation.
  • Swab results if relevant (STI NAATs).

Who not to refer

  • Incidental, asymptomatic polyp with benign appearance and no concerning features (local policy may vary).
