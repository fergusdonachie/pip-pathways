Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the local wording around
  • Symptoms ≠ screening: the NHS Cervical Screening Programme guidance states that people with symptoms consistent with cervical cancer (e.g. postcoital bleeding or persistent unexplained vaginal discharge) are not suitable candidates for screening; after common causes are excluded, they should be referred for gynaecological assessment, with onward symptomatic colposcopy if cancer is suspected.
  • Immediate suspected cancer trigger: NICE NG12 (and the screening programme guidance) both support: suspicious-looking cervix on examination → suspected cancer pathway referral (do not delay for smear).
  • Service model variation: different NHS areas route non-suspicious PCB as (a) general gynae first, (b) direct symptomatic colposcopy for defined triggers, and they differ on whether TVUS is expected pre-referral and who owns suspected cervicitis/STI pathways.

1) NICE NG12 — suspected cervical cancer (trigger is exam appearance)

  • NICE NG12 includes a gynaecological cancers section and states to consider a suspected cancer pathway referral for women if, on examination, the appearance of the cervix is consistent with cervical cancer.
  • NG12 is intentionally high-level for PCB; local pathways define what happens when the cervix is not suspicious.

2) NHS Cervical Screening Programme / colposcopy management — symptoms pathway

  • The screening programme guidance states: individuals presenting with symptoms of cervical cancer (examples given: postcoital bleeding or persistent vaginal discharge that cannot be explained by infection or other causes) are not suitable candidates for screening.
  • If common causes are excluded in general practice (infection, contraception), the individual must be referred for examination by a gynaecologist experienced in cervical disease management; onward symptomatic colposcopy may be arranged if cancer is suspected.
  • Also explicitly: contact bleeding at the time of cervical sampling is not an indication for colposcopy referral in the absence of other symptoms.

3) Example UK local models (not D&G) — how others operationalise “normal cervix PCB”

  • Royal Cornwall Hospitals (RMS pathway): notes PCB rarely indicates cervical cancer with no other signs; recommends chlamydia testing and smear if due; routine referral to benign gynae clinic for PCB with normal cervix once common causes excluded; excludes referral to colposcopy with normal screening (colposcopy is lab-driven after abnormal screening results).
  • Devon pathway examples: describe a direct suspected cancer route to colposcopy when cervix is abnormal/suspicious; otherwise manage common causes and refer by local benign gynae route if persistent/troublesome.

PCB routing decisions (D&G)

Background

  • This is an operational note to make PCB wording unambiguous for D&G (general gynae vs symptomatic colposcopy; TVUS expectation; STI/cervicitis route).
  • Clinical rule stays constant: suspicious cervix/vulva/vagina on examination → suspected cancer pathway referral (do not delay for smear).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Decide “suspicious” vs “non-suspicious” exam: speculum/vaginal exam findings drive urgency.
  • If non-suspicious: exclude/treat common causes (STI/cervicitis; contraception/HRT; cervical ectropion/polyp; pregnancy if relevant) and safety-net.
  • If persistent/troublesome PCB with non-suspicious exam: route using the locally agreed model (below).

Primary care management

  • Symptoms consistent with cervical cancer are not “screening”: if common causes are excluded and symptoms persist, use the symptomatic referral model rather than relying on a smear to manage risk.
  • Screening if due/overdue: can be taken when appropriate, but do not delay urgent suspected cancer referral when exam is suspicious.
