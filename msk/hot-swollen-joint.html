Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Assume septic arthritis until excluded in any acute hot, swollen, painful joint — delays in diagnosis/treatment increase morbidity and mortality.
  • Urgent aspiration + culture is central: synovial fluid should be aspirated and sent for microscopy/culture before antibiotics where feasible (do not delay antibiotics if the patient is septic/unstable).
  • It can still be septic without fever and normal inflammatory markers do not exclude it — use clinical judgement and risk factors.

1) BSR/BHPR/BOA/RCGP/BSAC “hot swollen joint” guideline (2006) + BSR update programme

  • Classic UK consensus guideline (2006) emphasised: treat high suspicion as septic arthritis even without fever; aspirate (and send synovial fluid for Gram stain/culture) prior to antibiotics where possible.
  • BSR published a 2025 guideline scope noting diagnostic/management advances since 2006 and ongoing need for high-quality, updated recommendations; reiterates the core diagnostic approach: history/exam + labs + aspiration; imaging (USS/MRI/CT) can help guide aspiration and assess complications.

2) NHS guidance (patient-facing, but useful for urgency framing)

  • Septic arthritis is described as a serious joint infection needing urgent assessment, usually with sudden severe pain, swelling, skin colour change and systemic symptoms; treatment is typically hospital admission with IV antibiotics and often drainage.
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • Confirm the D&G default receiving service for suspected septic arthritis from primary care: ED/acute medicine vs direct ortho vs rheumatology (and whether there is an SDEC/assessment unit pathway).
  • Confirm whether there is a same-day aspiration pathway (e.g., ED → ortho; rheum “hot joint” clinic; radiology/USS-guided aspiration) and expected timeframe.
  • Confirm local stance on starting antibiotics in community if transfer will be delayed (most pathways prefer aspiration first; exceptions may exist for sepsis).
  • Confirm the prosthetic joint hot swelling route (usually urgent orthopaedics) and whether GP should ring an on-call service directly.
This evidence summary is written for pathway design: it highlights only decision‑changing points and where local policy choices sit.

Hot swollen joint (adult) — septic arthritis vs crystal/inflammatory

Background

  • An acute hot, swollen, painful joint is septic arthritis until proven otherwise.
  • Septic arthritis can cause rapid joint destruction and systemic illness; same-day assessment is usually required.
  • Crystal arthritis (gout/CPPD) is common and can look identical; gout and sepsis can co-exist.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Triage first: unwell/rigors, hypotension, tachycardia, confusion, uncontrolled pain, rapidly progressive swelling/erythema → treat as emergency.
  • History:
    • Onset (sudden over hours vs gradual), single joint vs multiple, ability to weight-bear / use limb.
    • Systemic features: fever, rigors, malaise.
    • Risk factors for septic arthritis: immunosuppression, diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, skin infection/cellulitis, recent joint injection/surgery, IVDU, prosthetic joint.
    • Crystal clues: previous attacks, hyperuricaemia/gout history, diuretics/CKD, classic 1st MTP involvement (but gout can be elsewhere).
    • Trauma/anticoagulation (haemarthrosis), recent diarrhoea/urethritis (reactive arthritis).
  • Examination: vitals; inspect for erythema/cellulitis; range of motion (sepsis often very painful with movement); check for an effusion; examine for multiple joints, tophi, rash, conjunctivitis/urethritis; assess neurovascular status if limb swollen.
  • Do not rely on normal bloods to rule out septic arthritis; decision is primarily clinical.

Primary care management

  • If septic arthritis is possible: arrange same-day hospital assessment for aspiration and IV antibiotics.
  • Avoid starting antibiotics before aspiration if the patient is stable and transfer is rapid, because it can reduce culture yield (do not delay antibiotics if septic/unstable).
  • Analgesia while awaiting assessment (consider NSAID contraindications; consider colchicine only if confident crystal arthritis and sepsis is unlikely).
  • If classic recurrent gout/CPPD and systemically well with no red flags, treat in primary care and safety-net for non-response or deterioration.

Who to refer

If in doubt, treat as septic arthritis and refer for same-day assessment.

Emergency / same-day assessment

  • Suspected septic arthritis (especially monoarthritis with severe pain, reduced movement, fever/systemic upset, or significant risk factors) → urgent hospital assessment for aspiration + IV antibiotics.
  • Prosthetic joint with acute hot swelling/pain → urgent orthopaedics (treat as infection until excluded).
  • Systemically unwell / possible sepsis → 999 / ED as appropriate.
  • Route (draft): ED / acute assessment (SDEC) → ortho/rheum review → aspiration (± ultrasound-guided) → microbiology-directed antibiotics.

Urgent (but not emergency) specialist input

  • Multiple hot swollen joints or recurrent episodes with diagnostic uncertainty → consider urgent rheumatology / acute medicine assessment (local process).
  • Suspected inflammatory arthritis (persistent synovitis >6 weeks, morning stiffness, multiple joints) → see separate inflammatory arthritis pathway (next draft).

Who not to refer

  • A classic recurrent gout flare in the typical pattern, systemically well, and septic arthritis is clinically unlikely — manage in primary care with safety-netting.
  • Known osteoarthritis with mild effusion and no inflammatory features — manage routinely (analgesia, activity modification, physio as appropriate).
