Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Suspected persistent synovitis (undetermined cause) → refer for specialist opinion; urgent if small joints hands/feet, >1 joint, or symptom-to-presentation delay ≥3 months (NICE NG100).
  • Do not let “normal bloods” delay referral: urgent referral still applies even with normal acute-phase response and/or negative RF/anti‑CCP (NICE NG100).
  • Suspected axial spondyloarthritis has a specific primary care referral rule (LBP <45 onset, >3 months + additional criteria; HLA‑B27 only if exactly 3 criteria) (NICE NG65).

1) NICE NG100 — Rheumatoid arthritis in adults (referral + key investigations)

  • Referral: refer any adult with suspected persistent synovitis of undetermined cause for specialist opinion.
  • Urgent referral (even if CRP/ESR normal and RF/anti‑CCP negative) if:
    • small joints of hands or feet are affected, or
    • more than 1 joint is affected, or
    • delay ≥3 months between symptom onset and seeking advice.
  • Primary care investigations (optional; do not delay referral): RF; consider anti‑CCP if RF negative; X‑ray hands/feet if suspected RA with persistent synovitis.

2) NICE QS33 — 3-working-day aspiration for “urgent referral” (audit target)

  • Quality standard aims for adults with suspected persistent synovitis affecting >1 joint or small joints of hands/feet to be referred to rheumatology within 3 working days of presenting in primary care (pragmatic audit timeframe; based on expert opinion).

3) NICE NG65 — Spondyloarthritis (recognition + referral)

  • Do not rule out spondyloarthritis based on any single sign/test (HLA‑B27 negative and/or normal CRP/ESR does not exclude).
  • Axial spondyloarthritis referral rule: low back pain started <45 years and >3 months duration → refer to rheumatology if 4+ additional criteria (or if exactly 3, do HLA‑B27 and refer if positive).
  • Peripheral spondyloarthritis: urgently refer suspected new-onset inflammatory arthritis to rheumatology unless RA/gout/CPPD suspected; refer dactylitis; refer enthesitis without mechanical cause if persistent/multisite or with SpA features.
  • Uveitis: immediate (same‑day) ophthalmology assessment for symptoms of acute anterior uveitis (eye pain/redness/photophobia/blurred vision).

4) NICE CG153 — Psoriasis (psoriatic arthritis)

  • As soon as psoriatic arthritis is suspected → refer to a rheumatologist for assessment and care planning.
  • In adults with psoriasis, a validated screening tool such as PEST can support case-finding (note: does not detect axial disease).

Suspected inflammatory arthritis

Background

  • Inflammatory arthritis causes joint inflammation (synovitis) with pain, swelling and stiffness; early diagnosis and treatment improves outcomes.
  • Key primary care job: (1) do not miss emergencies (septic arthritis/acute hot swollen joint) and (2) refer suspected persistent synovitis early (do not wait for “positive bloods”).
  • Inflammatory patterns include: rheumatoid arthritis (small joint synovitis), psoriatic arthritis (often with psoriasis/nail change; can be axial), and spondyloarthritis (inflammatory back pain, enthesitis, dactylitis, uveitis/IBD associations).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Confirm “inflammatory” pattern (suggestive features):
    • Morning stiffness >30 minutes and/or stiffness after rest (“gelling”).
    • Visible swelling (synovitis) and reduced function; symptoms persisting >3–4 weeks.
    • Systemic inflammatory symptoms sometimes occur (fatigue/malaise).
  • Emergency / same‑day: hot swollen joint with systemic upset, inability to weight-bear/use the joint, rapidly progressive symptoms, immunosuppression, or concern for septic arthritis → urgent same‑day assessment (do not sit on it). (See also: Hot swollen joint.)
  • Pattern helps triage:
    • Small joints hands/feet, symmetrical → rheumatoid arthritis more likely.
    • Dactylitis (whole digit) or enthesitis (tendon insertion pain, e.g. heel) → think spondyloarthritis/psoriatic arthritis.
    • Inflammatory back pain (young onset, improves with movement, night pain) → consider axial spondyloarthritis.
    • Look for psoriasis (including nails), uveitis symptoms (pain/redness/photophobia/blurred vision), IBD, and recent GU/GI infection.
  • Examination: document which joints are involved; check for synovitis; assess function; look at skin/nails; check for enthesitis/dactylitis.
  • Optional tests (do not delay referral):
    • CRP/ESR (normal does not exclude inflammatory arthritis).
    • Rheumatoid factor; if negative, consider anti‑CCP.
    • If suspected rheumatoid arthritis with persistent synovitis: X‑ray hands/feet (if locally helpful).

Primary care management

  • Safety‑net while awaiting rheumatology: advise to return urgently if a joint becomes hot, very painful, rapidly swollen, or if fever/systemic symptoms develop.
  • Symptom control: consider NSAID (if appropriate) and simple analgesia; consider gastroprotection based on risk; avoid delaying referral while “trying another NSAID”.
  • If inflammatory back pain suspected: keep active; consider NSAID trial (response within ~48h supports inflammatory cause but is not diagnostic).
  • Documentation to help triage: symptom onset date, duration of morning stiffness, swollen joints count/sites, function impact, psoriasis/nail disease, uveitis/IBD, family history of spondyloarthritis/psoriasis, and any red flags for infection.

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency. Do not delay urgent referral while waiting for blood test results.

Rheumatology (suspected inflammatory arthritis / persistent synovitis)

  • Refer for specialist opinion: any adult with suspected persistent synovitis of undetermined cause.
  • Refer urgently (even if CRP/ESR normal and/or RF/anti‑CCP negative) if any apply:
    • Small joints of hands or feet affected, or
    • More than 1 joint affected, or
    • Delay ≥3 months between symptom onset and seeking advice.

Rheumatology (suspected axial spondyloarthritis)

  • Low back pain started <45 years and lasted >3 months: refer if 4+ additional criteria are present:
    • back pain started <35 years
    • waking during the second half of the night because of symptoms
    • buttock pain
    • improvement with movement
    • improvement within 48 hours of NSAID
    • first-degree relative with spondyloarthritis
    • current/past arthritis
    • current/past enthesitis
    • current/past psoriasis
  • If exactly 3 additional criteria: do HLA‑B27; refer if positive.

Other referral triggers

  • Psoriatic arthritis suspected (psoriasis + inflammatory joint symptoms) → refer rheumatology.
  • Dactylitis → refer rheumatology.
  • Enthesitis without mechanical cause, persistent/multisite or with psoriasis/uveitis/IBD/infection/family history → refer rheumatology.
  • Acute anterior uveitis symptoms (eye pain, eye redness, photophobia, blurred vision) → same‑day ophthalmology assessment.

Who not to refer

  • Clear mechanical/MSK pain pattern without synovitis (manage in usual MSK pathway) unless inflammatory features develop or symptoms persist despite conservative measures.
  • Resolved, short‑lived self‑limiting arthralgia without synovitis and without inflammatory features (safety‑net).
