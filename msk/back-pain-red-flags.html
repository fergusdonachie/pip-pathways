Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Suspected cauda equina syndrome (CES) is a time‑critical emergency: arrange same‑day emergency assessment and urgent MRI via the local pathway (do not “watch and wait”).
  • Suspected MSCC / spinal metastases: NICE NG234 emphasises rapid recognition + urgent referral (local MSCC service / oncology / acute), because neurological outcome is time‑dependent.
  • Back pain without red flags: NICE NG59 discourages routine imaging in non‑specialist settings; focus on self‑management and appropriate MSK pathways.

1) NICE NG59 — low back pain & sciatica (over 16s)

  • Scope: assessment/management of non‑specific low back pain and sciatica.
  • Imaging: do not routinely offer imaging in a non‑specialist setting; consider imaging only in specialist settings if it is likely to change management.
  • Implication for a DGRefHelp “red flags” page: this page is primarily about excluding serious pathology and routing; once serious causes are unlikely, manage via usual MSK approach (keep active, appropriate analgesia, physio/MSK interface, etc.).

2) NICE NG234 — spinal metastases and metastatic spinal cord compression (MSCC)

  • Scope: recognition, referral, investigation and management of spinal metastases/MSCC.
  • Central message: outcomes depend on early recognition and rapid escalation; services should have a clear MSCC pathway.
  • Practical implication: back pain in a patient with known cancer (or features suggesting cancer) plus any neurological symptoms should trigger urgent same‑day escalation via the local MSCC route.

3) GIRFT National Suspected Cauda Equina Syndrome pathway (Jan 2026)

  • National pathway framing for suspected CES: emphasises rapid assessment, documentation of red‑flag symptoms, and urgent access to MRI (and spinal surgery opinion where indicated).
  • Practical implication: treat new bladder/bowel dysfunction or saddle sensory change in the context of back pain/sciatica as suspected CES until excluded.
Local agreement needed (drafting-site only)
  • Confirm the D&G suspected CES route: ED vs direct acute spinal/neurosurgical pathway; where the MRI is arranged (and expected timeframe).
  • Confirm the D&G MSCC route: single point of access number, oncology vs acute medicine vs ED, and whether there is an MSCC coordinator service.
  • Confirm where “urgent MRI spine” is available (DGRI vs tertiary centre) and how primary care should trigger it.
  • Confirm whether any “hot spine” red flags (infection/epidural abscess) should go via ED/acute medicine, and if there is a preferred receiving specialty.
This evidence summary is written for pathway design: it highlights only decision‑changing points and where local policy choices sit.

Back pain red flags (CES / cancer-MSCC / infection)

Background

  • Most back pain is non-specific and improves with time; the key task in primary care is to identify red flags for serious pathology and route appropriately.
  • Time-critical diagnoses to consider: cauda equina syndrome (CES), spinal metastases / metastatic spinal cord compression (MSCC), and spinal infection (e.g., epidural abscess/discitis).
  • If there are no red flags, routine imaging is not usually helpful in non-specialist settings (NICE NG59).

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • Quick triage questions (document clearly):
    • Bladder/bowel: new urinary retention, overflow incontinence, loss of bladder sensation, new faecal incontinence, loss of anal tone.
    • Saddle sensory change: numbness/altered sensation in perineum/genitals/buttocks (“saddle area”).
    • Leg neurology: new or progressive bilateral sciatica, weakness, gait disturbance.
    • Cancer/MSCC risk: known malignancy; unexplained weight loss; severe or progressive night/rest pain; thoracic pain; systemic symptoms.
    • Infection risk: fever/rigors; immunosuppression; IVDU; recent invasive spinal procedure; recent significant infection; new severe back pain with systemic upset.
  • Examination (minimum): vitals; neuro exam of lower limbs (power, reflexes, sensation); gait; perianal/saddle sensation if CES suspected; PR exam only if clinically indicated and appropriate.
  • If any CES/MSCC/infection red flags → treat as urgent same‑day escalation (see “Who to refer”).

Primary care management

  • If red flags present: do not delay escalation for routine investigations. Provide analgesia and arrange same-day urgent assessment via the appropriate pathway.
  • If no red flags and patient clinically stable:
    • Reassure and advise to keep active; provide self-management advice.
    • Analgesia as appropriate (follow local formulary; NSAID risks/contraindications; avoid dependence-forming medicines where possible).
    • Consider MSK/physio referral and safety-net for deterioration/red flags.
    • Imaging is not routinely indicated in non-specialist settings (NICE NG59).

Who to refer

Use the correct urgency. If you suspect CES or MSCC, treat as time critical.

Emergency (same day / blue-light if unstable)

  • Suspected cauda equina syndrome (CES): any of the following in the context of back pain/sciatica:
    • new urinary retention / overflow incontinence / loss of bladder sensation, or
    • new faecal incontinence / loss of anal tone, or
    • saddle/perineal sensory change, or
    • rapidly progressive or bilateral neurological deficit.
  • Route (draft): ED / acute assessment → urgent MRI spine ± spinal surgical opinion (follow D&G CES pathway).

Urgent same-day escalation

  • Suspected MSCC/spinal metastases (NICE NG234): severe or progressive back pain in a patient with known cancer, especially with neurological symptoms/signs → escalate via local MSCC route.
  • Suspected spinal infection: severe back pain + fever/systemic upset, immunosuppression/IVDU, or new neuro deficit → urgent acute assessment (often ED/acute medicine).

Routine MSK pathway

  • Back pain without serious red flags: refer to local MSK interface/physio as appropriate (NICE NG59 management principles).

Who not to refer

  • Non-specific low back pain without red flags or progressive neurological deficit — manage conservatively, consider MSK/physio, and safety-net.
  • Stable sciatica without CES features — manage per usual MSK pathway and review if worsening.
