DGRefHelp drafts / Head injury (adults)

Primary care triage after head injury: identify who needs 999 transfer, who needs ED assessment even if now well, and document the key handover metrics (GCS, anticoagulants, mechanism, symptoms since injury).
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑15

Evidence summary (NICE NG232 — community referral triggers)

Key design points (primary care)
  • NICE NG232 explicitly lists community/GP referral triggers for ED after head injury (these are broader than “need CT” triggers). Use them as a safe “send to ED” rule-set.
  • Particularly high-risk patterns for 999 include: reduced consciousness, focal neurology, suspected skull fracture/penetrating injury, post-traumatic seizure, and high-energy mechanism (NICE NG232).
  • Even if now alert, people should be referred to ED if there was any loss of consciousness, amnesia, persistent headache, vomiting, intoxication, coagulopathy, or use of anticoagulant or antiplatelet (excluding aspirin monotherapy) therapy (NICE NG232).
  • Imaging decisions are ED-led, but NICE NG232 CT criteria emphasise classic CT-within-1-hour red flags (GCS <15 at 2h, skull fracture signs, seizure, focal deficit, >1 vomiting episode) and an 8-hour window if LOC/amnesia plus risk factors (age ≥65, coagulopathy, dangerous mechanism, >30 min retrograde amnesia).

What to document (high yield)

  • GCS (and E/V/M components if not 15), plus pupils.
  • Whether there was LOC, amnesia, vomiting count, seizure, persistent headache.
  • Anticoagulant/antiplatelet use (excluding aspirin monotherapy), and last dose if known.
  • Mechanism (dangerous/high-energy) + time since injury.
  • Any focal neurological deficit and whether improving/worsening.
  • Safeguarding concerns / intoxication.

Head injury — adults (primary care triage)

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Unconsciousness or lack of full consciousness (e.g. cannot keep eyes open).
  • Focal neurological deficit since the injury.
  • Suspected complex skull fracture or penetrating head injury.
  • Seizure since the injury.
  • High-energy head injury (e.g. high-speed RTC; significant fall) — treat as high risk.
  • No other safe way to transport to ED (practical 999 trigger).

ED referral needed (same day)

  • Any loss of consciousness because of the injury (even if now fully recovered).
  • Amnesia for events before/after the injury.
  • Persistent headache since the injury.
  • Any vomiting episodes since the injury.
  • Previous brain surgery.
  • Bleeding/clotting disorder.
  • Anticoagulant treatment (warfarin/DOAC/heparin/LMWH) OR antiplatelet treatment (excluding aspirin monotherapy).
  • Drug/alcohol intoxication (do not assume symptoms are due to intoxication alone).
  • Safeguarding concerns (including suspected assault / vulnerable adult / domestic violence / non-accidental injury concerns).
  • Continuing professional concern about the diagnosis or ability to safely observe at home.

What to do before transfer (do not delay)

  • Observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air.
  • Neurological status: GCS (document E/V/M if <15), pupils, focal deficit exam.
  • History: time of injury; mechanism; LOC/amnesia; vomiting count; seizure; headache; neck pain.
  • Antithrombotics: identify anticoagulant/antiplatelet and last dose if known.
  • If ambulance not required, ensure they are accompanied by a competent adult for transport.

Who to refer (summary)

  • 999 if any “Immediate red flags”.
  • ED same day if any “ED referral needed” criteria.
