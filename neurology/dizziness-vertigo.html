Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Acute vestibular syndrome (sudden-onset continuous vertigo + nausea/vomiting + gait unsteadiness): NICE NG127 says HINTS (if trained) to help exclude stroke; otherwise treat as possible posterior circulation stroke and refer immediately.
  • Transient rotational vertigo triggered by head movement: NICE NG127 says do Dix–Hallpike (if trained) and treat BPPV with a canalith repositioning manoeuvre (e.g., Epley) if safe (no unstable cervical spine disease).
  • Central red flags: CKS emphasises mismatch with peripheral patterns, focal neuro signs, central-type nystagmus, or inability to stand/walk even with eyes open → urgent assessment (stroke pathway unless clear alternative).

1) NICE NG127: suspected neurological conditions — dizziness/vertigo

  • Sudden-onset dizziness + focal neuro deficit (examples include vertical/rotatory nystagmus, new unsteadiness, new deafness): exclude hypoglycaemia (if diabetes), then if BPPV/postural hypotension don’t explain → refer immediately to exclude posterior circulation stroke (NG128 pathway).
  • Acute vestibular syndrome: perform HINTS if trained/experienced.
  • After HINTS: a negative HINTS makes stroke very unlikely; if HINTS indicates stroke (normal head impulse, direction-changing nystagmus, or skew deviation) → refer immediately for neuroimaging.
  • If HINTS not available and BPPV/postural hypotension don’t account → refer immediately via local stroke pathways.
  • Positional vertigo: offer Dix–Hallpike (Hallpike) if trained; if BPPV diagnosed offer Epley (or other canalith manoeuvre) if trained and no unstable cervical spine disease; otherwise refer per local pathway.
  • Vestibular migraine: consider when episodes last 5 minutes to 72 hours plus history of recurrent headache.

2) NICE CKS (overview summaries): peripheral vs central framing

  • Peripheral vertigo is more common (e.g., BPPV, vestibular neuronitis, labyrinthitis, Ménière’s disease).
  • Central vertigo is uncommon but serious (brainstem/cerebellar pathology including stroke/TIA).
  • Features increasing suspicion of a central cause include: prolonged severe vertigo; new headache or recent trauma; focal neurological symptoms/signs; central-type nystagmus; abnormal response to Dix–Hallpike; and inability to stand/walk even with eyes open.

3) NICE CKS: quick condition discriminators (useful for primary care triage)

  • BPPV: brief episodes triggered by head position; Dix–Hallpike supportive; imaging not needed unless atypical; treat with Epley; drug therapy usually not helpful; review if not resolved by ~4 weeks.
  • Vestibular neuronitis: acute spontaneous prolonged vertigo (peripheral), no hearing loss/tinnitus, no focal neuro symptoms; severe symptoms often 2–3 days then gradual recovery over weeks; consider short-term antiemetic/vestibular suppressant if severe; refer/admit if atypical or not improving.
  • Labyrinthitis: similar but hearing loss present.
  • Ménière’s disease: recurrent episodes lasting 20 minutes to hours plus fluctuating hearing loss/tinnitus/aural fullness; needs ENT/audiology confirmation; consider short course antiemetic during acute attacks; consider betahistine trial to reduce attack frequency (local practice varies).

Dizziness/vertigo

Background

  • Dizziness is common; true rotational vertigo is less common and suggests a vestibular disorder.
  • Two priorities: (1) exclude posterior circulation stroke in acute presentations, and (2) identify BPPV (brief positional episodes) which is treatable in primary care if trained.
  • If symptoms do not fit a peripheral pattern, or there are focal neurological signs, treat as possible central cause until proven otherwise.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • First: are they unstable? Severe vomiting with dehydration, collapse, chest pain/arrhythmia symptoms, or severe headache → urgent assessment.
  • Clarify the symptom:
    • Rotational vertigo (spinning) vs lightheadedness/presyncope vs imbalance.
    • Timing: continuous (hours–days) vs episodic; duration of episodes.
    • Triggers: head movement/position change (suggests BPPV); standing up (orthostatic).
    • Associated features: hearing loss/tinnitus/aural fullness; migraine history; infection prodrome; focal neurological symptoms (speech, limb weakness, diplopia, dysarthria, ataxia).
  • Examination (minimum): obs; glucose if diabetic/concern; ear exam; gait; neuro exam (cranial nerves, limb power/sensation, cerebellar signs).
  • Nystagmus clues: vertical or direction-changing nystagmus, new deafness, or new unsteadiness are concerning for central cause.
  • Acute vestibular syndrome (sudden-onset continuous vertigo + nausea/vomiting + gait unsteadiness):
    • If a trained clinician is available, perform HINTS.
    • If HINTS indicates stroke (normal head impulse, direction-changing nystagmus, or skew deviation) → refer immediately for neuroimaging.
    • If HINTS not available and BPPV/postural hypotension do not explain → refer immediately via local stroke pathway.
  • Positional vertigo (brief episodes triggered by head movement): if trained, do Dix–Hallpike to check for BPPV.

Primary care management

  • BPPV likely/confirmed (brief triggered episodes, typical Dix–Hallpike):
    • Offer canalith repositioning (e.g., Epley) if trained and no unstable cervical spine disease.
    • Consider Brandt–Daroff exercises if appropriate.
    • Drug therapy is not usually helpful for BPPV.
    • Safety‑net: review if not resolved by ~4 weeks or if symptoms are atypical/worsening.
  • Vestibular neuronitis / labyrinthitis likely (acute prolonged vertigo; peripheral signs; no focal neuro signs):
    • Encourage early mobilisation as tolerated (promotes central compensation) + falls advice.
    • If severe nausea/vomiting: consider short course vestibular suppressant/antiemetic (short term only).
    • Hearing loss suggests labyrinthitis (consider ENT/audiology depending on severity/timeline).
  • Vestibular migraine possible (episodes 5 min–72 h + migraine history): manage as migraine and consider neurology if diagnostic uncertainty or significant impairment.
  • General safety advice: driving/work at heights, machinery, falls risk; document safety‑net and when to seek urgent help.

Who to refer

If anything is atypical, or doesn’t fit a benign peripheral pattern, escalate early (posterior circulation stroke is easy to miss).

Refer immediately (treat as possible posterior circulation stroke)

  • Sudden-onset dizziness/vertigo with focal neurological deficit (e.g., vertical/rotatory nystagmus, new unsteadiness/ataxia, diplopia/dysarthria, new deafness), and BPPV/postural hypotension do not explain.
  • Acute vestibular syndrome:
    • If trained HINTS is available and indicates stroke (normal head impulse, direction-changing nystagmus, or skew deviation).
    • If HINTS is not available and BPPV/postural hypotension do not account.
  • Unable to stand/walk even with eyes open, or other features strongly suggesting central vertigo.
  • Route (draft): local stroke pathway (999/ED/SDEC per D&G agreement).

Refer to balance/ENT/audiovestibular service (routine/urgent depending on severity)

  • BPPV suspected but Dix–Hallpike/Epley expertise is not available, or manoeuvres are unsafe (e.g., cervical spine instability).
  • Symptoms or signs are atypical (diagnostic uncertainty, atypical nystagmus, additional neurological symptoms).
  • Persistent symptoms not improving after ~1 week (vestibular neuronitis) or not resolved by ~4–6 weeks (BPPV/other).
  • Ménière’s disease suspected (needs ENT + formal audiology confirmation).
  • Route (draft): SCI Gateway → ENT/audiovestibular/balance clinic (local naming to confirm).

Who not to refer

  • Typical BPPV that responds to canalith repositioning manoeuvre, with no red flags.
  • Dizziness without imbalance or other focal neurological deficit is unlikely to indicate a serious neurological condition (safety‑net if symptoms persist/worsen).
