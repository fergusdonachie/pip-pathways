DGRefHelp drafts / First suspected seizure / seizure recurrence

Primary care framing: identify emergencies (ongoing seizure, persistent reduced consciousness, injury, focal deficit, pregnancy/eclampsia, infection/meningism). For a first suspected seizure or recurrence after remission, do a focused workup (incl. 12‑lead ECG and glucose) and arrange urgent specialist assessment within 2 weeks.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑11

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • Urgent specialist assessment within 2 weeks after a first suspected seizure, and also after seizure recurrence following a period of remission (NICE NG217).
  • For adults after a first suspected seizure: refer to a clinician with expertise in assessing first seizures and diagnosing epilepsy (NICE NG217; NICE QS211).
  • Do a 12‑lead ECG in the evaluation of a first suspected seizure / TLoC to identify cardiac mimics (NICE NG217; NICE CG109).
  • Be aware of metabolic causes (notably hypoglycaemia) that can present with seizures (NICE NG217; NICE CG109).

First suspected seizure / seizure recurrence — initial actions (primary care)

Immediate triage (is this an emergency?)

  • Ongoing seizure (or repeated seizures without recovery) → 999 / ED now (status epilepticus risk).
  • Not back to baseline (persistent reduced consciousness, ongoing confusion, or new focal neurology) → 999 / ED now.
  • Severe injury (head injury, suspected fracture, significant bleeding) during the episode → urgent assessment (often ED).
  • Suspected CNS infection (fever, meningism, severe headache) → 999 / ED now.
  • Pregnancy/post‑partum with seizure (consider eclampsia) → 999 / ED now.
  • Hypoglycaemia or other major metabolic derangement suspected → treat/transfer urgently.

If now well: key history to document (and send with referral)

  • Before: trigger (sleep deprivation, alcohol/withdrawal, illness), posture, prodrome (light-headedness, sweating, warmth), déjà vu/jamais vu, aura.
  • During (witness): eyes open/closed, colour, head turning, unusual posturing, duration, limb jerking duration, incontinence, tongue biting (side vs tip).
  • After: confusion duration, amnesia, focal weakness (Todd’s paresis), headache, myalgia, injuries.
  • Past risk factors: prior episodes, stroke/TIA, head injury, CNS infection, learning disability, alcohol/substance use, medication changes.

Focused exam + baseline tests (do not delay urgent referral if unstable)

  • Observations: HR, BP (consider lying/standing if syncope possible), RR, Temp, SpO2.
  • Neuro exam (document normality or focal signs).
  • Capillary glucose (exclude hypoglycaemia).
  • 12‑lead ECG (cardiac mimics/arrhythmia; follow NICE CG109 red flags for urgent cardio review if abnormal).
  • Consider bloods if available and appropriate: U&Es (Na), Ca, FBC, LFTs; pregnancy test where relevant.

Who to refer / where to send

  • 999 / ED: ongoing seizure; not recovered to baseline; persistent focal deficit; suspected infection; pregnancy/post‑partum seizure; major injury.
  • Urgent specialist assessment (within 2 weeks): first suspected seizure OR seizure recurrence after remission (NICE NG217).
  • Urgent cardiovascular assessment: if presentation is TLoC with ECG abnormalities or other CG109 red flags (e.g. exertional TLoC, heart failure signs, family history sudden cardiac death <40).

Advice while awaiting assessment

  • Driving: advise the person to stop driving immediately and tell DVLA (GOV.UK).
  • Safety: avoid swimming/bathing alone, heights, machinery; consider sleep and alcohol moderation; provide seizure first-aid advice for family/carers (NICE NG217 info/support after first seizure).
  • Safety net: if another seizure occurs, or they develop red flags (persistent confusion, severe headache/fever, injury) → seek urgent help / 999 as appropriate.

Referral‑letter minimum (suggested)

  • Clear event narrative + witness account + duration + recovery phase.
  • Exam + obs; capillary glucose.
  • ECG result (attach/describe) and any red-flag features.
  • PMH risk factors and current meds (incl. substance/alcohol history where relevant).

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • Local referral route for first seizure clinic / neurology (eRS vs phone; who accepts referrals; expected information/bloods/ECG).
  • Local same-day options for “now well” but concerning story (acute medicine/SDEC vs ED).
  • Local access to urgent EEG/neuroimaging (who requests; what indications trigger same-day imaging).
  • Local patient information leaflets (seizure first aid + safety advice + DVLA wording).
