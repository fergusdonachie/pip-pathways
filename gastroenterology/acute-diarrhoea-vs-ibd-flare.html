DGRefHelp drafts / Acute diarrhoea vs IBD flare

Primary care triage for sudden diarrhoea: rapidly identify dehydration/sepsis/acute abdomen, decide when to send stool tests (incl. C. difficile), and recognise red flags for acute severe ulcerative colitis or complicated Crohn’s disease that need same‑day hospital assessment. Includes “don’t miss” points for patients already on steroids/biologics.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑11

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • Start with physiology, not labels: acute diarrhoea can be infectious, medication-related (e.g. metformin/PPIs/antibiotics), or an IBD flare; the immediate risk is dehydration, sepsis, or acute abdomen → use same‑day referral thresholds early (Patient.info).
  • Stool testing is targeted: send stool culture when the person is unwell (fever/dehydration), immunocompromised, has blood/pus, recent antibiotics/hospital exposure (request C. difficile testing), uncertainty/persistence (>1 week), or high‑risk travel (Patient.info).
  • C. difficile is a “don’t miss” cause of acute diarrhoea after antibiotics or healthcare exposure. NICE NG199 defines CDI as diarrhoea with a positive toxin test, or toxin test pending with clinical suspicion; management includes review/stop non‑essential antibiotics, manage fluids, avoid antimotility drugs, and refer to hospital if severely unwell/worsening (NICE NG199).
  • IBD flare requires infection exclusion + severity recognition: NICE Crohn’s (NG129) and UC (NG130) describe steroid use to induce remission for inflammatory exacerbations, but in undifferentiated acute diarrhoea (esp. with fever or recent antibiotics) clinicians should be cautious and exclude infection/C. difficile and/or seek specialist advice before escalating immunosuppression.
  • Avoid loperamide in high‑risk situations: it is generally for nonspecific watery diarrhoea but should be avoided in bloody diarrhoea or diarrhoea with fever, and in suspected/confirmed C. difficile or ulcerative colitis where toxic megacolon is a concern (Patient.info; NICE NG199).

Acute diarrhoea vs IBD flare — triage + first tests (adults)

Immediate red flags (same‑day ED / acute assessment / SDEC)

  • Severe dehydration / shock (confusion, SBP <90, anuria/oliguria, marked tachycardia, collapse).
  • Unable to keep fluids down (persistent vomiting) → admission risk (Patient.info).
  • Suspected sepsis (systemically unwell, rigors, hypotension, worsening trajectory).
  • Acute abdomen: severe or localised abdominal pain/tenderness, guarding, distension, peritonism, suspected obstruction/toxic megacolon.
  • Significant GI bleeding (large-volume PR blood, syncope, Hb drop features).
  • Known IBD with severe flare features (see below) or immunosuppressed with acute diarrhoea.

Step 1 — define the problem quickly

  • Onset + duration; stool frequency; blood/mucus; nocturnal symptoms; tenesmus.
  • Associated features: fever, severe cramping, vomiting, weight loss, recent travel, sick contacts, suspected food poisoning.
  • Medication + exposures: recent antibiotics, recent hospital stay/care home, PPIs, metformin, laxatives; NSAID use; new meds.
  • IBD context (if known): diagnosis (UC vs Crohn’s), usual baseline, prior severe flares, surgery/stoma, current meds (steroids/thiopurines/methotrexate/biologics/JAKs), and existing rescue plan / contact route (IBD nurse line).
  • Assess hydration and observations: HR, BP (incl postural), temperature; consider NEWS2 if used locally.

Step 2 — treat/support while you investigate

  • Oral rehydration advice; consider same‑day IV fluids if moderate/severe dehydration or unable to maintain intake.
  • Safety‑net: return/seek urgent care if worsening, not improving, signs of dehydration, new severe pain, or increasing blood.
  • Avoid loperamide if bloody diarrhoea, diarrhoea with fever/systemic upset, suspected/confirmed C. difficile, or suspected severe ulcerative colitis (Patient.info; NICE NG199).

Step 3 — choose investigations (targeted)

  • Bloods (if unwell, older/frail, IBD, blood in stool, or concern re dehydration/AKI): FBC, U&E, CRP, LFTs; consider magnesium; consider venous blood gas/lactate if very unwell (local).
  • Stool tests (send when any of the following apply) (Patient.info):
    • Unwell (fever/dehydration) or severe symptoms.
    • Blood or pus in stool.
    • Immunocompromised (incl. high‑dose steroids, biologics, chemo, advanced HIV, transplant).
    • Recent antibiotics or hospital admission → request C. difficile testing specifically.
    • Persistent diarrhoea (>1 week) or diagnosis uncertain.
    • High‑risk travel (outside Western Europe/North America/Aus/NZ) → include ova/cysts/parasites per local lab advice.
  • Faecal calprotectin: consider when distinguishing IBD activity from functional symptoms in a stable patient; in an acute severe presentation, don’t delay referral for calprotectin (local agreement).

Recognise “likely IBD flare” vs “likely infection” (practical clues)

  • More suggestive of infectious gastroenteritis: sudden onset, prominent vomiting, sick contacts/outbreak, travel/food exposure, short course (<7 days), fever.
  • More suggestive of IBD flare: known IBD; gradual worsening over days–weeks; nocturnal diarrhoea; blood/mucus, urgency/tenesmus; weight loss; extra‑intestinal symptoms; recent NSAID use; medication non‑adherence.
  • But: infection and flare can coexist (and C. difficile can mimic a flare), especially after antibiotics or in immunosuppressed patients → have a low threshold to test and to refer.

Known ulcerative colitis — identify acute severe disease (same‑day hospital assessment)

  • If suspected acute severe UC (classically: ≥6 bloody stools/day plus systemic toxicity such as tachycardia, fever, anaemia, raised inflammatory markers — based on Truelove & Witts severity categories referenced by NICE NG130) → same‑day admission / acute gastro review (local route).
  • Red flags for complications: severe abdominal pain/distension, peritonism, systemic toxicity → consider toxic megacolon/perforation and refer urgently.

Known Crohn’s disease — complicated flare red flags (same‑day assessment)

  • Severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, abdominal distension → consider obstruction/stricture.
  • High fever/rigors, focal tenderness, raised CRP with severe pain → consider abscess or perforation.
  • Perianal pain/swelling with fever → consider perianal sepsis.
  • Any severe flare in a patient on biologics/high‑dose steroids → lower threshold for hospital assessment.

Suspected C. difficile infection (community)

  • Send stool sample for C. difficile testing when compatible symptoms + recent antibiotics/hospital exposure (Patient.info; NICE NG199).
  • Review and stop non‑essential antibiotics; review PPIs/laxatives and medicines that worsen dehydration risk (NSAIDs, ACEi/ARB, diuretics) (NICE NG199).
  • Do not use antimotility drugs (e.g. loperamide) (NICE NG199).
  • Consider prompt specialist advice before starting CDI antibiotics in the community (NICE NG199) (local route: microbiology / ID / gastro).
  • Refer to hospital if severely unwell or rapidly worsening (NICE NG199).

Who to refer / where to send (D&G placeholders)

  • ED / acute medicine / SDEC (same day): any red flags; suspected acute severe UC; complicated Crohn’s flare; severe dehydration; suspected severe CDI; immunosuppressed patient with acute diarrhoea and systemic symptoms.
  • Urgent gastro/IBD team advice (same day/next day): stable known IBD with worsening symptoms but no red flags; need to enact a rescue plan; guidance on steroids vs infection exclusion.
  • Routine follow-up: mild, improving watery diarrhoea with no red flags and good hydration; provide safety net and review if persists >1 week.

Referral-letter minimum (suggested)

  • Stool frequency/day; blood/mucus; duration; fever; vomiting; hydration status/observations.
  • IBD diagnosis and current meds (include last biologic dose; current steroid dose); prior severe flares/surgery/stoma.
  • Recent antibiotics/hospital exposure; key comorbidities (CKD, frailty, pregnancy).
  • Results sent/pending: FBC/U&E/CRP; stool culture; C. difficile test; calprotectin (if relevant).

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • Local same‑day routing for suspected acute severe UC/complicated Crohn’s (ED vs SDEC vs direct gastro admission).
  • Primary care access to stool testing (PCR panel vs culture), how to request C. difficile toxin, and expected turnaround times.
  • Whether GPs should initiate rescue oral steroids for known IBD flares, and under what “infection excluded / specialist advice” conditions.
  • How to access IBD nurse advice lines and what the default patient instructions should be (number/email; hours; what to do out of hours).
  • Local use of faecal calprotectin in known IBD relapse assessment vs non‑IBD diarrhoea.
