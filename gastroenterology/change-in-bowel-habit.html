DGRefHelp drafts / Change in bowel habit

Adults with unexplained new/persistent change in bowel habit: triage red flags, do DRE + baseline tests, use FIT to guide suspected colorectal cancer referral, and safety‑net negative/non‑returned FIT.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑11

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • NICE NG12 / NICE HTG690: offer quantitative FIT (HM‑JACKarc or OC‑Sensor) to guide suspected colorectal cancer referral in adults with change in bowel habit (and several other lower‑GI presentations).
  • Referral threshold: refer on the suspected colorectal cancer pathway if FIT ≥10 µg Hb/g faeces (NG12; HTG690).
  • Don’t delay referral when there is strong clinical concern (e.g. abdominal mass, ongoing unexplained symptoms) even if FIT is negative / not returned; ensure safety‑netting is in place for FIT <10 or non‑return (NG12; HTG690).
  • Rectal mass (and unexplained anal mass/ulceration) does not need FIT before referral is considered; NG12 advises considering suspected cancer pathway referral for rectal mass.
  • ACPGBI/BSG guidance supports FIT use in symptomatic patients, and emphasises that negative FIT does not equal “no cancer”; safety‑netting and reassessment is required for persistent symptoms and for high‑risk clinical features.

Change in bowel habit — adults (suspected colorectal cancer framing)

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Suspected bowel obstruction: severe colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, absolute constipation, distension, peritonism.
  • Severe lower GI bleeding with haemodynamic compromise, syncope, or ongoing large-volume bleeding.
  • Sepsis / acute abdomen.

Urgent suspected cancer pathway (do not wait for FIT)

  • Rectal mass on DRE (NICE NG12: consider suspected cancer pathway referral).
  • Unexplained abdominal mass (NICE NG12 / HTG690: FIT can be offered, but referral should not be delayed if clinical concern is high).

Step 1 — define the problem (is it truly “unexplained” and persistent?)

  • Clarify change: new change from baseline (stool frequency/form, urgency/tenesmus, diarrhoea vs constipation, alternating pattern, nocturnal symptoms).
  • Duration: persistent / recurrent; document start date.
  • Associated symptoms: PR bleeding, weight loss, abdominal pain, mucus, fevers, anaemia symptoms, family history of colorectal cancer/polyposis.
  • Medication and reversible causes: antibiotics (C. diff risk), metformin, PPIs, opioids, iron, laxatives, dietary change, new alcohol excess; recent travel/infective contacts.

Step 2 — examination (don’t skip)

  • Abdominal exam: masses, focal tenderness, distension.
  • Digital rectal examination (DRE): rectal mass; stool/bleeding; fissure/haemorrhoids (document clearly).
  • If symptoms include PR bleeding: also use the separate PR bleeding page for triage and baseline tests.

Step 3 — baseline investigations (typical primary care set)

  • FBC (anaemia) and ferritin/iron studies if anaemia suspected.
  • Consider: U&Es/eGFR, LFTs, CRP (if inflammatory features), coeliac serology where appropriate, and stool tests if infective diarrhoea suspected.

Step 4 — use FIT to guide suspected colorectal cancer referral (NICE NG12 / HTG690)

  • Offer quantitative FIT (HM‑JACKarc or OC‑Sensor) in adults with unexplained change in bowel habit (plus other NG12/HTG690 indications).
  • FIT ≥10 µg Hb/g faeces → refer using suspected colorectal cancer pathway (2WW equivalent) (NICE NG12 / HTG690).
  • FIT <10 µg Hb/g OR sample not returned:
    • Ensure safety‑netting (planned review, clear return precautions, and a pathway for escalation).
    • Do not delay referral if strong clinical concern persists (e.g. mass, ongoing unexplained symptoms, significant weight loss) (NICE NG12 / HTG690).

Pragmatic safety-net triggers (examples)

  • Reassess promptly if symptoms persist/worsen despite negative FIT, especially if:
    • new/persistent iron‑deficiency anaemia
    • weight loss
    • progressive pain, or any palpable mass
    • recurrent PR bleeding
    • strong family history / high clinical concern
  • Consider (local policy): repeat FIT after an interval in persistent symptoms, and/or referral via an alternative pathway (e.g. non‑specific symptoms, direct access imaging) when cancer remains plausible.

Who to refer / where to send (D&G placeholders)

  • 999 / ED: obstruction, severe bleeding, acute abdomen, sepsis.
  • Suspected colorectal cancer pathway: FIT ≥10 µg Hb/g; rectal mass; or strong clinical concern (don’t delay for FIT).
  • Routine gastro/colorectal / alternative pathway: persistent symptoms with repeatedly negative FIT where non-malignant causes are more likely, but symptoms still need secondary-care assessment (local route).
  • Advice & guidance: uncertainty about FIT use, thresholds, repeat testing, or best next investigation (colonoscopy vs CT colonography) when FIT is negative but concern remains.

Referral-letter minimum (suggested)

  • Exact symptom description (what changed), onset date, persistence, associated PR bleeding/weight loss/pain.
  • Exam findings: abdomen + DRE (and whether rectal mass present).
  • Results: FIT value (with date) and baseline bloods (FBC ± ferritin/iron studies ± CRP).
  • PMH and meds relevant to bleeding risk (anticoagulants/antiplatelets) and GI symptoms.

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • What is the local default pathway for FIT ≥10: colorectal 2WW clinic vs direct-access colonoscopy vs CT colonography triage?
  • Local position on repeat FIT (when and for whom) if FIT <10 but symptoms persist.
  • What pathway is used for negative FIT but ongoing high clinical concern (non‑specific symptoms route? direct access imaging? A&G with colorectal?)
  • Expected baseline tests before referral (FBC + ferritin? CRP? coeliac serology?) and any local proformas.
  • Handling of patients on anticoagulants/antiplatelets: any local advice for FIT interpretation or referral thresholds?
Safety net
A negative FIT reduces colorectal cancer probability but does not eliminate it. If symptoms persist or clinical concern remains high, reassess and escalate via the appropriate pathway.
