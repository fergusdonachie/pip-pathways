DGRefHelp drafts / Abnormal LFTs

Primary care approach to abnormal liver blood tests: recognise urgent/synthetic failure, take a focused history/exam, interpret the pattern (hepatitic vs cholestatic vs isolated bilirubin), request a standard liver aetiology screen (BSG/BASL 2018), and risk‑stratify NAFLD/ARLD for fibrosis (FIB‑4/NFS → second‑line ELF/FibroScan; NICE NG49 + BSG).
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑11

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • Don’t “watch and wait” abnormal LFTs without context: BSG recommends people with abnormal liver blood tests should be considered for a liver aetiology screen irrespective of level/duration; the degree of abnormality does not reliably indicate severity (BSG/BASL 2018).
  • Use a standard liver aetiology screen to reduce missed chronic liver disease: in adults this includes abdominal ultrasound, hepatitis B surface antigen, hepatitis C antibody (PCR if positive), autoimmune markers (AMA/ASMA/ANA) + immunoglobulins, and iron overload tests (ferritin + transferrin saturation) (BSG/BASL 2018).
  • Risk‑stratify NAFLD for fibrosis rather than using “how high the ALT is”: first‑line non‑invasive fibrosis assessment via FIB‑4 or NAFLD fibrosis score (BSG/BASL 2018). NICE NG49 recommends assessing for advanced fibrosis in people with NAFLD and considers the ELF test; ELF ≥10.51 supports advanced fibrosis and warrants specialist referral (NICE NG49).
  • Escalate urgently when there is marked derangement, synthetic failure (e.g. raised INR, low albumin) and/or concerning symptoms/signs (BSG/BASL 2018 pathway figure) — exact local routes vary (ED/SDEC/acute med/gastro/hepatology).
  • Alcohol-related liver disease (ARLD): BSG recommends considering referral to alcohol services for dependency (AUDIT >19) and using fibrosis assessment; referral to secondary care if evidence of advanced disease or FibroScan >16 kPa (if available) (BSG/BASL 2018).

Abnormal LFTs — structured approach + referral triggers (adults)

Immediate red flags (same‑day emergency assessment / ED)

  • Acute severe illness + abnormal LFTs (sepsis/shock/hypoxia) → manage as acute medical emergency.
  • Jaundice with systemic upset, hypotension, fever/rigors, RUQ pain (possible cholangitis) → urgent hospital assessment.
  • Suspected acute liver failure / synthetic failure: confusion/encephalopathy, INR raised, hypoglycaemia, rapidly rising bilirubin, new ascites, or bleeding/bruising → urgent hospital assessment.
  • Suspected malignant obstruction (painless progressive jaundice, weight loss) → urgent same‑day assessment/urgent imaging route (local).
  • Marked derangement of liver enzymes with symptoms (or very high ALT/AST) → discuss urgently / refer same day (local thresholds; see “Local agreement needed”).

Step 1 — confirm + contextualise (don’t interpret in isolation)

  • Review previous LFTs and the clinical context (BSG).
  • Repeat if a clear transient cause is likely (e.g. viral illness, recent strenuous exercise affecting AST) but don’t delay aetiology screen if persistent/unexplained (BSG).
  • Check whether the panel includes: bilirubin, albumin, ALT, ALP, GGT + FBC (BSG recommendation for initial panel).

Step 2 — focused history + examination

  • Symptoms: jaundice, pruritus, RUQ pain, fever, weight loss, fatigue, anorexia, pale stools/dark urine.
  • Alcohol: units/week + pattern; use AUDIT where relevant.
  • Metabolic risk: BMI, waist, T2DM, dyslipidaemia, hypertension (NAFLD risk).
  • Drug history: prescribed/OTC/herbal; new drugs in last 3 months; statins, antibiotics, antiepileptics, methotrexate, etc.
  • Viral hepatitis risks: IVDU, transfusion abroad, tattoos/piercings, sexual risk, country of birth/exposure, household contacts.
  • Family history: haemochromatosis, autoimmune disease, liver disease.
  • Exam: jaundice, scratch marks, hepatosplenomegaly, ascites, peripheral oedema, stigmata of chronic liver disease.

Step 3 — identify the pattern (helps choose next tests/referral urgency)

  • Hepatitic pattern: ALT/AST predominant.
  • Cholestatic pattern: ALP (± GGT) predominant; think biliary obstruction, cholangitis, PBC/PSC, drug‑induced cholestasis.
  • Isolated raised bilirubin with normal ALT/ALP: consider Gilbert’s (unconjugated) vs haemolysis; check fractionation, haemolysis screen if indicated.
  • Low albumin / thrombocytopenia (or raised INR) may indicate advanced/chronic liver disease (synthetic/portal hypertension) → lower threshold to refer.

Step 4 — minimum investigation set (standard adult liver aetiology screen)

  • Abdominal ultrasound (assess fatty liver, biliary dilation/obstruction, masses, cirrhotic features) (BSG).
  • Viral hepatitis: HBsAg; HCV antibody (PCR if positive) (BSG).
  • Autoimmune: AMA, ASMA, ANA + serum immunoglobulins (BSG).
  • Iron overload: ferritin + transferrin saturation (BSG).
  • Also consider (case‑dependent): HbA1c/lipids, coeliac screen, CK (if isolated AST), TFTs, caeruloplasmin/alpha‑1 antitrypsin (usually specialist‑directed), pregnancy test.

Step 5 — NAFLD / metabolic risk pathway (fibrosis risk‑stratify)

  • If fatty liver/metabolic risk factors and no alternative cause: diagnose likely NAFLD (after excluding significant alcohol and other causes).
  • Risk‑stratify for advanced fibrosis:
    • First line: calculate FIB‑4 (or NAFLD fibrosis score) (BSG).
    • Second line: if intermediate/high risk → ELF or FibroScan/ARFI (BSG). NICE NG49: consider ELF; ELF ≥10.51 indicates advanced fibrosis → refer to hepatology.
  • Management basics (primary care): weight loss/lifestyle support; optimise metabolic comorbidities; statins should generally continue (NICE NG49; only consider stopping if enzymes double within 3 months of starting).

Step 6 — alcohol pathway (ARLD risk)

  • Quantify alcohol use; consider AUDIT.
  • If alcohol dependency (e.g. AUDIT >19) → consider referral to alcohol services (BSG).
  • Risk‑stratify harmful drinkers for fibrosis if available (FibroScan/ARFI); consider secondary care referral if advanced disease suspected or FibroScan >16 kPa (BSG).

Who to refer / where to send (D&G placeholders)

  • ED / acute medicine (same day): red flags above; suspected cholangitis/obstruction with systemic features; suspected synthetic failure/encephalopathy.
  • Urgent imaging/surgical/gastro (local): cholestatic pattern with suspected obstruction; ultrasound showing biliary obstruction or suspicious mass.
  • Routine/urgent hepatology/gastro referral:
    • Abnormal LFTs with positive aetiology screen (viral hepatitis, autoimmune markers + raised Ig, iron overload markers) (BSG).
    • NAFLD with advanced fibrosis on non‑invasive testing (e.g. ELF ≥10.51) (NICE NG49).
    • Evidence of advanced liver disease/portal hypertension (low platelets, low albumin, raised INR, splenomegaly/ascites, nodular liver on USS).
    • Persistent unexplained abnormal liver blood tests despite negative extended screen and no NAFLD risk factors → refer/discuss for further evaluation (BSG).

Referral-letter minimum (suggested)

  • LFT panel values + trend (ALT/AST/ALP/GGT/bilirubin/albumin) + INR if done; FBC incl. platelets.
  • Alcohol history (units/week + AUDIT if done); metabolic risk (BMI, T2DM, BP, lipids).
  • Full meds list incl. OTC/herbals + recent changes.
  • Aetiology screen results (HBsAg, HCV Ab ± PCR, AMA/ASMA/ANA, Ig, ferritin + transferrin saturation) and ultrasound report.
  • Fibrosis risk score (FIB‑4/NFS) and any ELF/FibroScan/ARFI result if available.

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • What counts as “marked derangement” in primary care for same‑day referral (e.g. ALT/AST >X, ALP >X, bilirubin >X) and what the default route is (ED vs SDEC vs direct specialty advice).
  • How to access/commission second‑line fibrosis testing (ELF vs FibroScan/ARFI), including cut‑offs used locally and referral destinations.
  • Local pathway for cholestatic LFTs: urgent ultrasound timing and which service receives suspected obstruction/cholangitis referrals.
  • Whether primary care should request the full standard liver aetiology screen up front, or whether some elements are added only after first specialist triage.
  • Local policy for statin monitoring and thresholds for interruption/rechallenge in people with abnormal baseline LFTs.
