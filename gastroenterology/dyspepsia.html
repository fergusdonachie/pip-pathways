DGRefHelp drafts / Dyspepsia

Adults with dyspepsia/reflux: identify emergency red flags, apply NG12 suspected cancer pathway triggers (dysphagia; ≥55 + weight loss + epigastric pain/reflux/dyspepsia), use NICE CG184 first‑line management (PPI and/or H. pylori test‑and‑treat), and safety‑net persistent symptoms.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑11

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • Suspected cancer pathway referral (NICE NG12): refer for oesophageal cancer if dysphagia, or if age ≥55 + weight loss plus any of: upper abdominal pain, reflux, or dyspepsia. The same trigger applies for stomach cancer in NG12.
  • Non-urgent direct-access endoscopy (NG12): consider for haematemesis, and for age ≥55 with treatment‑resistant dyspepsia (and several other symptom/test combinations such as low Hb with upper abdominal pain; thrombocytosis plus upper GI symptoms; or nausea/vomiting plus weight loss/reflux/dyspepsia/upper abdominal pain).
  • Same-day specialist assessment (CG184): dyspepsia with significant acute GI bleeding → immediate same‑day referral (also see NICE CG141 acute upper GI bleeding).
  • Initial management of uninvestigated dyspepsia (CG184): offer full‑dose PPI for 4 weeks and/or H. pylori “test and treat”. Leave a 2‑week PPI washout before breath test or stool antigen test for H. pylori.
  • Don’t miss differentials (CG184): review meds that can cause dyspepsia (e.g. NSAIDs, bisphosphonates, steroids) and consider cardiac or biliary disease where appropriate.

Dyspepsia / reflux symptoms — adults (NG12 + CG184 framing)

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Haemodynamic compromise, collapse/syncope, or suspected major upper GI bleed.
  • Severe/ongoing haematemesis or melaena with systemic upset.
  • Suspected perforation: sudden severe pain, rigid abdomen, peritonism.
  • Acute coronary syndrome mimic: central chest pain/pressure, breathlessness, diaphoresis (treat as cardiac until proven otherwise).

Same-day specialist assessment

  • Significant acute GI bleeding with dyspepsia → same‑day specialist assessment (NICE CG184; see also NICE CG141 acute upper GI bleeding).

Suspected cancer pathway referral (2WW equivalent) — NG12

  • Dysphagia → refer using suspected cancer pathway (oesophageal and/or stomach cancer framing) (NICE NG12).
  • Age ≥55 + weight loss + any of:
    • upper abdominal pain
    • reflux
    • dyspepsia
    → refer using suspected cancer pathway (NICE NG12; amended 2025).

Non-urgent direct-access upper GI endoscopy — NG12 (examples)

  • Haematemesis → consider non-urgent direct-access upper GI endoscopy (NG12).
  • Age ≥55 with treatment‑resistant dyspepsia → consider non-urgent direct-access endoscopy (NG12).
  • Age ≥55 with upper abdominal pain + low Hb → consider non-urgent direct-access endoscopy (NG12).
  • Age ≥55 with thrombocytosis plus upper GI symptoms (nausea/vomiting/weight loss/reflux/dyspepsia/upper abdo pain) → consider endoscopy (NG12).
  • Nausea/vomiting with weight loss/reflux/dyspepsia/upper abdo pain (age ≥55) → consider endoscopy (NG12).

Step 1 — define the presentation (don’t anchor too early)

  • Symptoms: epigastric pain/burning, post-prandial fullness, early satiety, heartburn, acid regurgitation, nausea/vomiting, bloating.
  • Duration and trajectory: new vs chronic; progressive vs intermittent; response to OTC antacids/alginate.
  • Risk factors / context: NSAID use, alcohol, smoking, known ulcer disease, previous endoscopy results.
  • Consider differentials: biliary colic, pancreatitis, cardiac causes, pregnancy, medication-induced oesophagitis.

Step 2 — medication review (CG184)

  • Check for drugs that can cause/worsen dyspepsia (e.g. NSAIDs, bisphosphonates, steroids, calcium antagonists, nitrates, theophyllines).
  • If referring, suspend NSAIDs where possible (CG184).

Step 3 — initial management of uninvestigated dyspepsia (CG184)

  • Offer full‑dose PPI for 4 weeks (CG184).
  • Offer H. pylori test-and-treat (CG184).
  • H. pylori testing: if using breath test or stool antigen test, ensure a 2‑week washout after PPI (CG184).
  • If symptoms recur: step down to lowest effective dose / as‑needed use where appropriate (CG184).
  • If inadequate response to PPI: consider an H2 receptor antagonist (CG184).

Step 4 — safety-netting (pragmatic)

  • Give clear advice to return urgently if dysphagia, weight loss, persistent vomiting, melaena/haematemesis, anaemia symptoms, or escalating pain/systemic illness.
  • Plan review if symptoms persist after initial treatment (e.g. after the 4‑week PPI trial), and reassess for NG12 criteria.

Who to refer / where to send (D&G placeholders)

  • 999 / ED: unstable/major bleed, perforation concern, severe systemic illness, ACS mimic.
  • Same-day specialist: significant acute GI bleeding with dyspepsia (CG184/CG141).
  • Suspected cancer pathway: dysphagia; or ≥55 + weight loss + (upper abdo pain/reflux/dyspepsia) (NG12).
  • Direct-access endoscopy (non-urgent): haematemesis; or ≥55 with treatment‑resistant dyspepsia; and other NG12 combinations (local route).
  • Routine gastro: persistent symptoms without NG12 triggers despite appropriate initial management (local route).

Referral-letter minimum (suggested)

  • Symptoms (exact description), onset date, progression, presence/absence of dysphagia and weight loss.
  • Bleeding features: melaena/haematemesis; anticoagulant/antiplatelet use.
  • Medications and risk factors (esp. NSAIDs, bisphosphonates, steroids, smoking, alcohol).
  • Treatments tried: PPI course (dose/duration), H. pylori test result and eradication regimen if given.

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • Local referral route for NG12 upper GI suspected cancer triggers: direct-to-endoscopy vs clinic-first.
  • Define “treatment‑resistant dyspepsia” operationally (duration, PPI dose, adherence) for direct-access endoscopy referrals.
  • Preferred local H. pylori test (breath vs stool antigen) and any local lab constraints; confirm PPI washout expectations.
  • Local first-line H. pylori eradication regimen (antimicrobial stewardship / resistance considerations; prior macrolide exposure policy).
  • Local pathway for significant dyspepsia with bleeding features: ED vs acute assessment unit vs gastro hot clinic.
