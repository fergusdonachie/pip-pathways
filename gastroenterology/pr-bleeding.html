DGRefHelp drafts / PR bleeding (rectal bleeding)

Primary care triage for rectal bleeding: identify unstable/major bleeding, stratify colorectal-cancer risk (NICE NG12 + FIT), and decide same-day vs urgent suspected cancer vs routine assessment.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑11

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • PR bleeding ranges from trivial anal bleeding to major acute lower-GI bleeding. In primary care, safe work is mostly triage + baseline assessment + risk stratification.
  • NICE NG12 (2023 update) now embeds FIT to guide suspected colorectal cancer referral for multiple symptom groups, including rectal bleeding and change in bowel habit.
  • NICE NG12: refer on suspected cancer pathway if FIT ≥10 micrograms Hb/g faeces.
  • BSG 2019 acute LGIB guideline supports structured acute risk stratification using: shock index (HR/SBP; ≥1 suggests unstable) and Oakland score (≤8 suggests minor self-terminating bleed potentially suitable for discharge if no other admission indications).

PR bleeding (rectal bleeding) — adults (primary care triage)

Definition / scope

  • PR bleeding = passage of blood per rectum (bright red blood, dark red/maroon, or blood mixed with stool).
  • Applies to adults. Separate pathways needed for children, pregnancy, known IBD, and anticoagulation/bleeding disorders (specialist nuance).
  • Always consider: is this truly lower-GI bleeding (vs haematuria, vaginal bleeding, or upper-GI bleed with melaena)?

Immediate red flags (send same day: ED / SDEC / acute assessment)

  • Haemodynamic compromise: syncope/collapse, SBP low, tachycardia, shock, new confusion.
  • Ongoing large-volume bleeding or clots, or recurrent bleeding with symptoms.
  • Severe anaemia symptoms: chest pain, breathlessness at rest, severe fatigue, or Hb very low (if known).
  • Suspected sepsis / colitis: fever, severe abdominal pain, profuse diarrhoea, peritonism.
  • Known anticoagulant use with significant bleeding (especially DOAC/warfarin) — needs urgent assessment and reversal planning.
Acute LGIB risk stratification (hospital-style concepts; optional in primary care)
  • Shock index = HR / SBP. ≥1 suggests unstable bleeding in BSG guidance.
  • Oakland score: a score ≤8 is described in BSG guidance as consistent with a minor self-terminating bleed potentially suitable for discharge if no other admission indications (local admission thresholds vary).
