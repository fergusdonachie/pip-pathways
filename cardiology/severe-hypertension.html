DGRefHelp drafts / Severe hypertension / very high BP

Adults (non-pregnant): decide emergency vs urgent (end-organ damage trumps the number), identify NICE same-day referral criteria (≥180/120 + retinal haemorrhage/papilloedema or life-threatening symptoms), and manage asymptomatic severe BP with rapid review + target-organ-damage checks.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • Emergency is about acute end‑organ damage (EOD), not the number: BIHS emphasises that even moderate BP elevation can be life‑threatening if there is acute EOD (for example, pulmonary oedema, encephalopathy, aortic dissection, intracranial haemorrhage, eclampsia), while many patients with BP ≥180/120 have no acute EOD and do not need rapid BP reduction.
  • NICE NG136 “same‑day specialist assessment” rule: refer same day if clinic BP ≥180/120 with retinal haemorrhage or papilloedema (accelerated/malignant hypertension) or life‑threatening symptoms such as new confusion, chest pain, signs of heart failure, or acute kidney injury. Also refer same day if suspected phaeochromocytoma.
  • Asymptomatic severe hypertension (≥180/120) without same‑day features: NICE recommends target‑organ‑damage investigations as soon as possible. If target‑organ damage is identified, consider starting antihypertensive treatment immediately (do not wait for ABPM/HBPM confirmation). If none, repeat clinic BP / arrange ABPM/HBPM with clinical review within 7 days.
  • Avoid iatrogenic harm: BIHS notes that aggressive/rapid BP lowering in people without acute EOD can cause harm; focus on measurement accuracy, identify EOD, and plan safe follow‑up and treatment optimisation.

Severe hypertension / very high BP (adults, non‑pregnant)

Step 0 — check it’s real (before escalating)

  • Use correct cuff size; patient seated, rested, arm supported; avoid talking.
  • Repeat measurement (take 2–3 readings; record the lower of the last 2 as per NICE technique for clinic BP).
  • If pulse irregular (e.g. AF), consider manual auscultatory BP (automated devices may be inaccurate).

Immediate emergencies (999 / ED now)

  • Neurology: new focal deficit, seizure, reduced consciousness, severe thunderclap headache, new confusion (possible stroke/ICH/SAH/hypertensive encephalopathy).
  • Chest pain suggesting ACS, or tearing chest/back pain suggesting aortic dissection.
  • Acute heart failure / pulmonary oedema: severe breathlessness at rest, hypoxia, widespread crackles, pink frothy sputum.
  • Severe acute kidney injury features with systemic illness (or rapidly worsening symptoms) → urgent hospital assessment.
  • Pregnancy / possible pre‑eclampsia/eclampsia (if relevant) → emergency obstetric pathway (this page otherwise assumes non‑pregnant).

NICE same‑day specialist assessment (arrange same day)

  • Clinic BP ≥180/120 with retinal haemorrhage or papilloedema (accelerated/malignant hypertension).
  • Clinic BP ≥180/120 with life‑threatening symptoms: new confusion, chest pain, signs of heart failure, or acute kidney injury.
  • Suspected phaeochromocytoma: labile BP or postural hypotension plus symptoms such as headache, palpitations, pallor, diaphoresis, abdominal pain.

If clinic BP ≥180/120 but no symptoms/signs needing same‑day referral

  • Do target‑organ‑damage checks ASAP (same/next day if possible):
    • Urine: dip for blood/protein; send urine ACR.
    • Bloods: U&Es/creatinine/eGFR, HbA1c, lipids (NICE).
    • ECG (LVH/AF/ischaemia).
    • Fundi for hypertensive retinopathy if you can (papilloedema/haemorrhage = same‑day referral).
  • If target‑organ damage is identified: consider starting antihypertensive treatment immediately (do not wait for ABPM/HBPM confirmation) and arrange close follow‑up.
  • If no target‑organ damage is identified:
    • Repeat clinic BP within 7 days, or
    • Arrange ABPM (or HBPM if ABPM unsuitable) with a clinical review within 7 days.
  • Do not aim for rapid BP reduction in the community if no acute EOD — optimise long‑term management (NICE NG136) and safety‑net.

Focused assessment (what to look for)

  • Symptoms suggesting acute EOD: chest pain, breathlessness/orthopnoea, neurological symptoms, severe headache/visual changes, oliguria, severe nausea/vomiting.
  • Medication review: missed antihypertensives; recent changes; OTC/illicit causes (NSAIDs, decongestants/sympathomimetics, steroids, cocaine/amphetamines); withdrawal states (e.g. clonidine).
  • Examination: neuro exam; volume status; heart failure signs; pulses/unequal BP (dissection); fundoscopy if possible.

Where to send / who to refer

  • 999 / ED: suspected stroke/ICH/SAH, ACS/dissection, pulmonary oedema/respiratory failure, seizure, reduced consciousness.
  • Same‑day specialist assessment (local route): NICE criteria above for ≥180/120 with papilloedema/retinal haemorrhage or life‑threatening symptoms; suspected phaeochromocytoma.
  • Urgent primary care follow‑up (within 7 days): asymptomatic ≥180/120 without same‑day features (with immediate TOD checks and treatment plan as above).
  • Routine hypertension pathway: 140/90–<180/120 → ABPM/HBPM confirmation and stepwise treatment per NICE NG136 (not the focus of this page).

Referral-letter minimum (suggested)

  • BP readings (including repeat readings, device type, arm used), HR/rhythm, and relevant symptoms/signs.
  • Medication list + adherence issues; recent OTC/illicit drug exposure; relevant comorbidities.
  • Urine dip and urine ACR (if available), creatinine/eGFR/U&Es, ECG findings; fundoscopy findings if done.
  • Any concern for ACS/stroke/heart failure/AKI/dissection and what has been ruled out clinically.

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • What is the default same‑day route for NICE 1.5.2 criteria: ED vs SDEC/acute medicine vs medical “hot clinic”?
  • Can primary care access same‑day U&Es/creatinine (to support the “AKI” trigger) and urgent urine ACR?
  • Is there a local method for documenting retinal haemorrhage/papilloedema (e.g., ED assessment, community optometry, urgent ophthalmology)?
  • Do we have a preferred local stance for starting/intensifying antihypertensives immediately in asymptomatic ≥180/120 with TOD (and who reviews within 48–72h)?
  • Local approach to suspected phaeochromocytoma: same‑day assessment route and first tests (plasma metanephrines vs referral first).
Safety net
Seek urgent help (999/ED) if chest pain, breathlessness, neurological symptoms, confusion, severe headache, or visual loss occurs — even if BP has been “high for a while”.
