DGRefHelp drafts / Syncope / collapse

Adults (>16): confirm true transient loss of consciousness (TLoC), do the minimum high‑yield tests (incl. 12‑lead ECG + postural BP), and use NICE CG109 red flags to route for urgent cardiovascular assessment.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • In UK guidance, “syncope” is usually handled under transient loss of consciousness (TLoC / blackout) because the key job is differentiate syncope vs seizure vs other, then identify high‑risk (cardiac) features and route appropriately.
  • NICE CG109 emphasises witness history + lying/standing BP (if appropriate) + 12‑lead ECG as the core initial assessment; treat specific ECG abnormalities as a red flag.
  • NICE CG109 “urgent cardiovascular assessment (reviewed within 24h)” red flags include: ECG abnormality, heart failure, TLoC during exertion, family history of sudden cardiac death <40/inherited condition, new/unexplained breathlessness, and heart murmur; also consider <24h assessment if age >65 with no prodrome.
  • ESC guidance (summarised by ACC) aligns on: everyone gets history + exam + standing BP + ECG; monitor (telemetry) if high risk/arrhythmic suspicion; do echo if structural disease suspected; and target prolonged monitoring to recurrent unexplained episodes with arrhythmic features.

Syncope / collapse (TLoC) — adults (>16): primary care triage

Step 0 — immediate safety (999 / ED now)

  • Not fully recovered, ongoing altered consciousness, or serious injury from the event.
  • Suspected life‑threatening underlying cause (use judgement): ongoing chest pain/ACS features; severe breathlessness/hypoxia; shock/hypotension; major haemorrhage; sepsis; stroke symptoms; anaphylaxis.
  • Persistent abnormal physiology: sustained bradycardia/tachycardia with symptoms, very low BP, hypoxia.

Step 1 — confirm TLoC and characterise the event (NICE CG109)

  • Ask patient + witness(es) what happened before / during / after. Try to phone a witness if not present.
  • Record: posture before event; triggers (pain, emotion, prolonged standing, micturition/cough); prodrome (warmth/sweating/nausea/visual dimming); colour; eyes open/closed; movements (brief limb‑jerking can occur in faints); tongue bite (side vs tip); duration to recovery; confusion; unilateral weakness.

Step 2 — core observations + exam

  • Obs: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 (on air if possible).
  • Lying/standing BP if clinically appropriate (and safe to do).
  • Cardiovascular exam: murmurs, heart failure signs; check for dehydration/volume depletion.
  • Neuro screen if indicated (especially post‑event focal symptoms).

Step 3 — ECG (do not skip)

  • Do a 12‑lead ECG with automated interpretation (or have reviewed by someone competent).
  • Treat as a red flag if ECG shows (NICE CG109 examples): conduction abnormality (bundle branch block/heart block), long/short QT, ST/T abnormalities; or manual review concerns such as persistent inappropriate bradycardia, ventricular arrhythmia/ectopy, Brugada pattern, ventricular pre‑excitation (WPW), pathological Q waves, significant T‑wave inversion, LVH/RVH, sustained atrial arrhythmia, paced rhythm.

Step 4 — targeted tests (only if suggested)

  • Do tests directed by suspicion (NICE CG109): e.g. capillary glucose if hypoglycaemia possible; FBC if anaemia/bleeding; pregnancy test if relevant; electrolytes if concern for metabolic cause.
  • Do not “shotgun” investigations if a clear benign faint is diagnosed and the patient is well — but document your reasoning + safety net.

High‑risk (cardiac) red flags → urgent cardiovascular assessment (reviewed & prioritised within 24h) (NICE CG109)

  • Any ECG abnormality as above.
  • Heart failure (history or signs).
  • TLoC during exertion (exercise‑related syncope).
  • Family history of sudden cardiac death <40 and/or inherited cardiac condition.
  • New or unexplained breathlessness.
  • Heart murmur.
  • Consider urgent assessment if age >65 with TLoC without prodromal symptoms.

Features strongly suggestive of epileptic seizure → epilepsy specialist (seen within 2 weeks) (NICE CG109)

  • Examples: bitten tongue; head turning; unusual posturing; prolonged limb‑jerking; prolonged post‑event confusion; déjà vu/jamais vu prodrome; amnesia for witnessed abnormal behaviour.
  • Consider not epilepsy if classic vasovagal story (sweating, prolonged standing trigger, prodrome abolished by sitting/lying).

Likely uncomplicated faint / situational syncope / postural hypotension (low risk)

  • If a firm diagnosis is made and no red flags: manage conservatively (trigger avoidance, hydration, manoeuvres, medication review if hypotensive).
  • Safety net and re‑review if episodes recur, become injurious, occur during exertion, or if new red‑flag symptoms appear.

Who to refer / where to send (D&G placeholders)

  • 999 / ED: any incomplete recovery, serious injury, persistent abnormal physiology, or suspected serious underlying cause.
  • Urgent cardiovascular assessment (within 24h prioritisation): any NICE CG109 red flag feature.
  • Routine cardiovascular assessment: recurrent unexplained episodes without red flags but not meeting criteria for “firm benign diagnosis” (local syncope/TLoC clinic, cardiology, or falls service depending on age/context).
  • Epilepsy specialist (within 2 weeks): features strongly suggestive of seizure.

Referral-letter minimum (suggested)

  • Clear witness account summary: posture/trigger/prodrome, movements, colour, duration, recovery confusion.
  • Obs + postural BP results (or why not done).
  • 12‑lead ECG attached + interpretation; prior ECG comparisons if relevant.
  • PMH: structural heart disease/HF; meds (esp. hypotensives/diuretics/QT‑prolonging); family history SCD <40.
  • Injury sustained; recurrence pattern; driving/occupation risk if relevant.

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • What is the default urgent front door for NICE CG109 red flags: ED vs acute medicine/SDEC vs cardiology hot clinic vs syncope unit?
  • Local access for ambulatory ECG (who orders; what duration options exist; turnaround time).
  • Is there a local falls/TLoC/syncope clinic for low‑risk/recurrent episodes (criteria + referral method)?
  • Local expectation for echo prior to cardiology referral (if murmur/abnormal ECG) vs “refer first”.
  • Local standard wording/process for driving advice after TLoC (documentation + DVLA signposting).
Safety net
Advise urgent reassessment if episodes occur during exertion, are associated with chest pain/breathlessness/palpitations, cause injury, or if recovery is prolonged/atypical.
