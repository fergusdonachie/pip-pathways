Evidence summary (triangulated)

  • Definition & Classification: Angina is a clinical diagnosis based on history. Typical angina has 3 features: (1) constricting discomfort in the front of the chest/neck/shoulders/jaw/arms; (2) precipitated by physical exertion; (3) relieved by rest or GTN within 5 minutes. Atypical angina has 2 features. Non-anginal pain has 0â€“1 feature (NICE CG95/CG126).
  • Assessment: Resting 12-lead ECG is first-line for all suspected stable angina (to check for previous MI, LBBB, LVH, ST changes). Bloods (FBC, U&E, LFT, lipids, HbA1c, TFTs) rule out exacerbating factors like anaemia or thyrotoxicosis.
  • Referral urgency:
    • Immediate (999/Admission): Unstable angina (pain at rest >20 mins, new onset <24h, crescendo pattern) or suspected MI.
    • Rapid Access Chest Pain Clinic (RACPC): For patients with stable chest pain suspected to be angina. NICE recommends assessment within 2 weeks (locally variable).
    • Routine: Known angina with stable symptoms for review, unless worsening (then RACPC/advice).
  • Management while waiting: Prescribe GTN spray. Consider starting aspirin 75mg and a statin if high probability of CAD (check local pathway). Beta-blocker or calcium channel blocker can be started for symptom relief if diagnosis is clear, but often deferred until specialist assessment to avoid masking stress test results (local variation).

Background

  • Definition: Symptomatic myocardial ischaemia caused by a mismatch between blood supply and demand.
  • Risk factors: Age, male sex, family history of premature CVD, smoking, diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidaemia.
  • Classification (NICE CG95):
    • Typical angina (3/3 features): 1. Constricting discomfort in front of chest, neck, shoulders, jaw, or arms. 2. Precipitated by physical exertion. 3. Relieved by rest or GTN within 5 minutes.
    • Atypical angina: 2 of the above features.
    • Non-anginal chest pain: 0 or 1 of the above features.

Assessment

  • History: Assess typicality (see Background). Check for red flags (pain at rest, unstable pattern).
  • Examination: Usually normal. Check BP, heart sounds (murmurs e.g., aortic stenosis), signs of heart failure, BMI.
  • Investigations:
    • ECG (12-lead): Mandatory for all suspected angina. Look for pathological Q waves, LBBB, ST/T wave changes.
    • Bloods: FBC (anaemia), U&E, LFTs, Lipid profile, HbA1c, TFTs.

Primary Care Management

  • Symptom relief: Prescribe GTN spray for all patients suspected of angina. Advise to use 1-2 sprays sublingually when pain starts or before exertion.
  • Risk factor control: Address smoking, hypertension, lipids, diabetes.
  • Initial medication (while waiting for clinic):
    • Consider Aspirin 75mg daily (if no contraindications) if CAD likely.
    • Rate-limiting medication (Beta-blocker or Calcium Channel Blocker) may be started for symptom control but check local RACPC criteria (some prefer patients off meds for stress echocardiography/imaging).
  • Safety netting: Advise to call 999 if pain lasts >15-20 mins, is not relieved by 2 doses of GTN 5 mins apart, or feels severe/different to usual.

Who to refer

  • Immediate (999 / Emergency admission):
    • Current chest pain suspected to be ischaemic (ACS).
    • Unstable angina: Pain at rest (>20 mins), new onset (<24h), or crescendo angina (increasing frequency/severity/duration).
    • Suspected angina with recent syncope or haemodynamic instability.
  • Rapid Access Chest Pain Clinic (RACPC):
    • New onset stable chest pain suspected to be angina (typical or atypical features).
    • Usually within 2 weeks (check local pathway).
    • Attach recent ECG and blood results.
  • Routine Cardiology:
    • Established angina for review/optimisation if not unstable.
    • Atypical/non-anginal pain where diagnostic uncertainty remains but RACPC criteria not met.

Who not to refer

  • Clear non-cardiac chest pain (e.g., musculoskeletal with tenderness, trauma, clear GORD responding to PPI) unless diagnostic doubt.
  • Patients already under active cardiology follow-up (seek advice/expedite appt).
