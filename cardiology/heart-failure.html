DGRefHelp drafts / Heart failure (suspected / decompensated)

Adults: triage decompensation (pulmonary oedema/shock/MI), do baseline tests, order NT‑proBNP for suspected new chronic HF, and refer for echo + specialist assessment using NICE thresholds (2 weeks / 6 weeks).
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • Triage first: suspected acute heart failure / decompensation (pulmonary oedema, shock, MI/arrhythmia) needs urgent hospital assessment; primary care role is recognise + route.
  • Suspected new chronic HF: NICE NG106 recommends NT‑proBNP testing in suspected HF, then referral for specialist assessment + transthoracic echo based on thresholds.
  • Referral urgency (NICE NG106): NT‑proBNP >2,000 ng/L → urgent specialist assessment + echo within 2 weeks; NT‑proBNP 400–2,000 ng/L → specialist assessment + echo within 6 weeks.
  • Low NT‑proBNP: in an untreated person, NT‑proBNP <400 ng/L makes HF less likely; review alternative causes and consider specialist discussion if still concerned (NICE NG106).
  • Baseline tests recommended by NICE NG106 alongside ECG/echo include: CXR and blood tests (renal, thyroid, liver, lipids, HbA1c, FBC) ± urinalysis and spirometry/peak flow to assess alternative/aggravating factors.

Heart failure — suspected/new or decompensated (adults)

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Severe breathlessness at rest, pulmonary oedema (pink frothy sputum, widespread crackles), or SpO2 low on air.
  • Shock / haemodynamic instability: systolic BP very low, cold/clammy, confusion, syncope, cyanosis.
  • Chest pain suggesting ACS, or new arrhythmia with instability.
  • Sepsis with suspected HF / severe physiology.
  • New neurological deficit (stroke/TIA) with HF/AF context → emergency pathway.

Urgent same‑day hospital assessment (even if not peri-arrest)

  • Suspected acute decompensated HF: rapidly worsening breathlessness, orthopnoea/PND, marked peripheral oedema/ascites, or new raised JVP.
  • New HF symptoms with significant hypoxia, tachypnoea, or significant tachycardia.
  • Possible precipitant needing inpatient workup: ACS, pneumonia, PE, uncontrolled AF, acute kidney injury.

Step 1 — focused assessment (aim: confirm syndrome + triage)

  • Observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air, weight (if feasible).
  • History: onset/trajectory; orthopnoea/PND; exercise tolerance; chest pain; palpitations; infection symptoms; medication changes (NSAIDs, steroids); alcohol/cocaine; known IHD/valve disease/AF.
  • Examination: JVP, crackles, wheeze, peripheral oedema, hepatomegaly/ascites; perfusion (cool peripheries); cardiac murmurs (valve disease).
  • 12‑lead ECG (AF, ischaemia, conduction disease).

Step 2 — baseline tests (typical, if stable enough)

  • Bloods (NG106): U&Es/eGFR, LFTs, TFTs, FBC; consider HbA1c and lipids (background risk/alternate dx).
  • CXR (cardiomegaly, pulmonary oedema, pleural effusions, infection) if available via local urgent imaging route.
  • Urinalysis (protein/haematuria; alternative causes/renal).
  • Spirometry/peak flow if respiratory differential and patient is stable (not in acute severe breathlessness).

Step 3 — suspected new chronic HF (not acutely unwell): NT‑proBNP → refer

  • Order NT‑proBNP for suspected HF (NICE NG106).
  • NT‑proBNP >2,000 ng/L → urgent specialist assessment + transthoracic echo within 2 weeks (NICE NG106/QS9).
  • NT‑proBNP 400–2,000 ng/L → specialist assessment + echo within 6 weeks (NICE NG106/QS9).
  • NT‑proBNP <400 ng/L (untreated) → HF less likely; review alternative causes. If still concerned, discuss with HF specialist service (NICE NG106).
  • Interpretation caveats (NICE NG106): obesity and HF meds (diuretics, ACEi/ARB/ARNI, beta‑blocker, MRA) can lower NP; many conditions can raise NP (e.g. renal impairment, sepsis, COPD, cirrhosis) — use clinical judgement.

Where to send / who to refer

  • 999 / ED: pulmonary oedema, shock/instability, suspected ACS, severe hypoxia/respiratory failure, severe arrhythmia with compromise.
  • Same‑day hospital assessment (local route: ED vs SDEC/acute medicine vs cardiology): suspected decompensated HF even if currently stable; HF with major comorbidity (AKI, infection, new AF with rapid rate, possible PE).
  • HF diagnostic pathway (echo + specialist assessment): suspected new chronic HF with elevated NT‑proBNP, using the NICE 2‑week / 6‑week thresholds.
  • Advice & guidance: diagnostic uncertainty with borderline NP; frailty/multimorbidity where referral route is unclear; recurrent decompensation despite “stable” NP.

Referral-letter minimum (suggested)

  • Symptom onset/trajectory (orthopnoea/PND; exercise tolerance; oedema) + NYHA-style functional limitation if known.
  • Observations incl. SpO2 on air, BP, HR; weight if available.
  • ECG attached + any prior ECG comparison.
  • NT‑proBNP result + date; baseline bloods (U&Es/eGFR, FBC, TFTs, LFTs) and CXR report if done.
  • Past cardiac history (IHD, valve disease, AF), comorbidities (CKD/COPD), and current meds (esp. diuretics, ACEi/ARB/ARNI, beta‑blocker, MRA, NSAIDs).

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • What is the default same‑day route for suspected decompensated HF that is not peri-arrest: ED vs SDEC/acute medicine vs cardiology hot clinic?
  • Can GPs order NT‑proBNP directly, and what is the expected turnaround time (same day / 24–48h / longer)?
  • Is there a local HF diagnostic clinic (echo + specialist assessment) and how is it booked (SCI Gateway form / A&G / phone)?
  • Is direct‑access echo available to GPs, and if so when should it be used vs the HF clinic referral?
  • Availability and remit of community HF nurses (can they support diuretic titration/monitoring; criteria for referral).
  • Local expectations for baseline tests before referral (CXR required? U&Es threshold? iron studies?) and what to do if tests can’t be arranged promptly.
Safety net
Advise urgent reassessment/999 if breathlessness worsens rapidly, new chest pain occurs, syncope develops, or oxygen saturations are low.
