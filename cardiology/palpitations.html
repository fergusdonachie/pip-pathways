DGRefHelp drafts / Palpitations

Adults with palpitations: identify emergency features (syncope/instability/exertional), do a 12‑lead ECG, check reversible causes, and choose ambulatory monitoring + referral urgency.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • Most palpitations in primary care are benign; the job is triage + capture rhythm + identify high‑risk features (local NHS referral criteria).
  • Red flags consistently include: palpitations with syncope/near syncope, haemodynamic instability, exercise‑precipitation, and family history of sudden death <40 / inherited cardiac disease.
  • 12‑lead ECG is the key first test; if the pulse is irregular and AF is suspected, NICE NG196 recommends confirming with a 12‑lead ECG.
  • If AF is suspected but not seen on the 12‑lead ECG, NICE NG196 recommends choosing ambulatory ECG monitoring based on symptom frequency (24‑hour monitor if episodes <24h apart; longer event monitoring if >24h apart).
  • For unstable tachyarrhythmia (or sustained tachyarrhythmia with significant symptoms), follow emergency care algorithms (Resuscitation Council UK) and arrange urgent transfer.

Palpitations — adults (primary care triage)

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Haemodynamic instability: hypotension/shock, severe dizziness, confusion, collapse.
  • Syncope or near syncope with palpitations (high‑risk feature).
  • Chest pain suggesting ACS OR breathlessness/hypoxia suggesting heart failure/PE.
  • Ongoing sustained tachyarrhythmia (especially if symptomatic) or suspected ventricular tachycardia.
  • Signs of decompensated heart failure (pulmonary oedema, new raised JVP, marked peripheral oedema) with palpitations.
  • Systemic cause with severe physiology (e.g. sepsis, severe anaemia, thyrotoxicosis).

Urgent same‑day / urgent cardiology assessment (red flags)

  • Palpitations precipitated by exercise.
  • Family history of sudden cardiac death <40 in a first‑degree relative OR known inherited cardiac condition.
  • Significant ECG abnormality (examples): high‑grade AV block, pre‑excitation/WPW pattern, long QT, Brugada pattern, sustained SVT/AF with rapid ventricular response, frequent runs of broad‑complex tachycardia.
  • Abnormal cardiac examination (new murmur, signs of structural disease) OR known structural heart disease.

Step 1 — focused assessment (aim: capture rhythm + risk)

  • Observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air.
  • History:
    • Onset/offset (sudden vs gradual), duration, frequency; regular vs irregular; triggers (exercise, alcohol, caffeine, drugs).
    • Associated symptoms: syncope/presyncope, chest pain, breathlessness, neurological symptoms.
    • Past cardiac disease; thyroid disease; anaemia; pregnancy/post‑partum; stimulant use.
    • Family history: sudden death <40; inherited cardiomyopathy/channelopathy.
  • Examination: pulse regularity; cardiac murmurs; heart failure signs; thyroid signs.
  • 12‑lead ECG (ideally during symptoms). If unavailable or symptoms severe → use clinical judgement and route urgently.

Step 2 — baseline tests (typical)

  • Bloods: FBC (anaemia), U&Es/eGFR, TFTs, electrolytes incl. calcium and magnesium (especially if arrhythmia suspected).
  • Consider: glucose; pregnancy test if relevant; further tests only if suggested by history (e.g. catecholamines if pheochromocytoma suspected).

Step 3 — suspected AF pathway (capture + confirm)

  • If irregular pulse and suspected AF (with or without symptoms): do a 12‑lead ECG to confirm (NICE NG196).
  • If paroxysmal AF suspected but not seen on 12‑lead ECG (NICE NG196):
    • Use 24‑hour ambulatory ECG if episodes are suspected to be <24 hours apart (or asymptomatic episodes suspected).
    • Use longer ambulatory/event monitoring for a period appropriate to capture AF if episodes are >24 hours apart.
  • Once AF is confirmed: assess stability (urgent if unstable) and plan follow‑up for stroke risk/anticoagulation decisions (separate AF page).

Step 4 — likely SVT / regular sudden-onset palpitations (stable patient)

  • Give safety net: if becomes unwell (syncope, chest pain, breathlessness) → urgent assessment/999.
  • Teach a Valsalva manoeuvre for future episodes (if no contraindications).
  • Consider trial of a cardio‑selective beta blocker (if appropriate) for frequent troublesome episodes; alternatives may be used locally (e.g. diltiazem) with usual contraindications/interactions considered.
  • Arrange rhythm capture (ambulatory monitor) if diagnosis not already documented; refer for cardiology/arrhythmia review if frequent, prolonged, or patient prefers definitive treatment (e.g. ablation).

Step 5 — sinus tachycardia / ectopics (normal exam + ECG + no red flags)

  • Address precipitating factors (sleep deprivation, anxiety, caffeine, alcohol, nicotine, recreational drugs; intercurrent illness).
  • Reassure when appropriate; consider a beta blocker if highly symptomatic and suitable.

Who to refer / where to send (D&G placeholders)

  • 999 / ED: any instability/syncope with ongoing symptoms; sustained tachyarrhythmia; suspected VT; palpitations with chest pain/breathlessness suggestive of ACS/PE/HF.
  • Urgent cardiology / same‑day assessment: exertional palpitations; concerning family history; abnormal ECG; structural heart disease signs.
  • Routine cardiology / arrhythmia clinic: documented arrhythmia needing specialist plan; persistent troublesome symptoms despite reassurance and initial measures; abnormal ambulatory monitor findings.
  • Advice & guidance: diagnostic uncertainty; choosing the most appropriate ambulatory monitor; borderline ECG findings.

Referral-letter minimum (suggested)

  • Episode pattern (sudden/gradual, duration, frequency), triggers, associated syncope/chest pain/breathlessness.
  • Obs during symptoms if available (HR/BP/SpO2) and full set of baseline obs.
  • ECG(s) attached (and ambulatory monitor report if done).
  • Blood results: FBC, U&Es/eGFR, TFTs ± Mg/Ca.
  • PMH incl. structural heart disease; meds (including stimulants); family history of sudden death.

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • Which service is the default urgent front door for palpitations with red flags but currently stable: ED vs SDEC/acute medicine vs cardiology hot clinic?
  • Local access for ambulatory ECG: who orders (GP vs cardiology), and what’s available (24‑hour Holter vs 7–14 day patch vs event recorder)?
  • Is there a local arrhythmia “hot clinic” or rapid access pathway (criteria + referral method)?
  • Local expectation for echo prior to routine referral when ECG/monitor shows arrhythmia or exam is abnormal.
  • Local guidance on driving advice documentation for arrhythmias (who counsels; where recorded).
Safety net
Advise urgent reassessment if palpitations are associated with syncope/presyncope, chest pain, breathlessness, or if episodes are prolonged/ongoing.
