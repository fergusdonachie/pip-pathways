DGRefHelp drafts / Chest pain / suspected ACS

Adults with acute chest pain: identify suspected ACS needing emergency transfer, do a 12‑lead ECG without delaying transfer, give aspirin if appropriate, and route pain‑free recent episodes via the local same‑day assessment/troponin pathway.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • Primary care work for suspected ACS is mainly triage + immediate actions + correct routing, not ruling out MI in the community.
  • NICE CG95 gives a simple time‑based triage for suspected ACS: current chest pain (or pain in last 12 hours with abnormal/unavailable ECG) → emergency hospital referral.
  • If pain is now resolved but the episode was recent, NICE CG95 supports urgent same‑day assessment (typical use case: access to ECG + troponin pathway).
  • When ACS is suspected, take a 12‑lead ECG as soon as possible, but do not delay transfer (NICE CG95).
  • For acute STEMI, NICE NG185 recommends a single loading dose of aspirin 300 mg as soon as possible unless allergic (prehospital/primary care implementation is usually straightforward, but confirm local protocols).

Chest pain / suspected ACS — adults (primary care triage)

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Suspected ACS with current chest pain (especially >15 minutes, or recurrent at rest) (NICE CG95).
  • Suspected ACS + pain in the last 12 hours AND ECG abnormal OR ECG not available (NICE CG95).
  • Haemodynamic instability: hypotension, shock, syncope/collapse, altered mental state.
  • Acute pulmonary oedema / severe breathlessness or hypoxia (possible ACS complication; urgent assessment).
  • Life‑threatening alternative diagnoses suspected (e.g. aortic dissection, PE, tension pneumothorax).

Step 1 — quick initial assessment (do not delay transfer)

  • Observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air.
  • Focused history: onset, duration (>15 min), character, radiation (arm/jaw/back), exertional vs at rest; associated nausea/vomiting, sweating, breathlessness.
  • Risk context: known IHD, diabetes, CKD, smoking, strong FH, older age; recent cocaine/stimulants.
  • 12‑lead ECG ASAP if available, but do not delay transfer (NICE CG95). Send ECG ahead if possible.
  • Do not use response to GTN to make a diagnosis (NICE CG95).

Step 2 — immediate actions while arranging transfer (if suspected ACS)

  • Aspirin: give 300 mg as soon as possible unless clear allergy/contraindication (NICE NG185 for STEMI; CG95 also recommends aspirin when MI suspected). If unsure, follow local protocol.
  • Positioning + reassurance; avoid exertion.
  • GTN may help symptoms if previously prescribed/appropriate, but symptom relief does not rule out ACS (NICE CG95).

Step 3 — pain-free now: decide emergency vs urgent same-day assessment (NICE CG95)

  • If suspected ACS and now pain-free but had pain in last 12 hours:
    • Normal ECG available → urgent same-day assessment (local pathway).
    • ECG abnormal or ECG not available → emergency hospital referral.
  • If suspected ACS and last episode was 12–72 hours ago → urgent same-day assessment (local pathway).
  • If suspected ACS and there are complications (e.g. pulmonary oedema) → refer to hospital (use judgement: emergency vs urgent same-day) (NICE CG95).

Who to refer / where to send (D&G placeholders)

  • 999 / ED: any red flags above; any ongoing pain concerning for ACS; unstable vitals; suspected STEMI/NSTEMI on ECG.
  • Urgent same-day assessment (SDEC/ambulatory care/acute medicine/cardiology pathway): pain-free recent episodes where ACS is suspected and local pathway supports ECG + high-sensitivity troponin with timely review.
  • Non-ACS chest pain: consider other pathways (e.g. suspected PE if clinically indicated; pneumothorax; pneumonia; GI/musculoskeletal).

What to include in the referral / handover

  • Symptoms + onset; duration; whether ongoing; associated symptoms (SOB, sweating, nausea, syncope).
  • Observations incl. SpO2 on air.
  • ECG findings (attach/forward trace; include time recorded) and any previous ECG comparison if available.
  • Risk factors + cardiac history; key comorbidities (CKD, bleeding history).
  • Meds (antiplatelets/anticoagulants, nitrates, PDE5 inhibitors, diabetes meds) + allergies.
  • What was given already (e.g. aspirin 300 mg) + response.

Safety-netting

  • If discharged or awaiting assessment: advise urgent care/999 for recurrent chest pain, worsening breathlessness, syncope, or new severe symptoms.
  • Do not be reassured by atypical pain alone: ACS can present without central chest pain (NICE CG95).

Local agreement needed (D&G)

Reminder
If ACS is on the table, the safe default is same‑day hospital assessment. Scoring tools are not the main decision point here; routing is.
