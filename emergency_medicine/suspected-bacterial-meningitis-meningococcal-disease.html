Evidence summary (triangulated)

NICE NG240: key recognition messages
  • Rapidly evolving conditions; can present with non‑specific symptoms, and people (especially young people/young adults) may appear relatively well early on.
  • Meningitis and sepsis can occur at the same time, particularly with a rash.
  • NICE defines a red‑flag combination for bacterial meningitis: fever + headache + neck stiffness + altered level of consciousness/cognition (but meningitis can still be suspected without all 4).
  • For meningococcal disease, NICE highlights a particularly high‑risk red flag: haemorrhagic, non‑blanching rash with lesions >2 mm (purpura).
Transfer + early antibiotics
  • If you suspect or strongly suspect bacterial meningitis or meningococcal disease: emergency transfer to hospital is recommended (NICE NG240).
  • If strongly suspected meningococcal disease, NICE recommends giving IV/IM ceftriaxone or benzylpenicillin as soon as possible outside hospital unless this will delay transfer (and not if severe antibiotic allergy).
  • NICE QS19 quality statement: people with suspected bacterial meningitis / meningococcal disease should receive IV antibiotics within 1 hour of arrival at hospital (some may have received pre‑hospital antibiotics).
Scope note
This page is deliberately focused on community recognition + immediate actions (primary care/OOH/ambulance handover). It is not a definitive in‑hospital management protocol.

Suspected bacterial meningitis / meningococcal disease — community recognition + immediate actions

Immediate safety actions (do first)

  • Do not delay transfer for detailed assessment or investigations.
  • Emergency transfer to hospital (typically 999 / blue‑light) for suspected meningitis or meningococcal disease.
  • If airway/breathing/circulation compromised: manage as per resuscitation / suspected sepsis and transfer.

When to strongly suspect bacterial meningitis (NICE red‑flag combination)

  • Fever + headache + neck stiffness + altered level of consciousness or cognition (confusion/delirium).
  • Even if not all 4 are present: meningitis can still be suspected on clinical assessment.

When to strongly suspect meningococcal disease (very high risk)

  • Haemorrhagic, non‑blanching rash with lesions >2 mm (purpura) — treat as meningococcal disease until proven otherwise.
  • Be aware the rash may be harder to see on darker skin; check all over (including conjunctivae for petechiae).

Antibiotics before hospital (only if it will not delay transfer)

  • If strongly suspected meningococcal disease: give IM/IV ceftriaxone or benzylpenicillin as soon as possible in the community unless this will delay transfer.
  • Do not give if the person has a history of severe antibiotic allergy (use local anaphylaxis guidance).
  • If you cannot give immediately without slowing transfer: prioritise rapid hospital arrival (IV antibiotics should be started within 1 hour of arrival).

Minimum handover metrics (document + pass to ambulance/ED)

  • Time of symptom onset + evolution (including any rash change; blanching → non‑blanching).
  • Full observations if possible: RR, SpO2, HR, BP, temperature.
  • Conscious level (e.g., GCS/AVPU) and key neuro findings (neck stiffness, focal deficits, seizures).
  • Rash description + distribution (including whether purpuric).
  • Allergies (especially beta‑lactams) and whether any antibiotic was given (drug/dose/route/time).
  • Risk factors if known (immunosuppression/asplenia/complement inhibitor therapy, recent close contact outbreak context).
