Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key decision points to build the page around
  • Don’t overcall “cellulitis”: exclude common mimics (venous stasis dermatitis, inflammatory bite reaction, gout, contact dermatitis, DVT) before antibiotics.
  • Same‑day / hospital if features suggest a serious soft‑tissue infection or systemic illness (necrotising fasciitis, sepsis, orbital/periorbital cellulitis, septic arthritis/osteomyelitis).
  • Oral antibiotics first line if the patient can take oral medicines and isn’t severely unwell; reassess if not improving in 2–3 days or if rapidly worsening.

1) NICE NG141 (cellulitis and erysipelas: antimicrobial prescribing) — the practical rules

  • Before treating, exclude other causes of redness (for example inflammatory reaction to immunisation/insect bite; chronic venous insufficiency).
  • Swabs are rarely useful: consider only if the skin is broken and there is a penetrating injury, water exposure, or acquisition outside the UK.
  • Draw around the erythema to monitor progress (note: erythema may be less visible on darker skin tones).
  • Oral first line unless severity requires IV; if IV used, review by 48h and switch to oral if possible.
  • Reassess if symptoms worsen rapidly/significantly, do not start to improve within 2–3 days, severe pain out of proportion, or spreading beyond initial presentation.
  • Refer to hospital if signs suggest more serious illness/condition (examples given: orbital cellulitis, osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, necrotising fasciitis, sepsis).
  • Consider referral / specialist advice if severely unwell, infection near eyes/nose, possible uncommon pathogens, spreading infection not responding to oral antibiotics, lymphangitis, or cannot take oral antibiotics (consider local IV-at-home/community options where appropriate).
  • Manage predispositions: diabetes, venous insufficiency, eczema, oedema (incl. CCB‑related).

2) What this implies for a D&G DGRefHelp page (scope decisions)

  • This page is triage + routing focused; antibiotic choice should be linked to (or deferred to) local antimicrobial guidance (NICE gives reasonable defaults).
  • Keep a tight list of “don’t miss” differentials and the “triangle of danger” (eyes/nose) rule as a referral accelerator.
  • Keep Local agreement needed items explicit (e.g., SDEC/OPAT availability, who owns follow‑up at 48–72h, and which service takes ‘failed oral’ cases).

Cellulitis/erysipelas

Background

  • Cellulitis/erysipelas is an acute bacterial skin/soft-tissue infection (often streptococci/staphylococci). Erysipelas is typically more superficial with a well-demarcated edge.
  • Don’t miss: necrotising fasciitis/serious soft-tissue infection, sepsis, periorbital/orbital cellulitis, septic arthritis/osteomyelitis.
  • Don’t overcall: common mimics include venous stasis dermatitis/lipodermatosclerosis, inflammatory bite reactions, contact dermatitis, gout/pseudogout, superficial thrombophlebitis and DVT.

Assessment (primary care) — collapsible

  • History: onset/progression; systemic symptoms (rigors, confusion); pain severity; breaks in skin (eczema/ulcer/tinea pedis); bites/trauma; water exposure/travel; prior MRSA; immunosuppression; diabetes/lymphoedema/venous disease.
  • Examination: obs (T, HR, BP); size/spread; lymphangitis; fluctuance/abscess; crepitus; bullae; disproportionate pain; reduced sensation; joint involvement; facial/periorbital involvement.
  • Practical tip: draw around erythema margin to monitor progression (NICE). Be aware erythema may be less visible on darker skin tones.
  • If skin is broken: consider swab only if penetrating injury, water-borne exposure, or acquired outside the UK (NICE).
  • Consider differentials: stasis dermatitis/lipodermatosclerosis, gout, contact dermatitis, superficial thrombophlebitis, DVT, inflammatory bite reaction.

Primary care management

  • If uncomplicated and not severely unwell: treat with oral antibiotics first line (use local antimicrobial guidance; NICE NG141 provides default choices).
  • Supportive care: analgesia; elevate affected limb; mark the margin; advise skin may take time to look normal after antibiotics finish.
  • Address entry points / predispositions: treat tinea pedis/interdigital maceration; optimise eczema care; manage oedema/venous insufficiency; optimise diabetes control.
  • Safety-net / review: seek help if rapid worsening at any time, or if not starting to improve within 2–3 days (NICE).

Who to refer

Same-day hospital assessment (ED/SDEC/acute pathway)

  • Features of sepsis or the person is systemically very unwell.
  • Severe pain out of proportion, rapidly progressive infection, crepitus, skin necrosis, bullae, anaesthesia or concern for necrotising fasciitis.
  • Concern for orbital/periorbital cellulitis or infection near the eyes/nose (higher risk of intracranial complication).
  • Possible septic arthritis (hot swollen joint, severe pain with movement) or osteomyelitis.

Consider referral / seek specialist advice (local options)

  • Spreading infection not responding to oral antibiotics.
  • Lymphangitis.
  • Cannot take oral antibiotics (consider local IV-at-home / community IV options where available).
  • Possible uncommon pathogens (penetrating injury, water exposure, acquired outside UK) or MRSA risk (follow local advice).

Route (draft): DGRI SDEC/ED for severe/systemic/red flags; otherwise manage in primary care with planned review. If “failed oral” but stable, refer via locally agreed ambulatory IV/assessment route.

Who not to refer

  • Mild/localised cellulitis/erysipelas with no systemic features, no red flags and able to take oral antibiotics.
  • Likely mimic (e.g. venous stasis dermatitis, inflammatory bite reaction, gout) where infection is not the main diagnosis — manage the underlying condition and safety-net.
