DGRefHelp drafts / Suspected PE

Adults with possible pulmonary embolism: identify unstable/high-risk patients for emergency transfer, use NICE NG158 (2‑level PE Wells ± PERC) to decide D‑dimer/CTPA, and route via the local same‑day assessment pathway.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑10

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points
  • Primary care work for suspected PE is mainly triage + safe initial assessment + correct routing (same day), not definitive diagnosis.
  • NICE NG158 recommends using the 2‑level PE Wells score to guide testing: PE likely (>4) → CTPA immediately if possible; PE unlikely (≤4) → D‑dimer (then CTPA only if positive).
  • NICE allows PERC (pulmonary embolism rule‑out criteria) to help decide whether any PE investigations are needed when clinical suspicion is low on overall impression and other diagnoses are feasible.
  • When D‑dimer is used, NICE advises considering age‑adjusted thresholds for people aged >50 (and fully quantitative point‑of‑care tests if lab turnaround is not immediate).
  • If imaging cannot be done immediately when indicated, NICE recommends interim therapeutic anticoagulation while awaiting definitive imaging (local implementation needed).
Local-process sensitivity
  • Different areas use different front doors (ED vs SDEC/ambulatory emergency care vs “hot clinic”) for suspected PE and for arranging CTPA/VQ.
  • The biggest operational variable is whether primary care can obtain same‑day D‑dimer and/or same‑day imaging, and who can start interim anticoagulation safely while awaiting imaging.
  • This draft is written to be safe without D&G-specific routing agreed; see Local agreement needed.

Comparator sources used for the draft

  • NICE NG158 (Venous thromboembolic diseases): diagnostic pathway for suspected PE (2‑level PE Wells, D‑dimer, CTPA/VQ, interim anticoagulation when imaging delayed) and D‑dimer notes (POC, age-adjusted).
  • RCEMLearning: overview pages for Wells criteria/PERC (used as a secondary explainer; NICE remains the primary source).
Scope note
Adults only. Separate pathways needed for pregnancy/postpartum, children, and complex anticoagulation decisions (e.g., active bleeding, severe thrombocytopenia).

Suspected pulmonary embolism (PE) — adults (primary care triage)

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Shock / haemodynamic instability: collapse/syncope, hypotension, extreme tachycardia, clammy/grey, altered mental state.
  • Severe hypoxia or marked respiratory distress.
  • Ongoing large-volume haemoptysis.
  • High clinical concern where the patient is too unwell for outpatient assessment.

Step 1 — quick initial assessment

  • Observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2 on air.
  • History: pleuritic chest pain, dyspnoea, haemoptysis, syncope; leg pain/swelling; VTE risk factors (recent surgery/immobility, previous VTE, active cancer, oestrogen therapy, pregnancy/postpartum).
  • Exam: signs of DVT; chest exam (often normal); consider alternative diagnoses.
  • Initial tests (if available and not delaying same‑day assessment): ECG, CXR (NICE recommends offering a CXR to exclude other causes).

Step 2 — decide whether PE workup is needed (PERC, if genuinely low suspicion)

  • If your overall clinical impression is that PE suspicion is low and other diagnoses are feasible, consider using PERC to support a decision that no further PE investigations are needed (NICE).
  • If not low suspicion (or PERC positive / not applicable): proceed to Wells-based pathway.

Step 3 — apply NICE 2‑level PE Wells score

  • PE likely = Wells >4 → needs same-day imaging (usually CTPA), with interim anticoagulation if imaging cannot be done immediately (local pathway).
  • PE unlikely = Wells ≤4 → do D‑dimer (same day if possible):
    • D‑dimer negative → PE unlikely; consider alternative diagnoses; safety-net.
    • D‑dimer positive → proceed to CTPA (same day where possible).

D‑dimer notes (NICE)

  • Consider an age‑adjusted D‑dimer threshold in people aged >50 (local lab reporting/threshold rules).
  • If lab turnaround is not immediate, consider a fully quantitative point‑of‑care D‑dimer test (local availability).

Who to refer / where to send (D&G placeholders)

  • ED (999/urgent transfer): any red flags above; unstable vitals; severe hypoxia/distress; syncope with PE concern.
  • Same-day assessment (SDEC/ambulatory emergency care/acute medicine):
    • PE likely (Wells >4), or
    • PE unlikely + D‑dimer positive, or
    • high clinical concern where safe outpatient-only testing isn’t feasible.
  • Alternative routes (only if explicitly available locally): direct access D‑dimer with protocolled “positive → attend for imaging”, or direct imaging pathway.

What to include in the referral / handover

  • Symptoms + onset; haemoptysis/syncope; pleuritic pain.
  • Wells score and components (document clearly).
  • Observations incl. SpO2 on air.
  • Relevant PMH and meds (esp. anticoagulants, antiplatelets, oestrogen therapy).
  • Pregnancy/postpartum status (if relevant) — note: separate pathway is needed.
  • ECG/CXR findings (if done).

Safety-netting

  • Advise urgent care/999 for worsening breathlessness, syncope, chest pain, haemoptysis, or SpO2 dropping.
  • If PE has been ruled out but symptoms persist/worsen, re-assess for alternative diagnoses (pneumonia, pneumothorax, ACS, pericarditis, musculoskeletal pain, anxiety, etc.).

Local agreement needed (D&G)

Reminder
If the patient is unwell, do not spend time on scoring tools: send same day. Tools support decisions; they do not replace clinical judgement.
