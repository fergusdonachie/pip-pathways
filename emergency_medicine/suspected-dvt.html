DGRefHelp drafts / Suspected DVT

Adults with suspected deep vein thrombosis: triage red flags, use the 2‑level Wells score, and follow NICE NG158 (D‑dimer + ultrasound within 4 hours where possible; interim anticoagulation if delays).
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑11

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (primary care)
  • NICE NG158 recommends using the 2‑level DVT Wells score to stratify likely (≥2) vs unlikely (≤1) DVT.
  • Target timescales: imaging / D‑dimer results are ideally available within 4 hours; if not, NG158 recommends interim therapeutic anticoagulation while awaiting definitive testing, with ultrasound available within 24 hours.
  • For Wells likely (≥2): NG158 recommends proximal leg vein ultrasound first; if negative, do D‑dimer. If ultrasound is delayed (>4h), do D‑dimer + interim anticoagulation + ultrasound within 24h.
  • If initial ultrasound is negative but D‑dimer is positive, NG158 recommends repeat proximal ultrasound in 6–8 days (and stop interim anticoagulation in the meantime, unless anticoagulation is being used for another indication).
  • D‑dimer thresholds can be age‑adjusted in people >50 (NG158).

Suspected DVT — adults (primary care triage + NG158)

Immediate red flags (999 / ED now)

  • Haemodynamic compromise or severe breathlessness/chest pain → consider PE/other emergency.
  • Massively swollen, very painful, cyanotic leg (concern for severe venous obstruction / limb threat).
  • Acute limb ischaemia features (cold/pale limb, sensory loss, severe pain, absent pulses) → DVT alternative is not the main issue; emergency vascular assessment.

Step 1 — confirm presentation + exclude obvious alternatives

  • History: unilateral leg swelling/pain, onset, immobility/surgery, cancer, pregnancy/post‑partum, prior VTE, oestrogen therapy, recent long‑haul travel, infection/trauma.
  • Examination: unilateral pitting oedema, calf tenderness, whole leg swelling; check for alternative diagnosis (cellulitis, Baker’s cyst, muscle tear, chronic venous disease).
  • Baseline observations: HR, BP, RR, Temp, SpO2.

Step 2 — risk stratify using the 2‑level DVT Wells score (NICE NG158)

  • DVT likely: Wells score ≥2.
  • DVT unlikely: Wells score ≤1.

Step 3 — diagnostic pathway (NICE NG158)

If DVT likely (Wells ≥2)
  • Offer proximal leg vein ultrasound with result available within 4 hours if possible.
  • If ultrasound is negative → do D‑dimer.
  • If ultrasound cannot be obtained within 4 hours:
    • Do D‑dimer, then
    • Start interim therapeutic anticoagulation (unless contraindicated / already anticoagulated), and
    • Arrange ultrasound with result within 24 hours.
If DVT unlikely (Wells ≤1)
  • Offer D‑dimer with result available within 4 hours.
  • If D‑dimer cannot be obtained within 4 hours → consider interim therapeutic anticoagulation while awaiting the result.
  • If D‑dimer is negative: tell the person DVT is not likely; think about alternative diagnosis; provide safety‑net advice.
  • If D‑dimer is positive: offer proximal ultrasound (within 4 hours if possible; otherwise interim anticoagulation + ultrasound within 24 hours).

Interpreting results (NICE NG158)

  • Ultrasound positive → treat as confirmed DVT (start/continue anticoagulation per local guideline).
  • Ultrasound negative + D‑dimer negative:
    • Stop interim therapeutic anticoagulation (unless taking anticoagulation for another indication).
    • Tell the person DVT is not likely; consider alternatives; safety‑net.
  • Ultrasound negative + D‑dimer positive:
    • Stop interim anticoagulation (unless for another indication).
    • Arrange repeat proximal ultrasound in 6–8 days.
    • If repeat scan is positive → treat as confirmed DVT; if repeat scan negative → DVT unlikely; safety‑net.

Practical notes (typical)

  • Consider age‑adjusted D‑dimer threshold in people aged >50 (per NG158; local lab policy).
  • Before starting interim anticoagulation, take baseline bloods where feasible (do not delay urgent treatment/routing): FBC, U&Es/eGFR, LFTs, clotting ± pregnancy test if relevant.
  • Contraindications/bleeding risk: active bleeding, severe thrombocytopenia, recent major bleeding, severe renal impairment (agent choice), etc. Use clinical judgement and local guidance.

Who to refer / where to send (D&G placeholders)

  • 999 / ED: any instability; concern for PE; limb‑threatening features.
  • Same‑day assessment (SDEC/acute front door): Wells likely OR D‑dimer positive (if ultrasound not rapidly accessible from primary care) OR need interim anticoagulation initiation.
  • Direct to imaging (if local pathway exists): booked same‑day/next‑day proximal ultrasound with clear instructions on interim anticoagulation.
  • Advice & guidance: pregnancy, cancer‑associated thrombosis, recurrent VTE, complex anticoagulation (renal failure, extremes of weight, drug interactions).

Referral-letter minimum (suggested)

  • DVT Wells score + components; calf circumference (symptomatic vs asymptomatic side).
  • Bleeding risk/contraindications; current anticoagulant/antiplatelet meds.
  • Comorbidities: cancer, recent surgery/immobility, pregnancy/post‑partum, prior VTE.
  • Renal function if known; weight (for LMWH dosing).

Local agreement needed (add D&G specifics)

  • What is the default same‑day destination for suspected DVT needing ultrasound/interim anticoagulation: ED vs SDEC/acute medicine vs ambulatory care?
  • Can GPs order proximal leg vein ultrasound directly? If yes: expected turnaround (within 4h? within 24h?) and booking mechanism.
  • Access to D‑dimer with turnaround <4h (lab vs point‑of‑care): where and for whom?
  • Local policy for interim therapeutic anticoagulation in suspected DVT: preferred agent (LMWH vs DOAC), contraindication checklist, and who prescribes.
  • Pathway for pregnancy/post‑partum suspected DVT (direct obstetric/acute pathway? imaging access? anticoagulation choice).
  • If ultrasound negative + D‑dimer positive: who arranges the repeat scan at 6–8 days (and how is the patient safety‑netted)?
Safety net
Advise urgent reassessment if breathlessness, pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis, syncope, or rapid worsening leg swelling/pain develops (possible PE / progressing thrombosis).
