DGRefHelp drafts / Suspected sepsis (≥16, not pregnant)

Primary care triage using NICE NG253 (community table): think sepsis in any possible infection, don’t rely on fever, identify high‑risk features for urgent transfer, and hand over full observations.
Mode: quick
Last updated: 2026‑02‑13

Evidence summary (triangulated)

Key design points (NICE NG253)
  • Think “could this be sepsis?” in any presentation suggesting possible infection; sepsis can be non-specific and may occur without fever.
  • In the community, NICE recommends grading risk using the non‑acute (community/custodial) criteria table. Any one high‑risk criterion = high risk; any moderate‑to‑high criterion = moderate‑to‑high risk.
  • Remote assessment should actively look for vulnerability factors and new abnormalities of behaviour, circulation or respiration to decide urgency of face‑to‑face review.
  • NICE highlights neutropenic sepsis suspicion in people who become unwell after recent systemic anticancer treatment (within 30 days) or other immunosuppressive therapies (clinical judgement on ongoing neutropenia risk).
Local-process sensitivity
  • For primary care, the key operational variable is the front door (ED vs SDEC/ambulatory acute medicine) and whether any community IV/first-dose antibiotic protocol exists.
  • This draft is written to be safe without D&G-specific escalation routes confirmed; see Local agreement needed.

Comparator sources used for the draft

  • NICE NG253: “Could this be sepsis?” and “Evaluating risk (community table)” recommendations and criteria (people aged ≥16, not pregnant/recently pregnant).
  • AoMRC statement (2022) is referenced by NICE for NEWS2 recalculation intervals in acute settings (included here only as context; this pathway is primarily community triage).
Scope note
This page is for people aged 16 or over who are not pregnant and have not recently been pregnant. Use separate guidance for pregnant/recently pregnant and under‑16s. Local antimicrobial guidance should be used for antibiotic choices.

Suspected sepsis — age ≥16 (not pregnant / not recently pregnant) — primary care triage

Think “could this be sepsis?” (NICE)

  • Any symptoms/signs suggesting possible infection.
  • Be alert to non-specific presentations ("very unwell") and that the patient may have no fever.
  • Pay attention to concerns from family/carers about change from usual behaviour.
  • Take extra care if history is hard to obtain (language/communication challenges, cognitive impairment, severe mental illness, etc.).

Step 1 — immediate assessment (do not delay transfer if very unwell)

  • Obs: RR, HR, BP, Temp, SpO2 on air, AVPU/GCS, capillary glucose.
  • Look for: new confusion/altered mental state; mottling/ashen colour/cyanosis; non‑blanching rash; signs of hypoperfusion/dehydration (poor urine output, prolonged CRT).
  • Source clues: chest/urine/skin/abdo/wound; indwelling devices; recent procedures.
  • Vulnerability factors: frailty/multimorbidity; immunosuppression; surgery/invasive procedure in last 6 weeks; indwelling catheters; breach in skin integrity; age ≥75; social vulnerability (e.g. homelessness).

Step 2 — use NICE community risk criteria (NG253 table)

Any single high‑risk criterion = high risk. Any single moderate‑to‑high criterion (without high‑risk) = moderate‑to‑high risk. If neither: low risk.

High risk criteria (any one)

  • New altered mental state (objective evidence).
  • RR ≥25 breaths/min.
  • New need for oxygen (around FiO2 ≥40%) to maintain sats >92% (or >88% in chronic hypercapnic respiratory failure).
  • SBP ≤90 mmHg (or >40 mmHg below normal).
  • HR >130 bpm.
  • Not passed urine in previous 18 hours (or <0.5 ml/kg/hour if catheterised).
  • Mottled/ashen appearance, cyanosis, or non‑blanching petechial/purpuric rash.

Moderate-to-high risk criteria (any one; if no high-risk)

  • History of new onset altered behaviour/mental state (from patient/friend/relative).
  • Acute deterioration of functional ability.
  • Immunosuppression (illness or drugs incl. oral steroids).
  • Trauma/surgery/invasive procedure in last 6 weeks.
  • RR 21–24 breaths/min.
  • SBP 91–100 mmHg.
  • HR 91–130 bpm or new-onset arrhythmia.
  • Not passed urine in last 12–18 hours (or 0.5–1 ml/kg/hour if catheterised).
  • Tympanic temperature <36°C.
  • Signs of potential infection at surgical site (redness/swelling/discharge) or wound breakdown.

What to do (routing)

  • High risk: treat as time-critical — arrange urgent emergency assessment (ED / ambulance transfer depending on severity). Consider pre-alert. Do not delay transfer for tests.
  • Moderate-to-high risk: arrange same-day urgent assessment via the agreed acute pathway (ED/SDEC/ambulatory acute medicine). Escalate sooner if deterioration.
  • Low risk: manage as infection but safety-net strongly and reassess promptly if worsening/new features.

Special case: suspected neutropenic sepsis (NICE)

  • Suspect in any acutely unwell patient who has had systemic anticancer treatment within 30 days, or other immunosuppressants where neutropenia is plausible.
  • Route as emergency (time-critical) and ensure the receiving service is aware of the neutropenia risk.

What to include in the referral / handover

  • Presenting features + suspected source.
  • All observations (RR/HR/BP/Temp/SpO2 on air) + mental state.
  • Which NICE high‑risk / moderate‑to‑high criteria are met (be explicit).
  • Vulnerability factors: immunosuppression, recent procedures, frailty, indwelling devices.
  • Urine output history (if known); hydration status.
  • Allergies, current meds (esp. steroids/immunosuppressants/anticoagulants), and any antibiotics already given.

Safety-netting (for any non-transferred patient)

  • Escalate urgently for: new confusion, worsening breathlessness, mottling/ashen colour, non‑blanching rash, very low urine output, SBP ≤100, RR ≥21, HR ≥91, or the patient becoming “very unwell”.
  • Advise carers what deterioration looks like and to call 999/urgent services if concerned.

Local agreement needed (D&G)

Reminder
If the patient is unwell, don’t over-tool it: obs + urgency + transfer. Sepsis tools support decisions; they do not replace clinical judgement.
